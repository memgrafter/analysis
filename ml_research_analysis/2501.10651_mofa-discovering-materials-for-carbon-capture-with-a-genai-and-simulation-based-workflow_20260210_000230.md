# MOFA: Discovering Materials for Carbon Capture with a GenAI- and Simulation-Based Workflow

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2501.10651
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.10651
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: MOFA achieves linear scaling for MOF generation on 450-node supercomputer with top 10 CO2 adsorption capacity rankings

## Executive Summary
MOFA introduces an open-source workflow that integrates generative AI with molecular simulations to discover metal-organic frameworks (MOFs) for carbon capture applications. The system addresses the challenge of combining GPU-intensive AI tasks with CPU- and GPU-optimized simulation workloads on large-scale HPC systems. By implementing an online learning framework, MOFA dynamically optimizes resource utilization across heterogeneous computing tasks, achieving high-throughput generation of novel MOF structures. Performance tests on a 450-node supercomputer demonstrate linear scaling relationships and competitive CO2 adsorption capacities.

## Method Summary
MOFA employs a modular architecture that integrates generative AI models with molecular dynamics, density functional theory, and Monte Carlo simulations. The workflow operates on distributed HPC systems, utilizing both AMD Zen 3 CPUs and NVIDIA A100 GPUs across 450 nodes. The system implements online learning to dynamically allocate resources between AI model training/inference and molecular simulations, optimizing throughput for MOF structure generation and screening. The framework supports high-throughput screening of AI-generated MOFs while maintaining computational efficiency through GPU acceleration and task orchestration.

## Key Results
- Achieved linear scaling relationship between node count and MOF generation throughput on 450-node supercomputer
- Generated MOF structures with CO2 adsorption capacities ranking among top 10 in the hypothetical MOF dataset
- Successfully integrated distributed GenAI training with molecular simulations on heterogeneous HPC resources

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
MOFA's effectiveness stems from its unified online learning framework that bridges the gap between GPU-intensive AI workloads and simulation tasks. The system leverages distributed training capabilities for generative models while maintaining efficient CPU-GPU resource allocation for molecular simulations. By treating the entire workflow as a cohesive system rather than separate components, MOFA optimizes resource utilization across heterogeneous tasks, enabling high-throughput generation and screening of novel MOF structures.

## Foundational Learning
- **Online Learning Framework**: Dynamic resource allocation between AI and simulation tasks ensures optimal CPU-GPU utilization across heterogeneous workloads
- **GPU-Accelerated Molecular Simulations**: Accelerates computationally intensive molecular dynamics and Monte Carlo calculations for rapid MOF screening
- **Distributed GenAI Training**: Enables scalable generation of novel MOF structures through parallel processing across multiple GPU nodes
- **Modular Architecture Design**: Facilitates integration with other scientific applications and enables flexible workflow customization
- **High-Performance Computing Orchestration**: Manages complex task dependencies and resource allocation across 450-node supercomputer configurations
- **CO2 Adsorption Screening**: Implements rapid evaluation of generated MOFs for carbon capture potential through integrated simulation pipelines

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map: GenAI Model -> Online Learning Controller -> Molecular Simulations -> Screening Pipeline

Critical Path: Generative AI model training/inference → Online learning resource optimization → Molecular simulations → CO2 adsorption screening → Structure validation

Design Tradeoffs: The modular architecture prioritizes flexibility and integration capability over maximum single-task performance, enabling adaptation to various scientific workflows but potentially introducing overhead in resource management.

Failure Signatures: Resource contention between AI and simulation tasks, imbalanced GPU utilization, communication bottlenecks in distributed training, and simulation accuracy degradation under high-throughput conditions.
