# Quantum-Classical Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Solving Reservoir Seepage Equations

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2512.03923
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.03923
- Reference count: 0

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a Quantum-Classical Physics-Informed Neural Network (QCPINN) architecture that combines classical neural networks with quantum circuits to solve reservoir seepage equations. The hybrid approach leverages quantum superposition and entanglement through discrete-variable quantum circuits while maintaining classical preprocessing and postprocessing capabilities. The method addresses limitations of traditional Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) in parameter efficiency and nonlinear fitting by integrating quantum computing principles into the solution framework.

## Method Summary
The QCPINN architecture employs a hybrid quantum-classical framework where classical neural networks handle preprocessing and postprocessing tasks while a quantum core performs the main computation. The quantum circuit operates with discrete variables, utilizing superposition and entanglement properties to enhance computational efficiency. Three distinct quantum circuit topologies are implemented: Cascade, Cross-mesh, and Alternate configurations. The approach is evaluated on three representative reservoir seepage problems: heterogeneous single-phase flow, nonlinear Buckley-Leverett two-phase flow, and convection-diffusion with adsorption. The method maintains the physics-informed framework by incorporating governing equations directly into the loss function, ensuring physical consistency in predictions.

## Key Results
- QCPINNs achieve high prediction accuracy with fewer parameters than classical PINNs for all three tested reservoir models
- The Alternate quantum circuit topology demonstrates superior accuracy for heterogeneous flow and two-phase Buckley-Leverett equations
- Cascade topology excels in compositional flow problems while maintaining computational efficiency

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The QCPINN architecture works by leveraging quantum mechanical principles to enhance the representational capacity of neural networks for solving partial differential equations. Quantum superposition allows the network to explore multiple solution states simultaneously, while entanglement enables complex correlations between variables that are difficult to capture with classical architectures alone. The discrete-variable quantum circuits provide a natural framework for encoding the continuous variables in reservoir seepage problems, and the hybrid structure allows for seamless integration of quantum advantages with classical neural network capabilities.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Classical Preprocessing -> Quantum Circuit -> Classical Postprocessing -> Physics Loss Computation

**Critical Path**: The most critical computational path runs from input preprocessing through the quantum circuit to postprocessing, where the quantum core performs the essential nonlinear transformations that classical PINNs struggle with. The quantum circuit's ability to maintain superposition and entanglement states directly impacts solution accuracy.

**Design Tradeoffs**: The architecture balances quantum resource utilization against classical control, with circuit topology selection affecting both accuracy and computational efficiency. The choice between Cascade, Cross-mesh, and Alternate topologies involves tradeoffs between parameter count, training stability, and problem-specific performance. Classical preprocessing provides robustness but adds computational overhead, while the quantum core offers potential efficiency gains at the cost of hardware accessibility.

**Failure Signatures**: Poor convergence may indicate insufficient quantum circuit depth or inappropriate topology selection. Large physics constraint violations suggest the quantum circuit cannot adequately represent the governing equations. Parameter inefficiency compared to classical PINNs could signal suboptimal quantum encoding or circuit design. Hardware noise sensitivity would manifest as inconsistent predictions across similar inputs.

**First Experiments**: 1) Test quantum circuit response to simple linear reservoir problems to establish baseline performance, 2) Compare all three circuit topologies on a standard benchmark problem to identify optimal topology selection criteria, 3) Perform sensitivity analysis on quantum circuit parameters to determine minimum requirements for accurate solutions
