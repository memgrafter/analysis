# Can Conversational AI Counsel for Change? A Theory-Driven Approach to Supporting Dietary Intentions in Ambivalent Individuals

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2511.02428
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.02428
- Authors: Michelle Bak; Kexin Quan; Tre Tomaszewski; Jessie Chin
- Reference count: 40
- CounselLLM significantly increased dietary change intentions through theory-driven conversational AI

## Executive Summary
CounselLLM is a conversational AI system designed to support dietary behavior change in ambivalent individuals by integrating Motivational Interviewing (MI) and Transtheoretical Model (TTM) principles. The system employs few-shot learning with carefully crafted persona descriptions to embody MI techniques such as open-ended questions, affirmations, and reflective listening. Through a randomized controlled trial with 38 participants, CounselLLM demonstrated effectiveness in increasing dietary change intentions while maintaining high user satisfaction and linguistic quality comparable to human counselors.

The system's architecture combines LLM capabilities with behavioral change theory to create personalized, empathetic interactions that engage users in both cognitive and affective reflection about their dietary habits. By focusing on ambivalent individuals who recognize the need for change but face barriers to action, CounselLLM addresses a critical gap in digital health interventions and demonstrates the potential for scalable, theory-driven AI counseling systems.

## Method Summary
The study employed a randomized controlled trial design with 38 participants (19 per condition) comparing CounselLLM against human counselors in dietary behavior change support. Participants completed pre-intervention questionnaires assessing dietary knowledge, perceptions, and intentions, followed by a single counseling session, then post-intervention assessments of the same measures plus user experience ratings. The CounselLLM system was developed using few-shot learning with persona descriptions incorporating MI principles and TTM stages of change, generating personalized conversations about dietary habits and change motivations.

## Key Results
- Participants showed significantly increased intentions to change dietary behaviors after CounselLLM interactions
- System matched human counselors in linguistic quality while producing more concrete, actionable language
- Participants rated CounselLLM as supportive, easy to use, and understandable with high satisfaction scores

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The system's effectiveness stems from its integration of established behavioral change theories into conversational AI design. By embedding Motivational Interviewing techniques through persona descriptions and few-shot prompting, CounselLLM creates empathetic, non-judgmental interactions that help users explore ambivalence and build self-efficacy. The Transtheoretical Model framework allows the system to meet users at their current stage of change and provide appropriate support for progression.

## Foundational Learning
- Motivational Interviewing techniques: needed to create supportive, non-confrontational dialogue; quick check: system uses open-ended questions and affirmations
- Transtheoretical Model stages: needed to tailor interventions to user readiness; quick check: system adapts responses based on change stage
- Few-shot learning with personas: needed to guide LLM behavior without extensive fine-tuning; quick check: persona descriptions include MI principles
- Ambivalence in behavior change: needed to target appropriate user population; quick check: participants recognize need for change but face barriers
- Linguistic quality assessment: needed to ensure AI matches human counselor standards; quick check: comparative analysis of conversation transcripts

## Architecture Onboarding
- Component map: User Input -> LLM with MI Persona -> Response Generation -> User Output
- Critical path: Persona design → Few-shot prompt engineering → Conversation generation → User engagement → Outcome measurement
- Design tradeoffs: Few-shot learning vs. fine-tuning (flexibility vs. control), single-session vs. longitudinal design (practicality vs. sustainability)
- Failure signatures: Generic responses when persona not properly engaged, overly directive language violating MI principles, failure to address user ambivalence
- First experiments: 1) Test different persona descriptions on MI technique implementation, 2) Compare few-shot vs. fine-tuning approaches, 3) Validate stage-matching accuracy across TTM

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
None

## Limitations
- Small sample size (n=38) limits generalizability and statistical power
- Single-session design restricts conclusions about long-term behavior change sustainability
- Absence of follow-up assessments prevents evaluation of intention-to-behavior translation

## Confidence
- High: Linguistic quality and user experience metrics due to direct human comparison
- Medium: Effectiveness in increasing dietary change intentions with significant improvements shown
- Low: Ability to produce sustained behavior change due to single-session design

## Next Checks
1. Conduct a longitudinal study with 3-month and 6-month follow-up assessments to evaluate whether increased intentions lead to sustained dietary behavior changes and measure actual food diary outcomes.

2. Replicate the study with a larger, more diverse sample (n > 100) across different demographic groups and dietary goals to assess generalizability and identify potential population-specific effects.

3. Perform head-to-head comparisons between CounselLLM and established digital health interventions (e.g., text-based counseling, mobile apps) to establish relative effectiveness and identify unique advantages of the conversational AI approach.
