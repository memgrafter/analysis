# Online Social Welfare Function-based Resource Allocation

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID:** 2602.01400
- **Source URL:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.01400
- **Reference count:** 40
- **Primary result:** Near-optimal regret of O(n + √(nkT)) for online social welfare maximization

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a framework for online social welfare maximization in resource allocation problems using social welfare functions (SWFs). The authors develop the SWF-UCB algorithm that achieves near-optimal regret while providing anytime-valid inference tools. The framework is instantiated on three normatively distinct SWF families (Weighted Power Mean, Kolm, and Gini) with efficient oracle algorithms for each. Experiments confirm the theoretical √T scaling of regret and reveal non-monotonic dependencies on the number of resources.

## Method Summary
The paper presents a confidence sequence lifting theorem that uses monotonicity of social welfare functions to generate anytime-valid bounds on optimal welfare. This theoretical foundation enables the development of SWF-UCB, an algorithm that balances exploration and exploitation while maintaining statistical validity. The algorithm achieves near-optimal regret by leveraging the structure of different SWF families through problem-specific oracle implementations. Three SWF families are explored: Weighted Power Mean (parametrized by α), Kolm (parametrized by β), and Gini (parametrized by γ), each with distinct normative properties.

## Key Results
- SWF-UCB achieves near-optimal regret bound of O(n + √(nkT)) for k resources among n individuals over T time steps
- √T scaling of regret is confirmed experimentally, matching theoretical predictions
- Non-monotonic dependencies on number of resources observed, with intermediate resource counts sometimes yielding lower regret

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework succeeds by exploiting the monotonicity property of social welfare functions to lift confidence sequences from individual rewards to the overall social welfare. This allows for anytime-valid inference while maintaining strong regret guarantees. The oracle-based approach for different SWF families enables efficient optimization that respects the specific structural properties of each welfare function family.

## Foundational Learning

**Social Welfare Functions** - Mathematical functions that aggregate individual utilities into a collective measure of societal well-being. Needed to formalize the objective of maximizing societal benefit rather than individual gains. Quick check: Verify that the chosen SWF satisfies required monotonicity and continuity properties.

**Confidence Sequences** - Time-uniform confidence bounds that hold at all time points simultaneously. Needed for anytime-valid inference in online settings. Quick check: Confirm the confidence sequence maintains valid coverage probability throughout execution.

**Multi-armed Bandits with Multiple Plays** - Extension of classical bandit problems where multiple arms can be selected simultaneously. Needed to model resource allocation scenarios with multiple recipients. Quick check: Validate that the algorithm correctly handles the combinatorial nature of the allocation problem.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Confidence Sequence Lifting -> Oracle Solver -> SWF-UCB Algorithm -> Regret Analysis

**Critical Path:** The algorithm operates by first lifting confidence bounds from individual rewards to social welfare using the monotonicity theorem, then using oracle solvers specific to each SWF family to find optimal allocations within these bounds.

**Design Tradeoffs:** The framework trades off computational efficiency for theoretical guarantees - efficient oracles are available for specific SWF families but may not generalize to arbitrary welfare functions. The anytime-valid inference comes at the cost of potentially wider confidence bounds compared to fixed-time methods.

**Failure Signatures:** Algorithm may underperform when SWF assumptions (monotonicity, continuity) are violated. Performance degradation expected when oracle solvers become computationally expensive for large problem instances.

**First Experiments:** 1) Validate √T regret scaling on synthetic data with varying T. 2) Test sensitivity to SWF parameters across different problem instances. 3) Benchmark against non-SWF-based resource allocation algorithms.

## Limitations

- Assumes fixed, known social welfare function across all time periods, which may not reflect evolving preferences
- Monotonicity assumption for confidence sequence lifting could be restrictive for certain applications
- Limited experimental validation to synthetic data without real-world application demonstrations

## Confidence

High: Theoretical regret bounds and algorithm design appear sound given stated assumptions; √T scaling matches established bandit literature

Medium: Efficient oracle implementations presented but not thoroughly validated for scalability; relationship between SWF parameters and regret needs more empirical exploration

Low: Practical utility of anytime-valid inference tools in real resource allocation scenarios remains speculative without case studies
