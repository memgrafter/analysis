# Quasiparticle Interference Kernel Extraction with Variational Autoencoders via Latent Alignment

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2506.05325
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05325
- Authors: Yingshuai Ji; Haomin Zhuang; Matthew Toole; James McKenzie; Xiaolong Liu; Xiangliang Zhang
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: First AI-based solution for extracting single-scatterer QPI kernels from multi-scatterer STM images using VAEs

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of extracting single-scatterer quasiparticle interference (QPI) kernels from multi-scatterer STM images, a fundamental inverse problem in quantum materials research. The core difficulty lies in the fact that overlapping signals from multiple defects make it impossible to directly infer the underlying kernel pattern. To solve this, the authors propose a two-step machine learning framework based on variational autoencoders (VAEs). First, a VAE is trained to learn a compact latent space of physically valid scattering kernels. Then, a separate encoder aligns observational QPI data with this latent space, enabling robust kernel inference under complex scattering conditions.

The method is evaluated on a novel simulated QPI dataset of 100 unique kernels with 50,000 samples, and validated on real STM data from Ag and FeSe. Results show significant improvements over a direct one-step baseline, with up to 18% lower RMSE, and successful generalization to unseen kernels. The work provides the first AI-based solution for QPI kernel extraction, demonstrating the power of representation learning for solving ill-posed inverse problems in quantum materials.

## Method Summary
The authors develop a two-step machine learning framework for QPI kernel extraction. First, a VAE is trained on idealized single-scatterer QPI patterns to learn a latent space of physically valid kernels. This VAE learns a compressed representation of the scattering patterns, capturing the essential physics of quasiparticle scattering. Second, a separate encoder is trained to map multi-scatterer observational data to this learned latent space through a regression task. This two-step approach leverages the VAE's learned physics constraints while enabling alignment with real experimental data. The framework is trained on a simulated dataset of 50,000 samples spanning 100 unique kernels, then validated on both simulated and real STM data from Ag(111) and FeSe surfaces.

## Key Results
- Achieved up to 18% lower RMSE compared to direct one-step baseline method
- Successfully generalized to unseen scattering kernels not present in training data
- Validated method on real STM data from Ag and FeSe surfaces with promising results
- Demonstrated first AI-based solution for QPI kernel extraction from multi-scatterer images

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method works by addressing the fundamental ill-posed nature of the inverse problem through learned physical constraints. The VAE in the first step learns a compact latent representation of physically valid scattering kernels, effectively constraining the solution space to patterns that obey the underlying physics of quasiparticle scattering. The second step then uses this learned representation as a prior when mapping complex multi-scatterer observations back to single-scatterer kernels. This two-step approach is more robust than direct regression because it separates the physics learning (VAE) from the observational alignment (encoder), allowing each to focus on its respective strengths. The latent space acts as a bridge between idealized physics and noisy experimental data.

## Foundational Learning
- Quasiparticle interference (QPI) in STM: Wave-like scattering of electrons from defects creates interference patterns in STM images. Understanding these patterns is crucial for probing electronic structure.
  - Why needed: The method relies on understanding how single defects create characteristic patterns that can be extracted from complex multi-scatterer images.
  - Quick check: Verify that QPI patterns encode information about band structure and scattering processes.

- Variational autoencoders (VAEs): Neural networks that learn compressed latent representations of data while enforcing statistical constraints on the latent space.
  - Why needed: The VAE learns a compact, physics-constrained representation of single-scatterer kernels that can be used as a prior for inference.
  - Quick check: Confirm that the VAE learns meaningful latent dimensions that capture scattering physics.

- Inverse problems in imaging: Situations where we observe effects (multi-scatterer patterns) and need to infer causes (single-scatterer kernels).
  - Why needed: QPI kernel extraction is inherently an inverse problem, requiring special techniques to handle its ill-posed nature.
  - Quick check: Assess whether the problem meets criteria for being ill-posed (non-unique solutions, sensitivity to noise).

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Simulated QPI data -> VAE (latent space learning) -> Latent space of kernels -> Encoder (observational alignment) -> Kernel predictions -> Real STM data validation

Critical path: The core innovation is the separation of physics learning (VAE) from observational alignment (encoder). This allows the model to first learn what physically valid kernels look like, then learn how to map complex observations to this space. The critical insight is that direct regression from multi-scatterer images to single-scatterer kernels is too ill-posed, but constraining the solution space through learned physics makes the problem tractable.

Design tradeoffs: The two-step approach trades computational efficiency for robustness. While a single-step model might be faster, it would likely fail due to the ill-posed nature of direct regression. The VAE approach requires more training but provides crucial physics constraints. The simulated dataset is comprehensive but may not capture all experimental realities.

Failure signatures: The method could fail if the latent space doesn't adequately represent the diversity of real scattering kernels, if the encoder cannot properly align observational data with the latent space, or if real STM data contains noise/artifacts not present in simulation. Overfitting to the simulated dataset is another potential failure mode.

2. Evaluate the latent space visualization to verify that it captures meaningful physics of scattering patterns.
3. Compare kernel predictions on real STM data with established analysis methods to validate physical accuracy.

## Limitations
- Limited validation to only two material systems (Ag and FeSe) raises questions about generalization to other quantum materials
- Simulated training data may not fully capture all noise sources and artifacts present in real STM measurements
- Assumption that learned latent space from idealized kernels can represent complex multi-scatterer scenarios may not hold for all materials

## Confidence
High confidence: The core methodological innovation (two-step VAE approach), the mathematical formulation of the inverse problem, and the comparison with baseline methods are all well-established and reproducible.

Medium confidence: The effectiveness of the method on real STM data, given the limited number of material systems tested and the potential for unmodeled experimental factors.

Low confidence: Claims about the method's applicability to strongly correlated systems or materials with complex electronic band structures, as these have not been explicitly tested.

## Next Checks
