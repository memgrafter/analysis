# CND-IDS: Continual Novelty Detection for Intrusion Detection Systems

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2502.14094
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.14094
- Reference count: 37
- Achieves up to 6.1× F-score improvement over state-of-the-art unsupervised continual learning algorithms

## Executive Summary
CND-IDS addresses critical challenges in intrusion detection systems: evolving cyber attacks and the scarcity of labeled attack data. The framework introduces a continual novelty detection approach that can identify both known and zero-day attacks without requiring labeled attack examples. By leveraging only normal data for training, CND-IDS overcomes the fundamental limitation of traditional intrusion detection systems that require attack samples for supervised learning.

The system demonstrates significant performance improvements across four diverse intrusion datasets, achieving up to 6.1× better F-score and 6.5× improved forward transfer compared to existing unsupervised continual learning methods. The approach maintains low computational overhead, making it suitable for real-time deployment in production environments where detecting novel threats is crucial for maintaining security.

## Method Summary
CND-IDS combines a continual feature extractor with a PCA-based novelty detector to identify attacks in network traffic. The feature extractor continuously updates its representations using a novel continual novelty detection loss function that incorporates cluster separation, reconstruction, and continual learning objectives. This allows the system to adapt to evolving attack patterns without forgetting previously learned normal behaviors. The PCA-based novelty detector, trained solely on normal data, identifies attacks by measuring reconstruction errors - normal data should reconstruct well while attack traffic produces high reconstruction errors. The framework operates in an unsupervised manner, requiring no labeled attack data during training, making it practical for real-world deployment where attack samples are typically unavailable.

## Key Results

- Demonstrates 6.5× better forward transfer capability across evolving attack scenarios
- Successfully detects both known and zero-day attacks while maintaining low computational overhead for real-time deployment

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework's effectiveness stems from its dual-component architecture that separates feature learning from novelty detection. The continual feature extractor learns rich representations of normal network behavior that can adapt to gradual changes while maintaining distinctiveness from potential attacks. The PCA-based detector exploits the principle that normal data should have low reconstruction error while anomalous traffic produces high errors. This separation allows each component to specialize - the feature extractor focuses on representation quality while the detector focuses on anomaly scoring. The continual learning objective prevents catastrophic forgetting, ensuring the system maintains knowledge of established normal patterns while adapting to new ones.

## Foundational Learning

**PCA-based novelty detection**: Why needed - Provides a simple, effective method for anomaly detection without labeled data. Quick check - Verify that normal data produces consistently low reconstruction errors while known attacks produce high errors.

**Continual learning without catastrophic forgetting**: Why needed - Intrusion detection systems must adapt to evolving threats while maintaining detection of established attack patterns. Quick check - Test that the system maintains performance on previously seen normal traffic while learning new patterns.

**Unsupervised representation learning**: Why needed - Labeled attack data is scarce and expensive to obtain in real-world scenarios. Quick check - Confirm that the feature extractor learns meaningful distinctions between normal and anomalous traffic without attack labels.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Network traffic -> Continual Feature Extractor -> PCA-based Novelty Detector -> Attack/Novelty Detection

Critical path: Raw network packets → Feature extraction → PCA reconstruction → Threshold comparison → Attack decision

Design tradeoffs: The system prioritizes adaptability to new threats over perfect detection of known attacks, accepting that some known attacks might be missed if they don't significantly deviate from normal traffic patterns. The unsupervised approach sacrifices some precision for the ability to detect zero-day attacks.

Failure signatures: High false positive rates may indicate that the normal behavior distribution is too broad or that the threshold is too sensitive. Catastrophic forgetting would manifest as degraded performance on previously seen normal traffic after learning new patterns.

First experiments: 1) Test PCA reconstruction error distributions on known normal vs. attack data to validate the core detection principle. 2) Evaluate catastrophic forgetting by measuring performance degradation on initial data after training on new data. 3) Benchmark computational overhead to ensure real-time feasibility.

## Limitations

- The framework may struggle with sophisticated attacks that closely mimic normal traffic patterns
- Performance depends on the quality and representativeness of the normal data used for training
- The threshold-based detection may require careful tuning for different network environments and traffic patterns
