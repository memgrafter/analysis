# Edge Nearest Neighbor in Sampling-Based Motion Planning

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2506.13753
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.13753
- Authors: Stav Ashur; Nancy M. Amato; Sariel Har-Peled
- Reference count: 30
- Key outcome: Edge-NN improves narrow passage exploration in sampling-based motion planning by finding closest points on roadmap edges, reducing collision detection calls and iterations

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces edge-NN, a nearest neighbor algorithm for sampling-based motion planning that extends beyond traditional vertex-only approaches by finding the closest point on roadmap edges to a sampled configuration. This method addresses the challenge of narrow passage exploration by enabling extensions from interior points of edges rather than just vertices. The approach uses a hierarchical AABB-tree data structure modified for mixed Euclidean and cyclic coordinate spaces, providing theoretical guarantees of improved edge length distributions and empirical evidence of reduced computational overhead in challenging motion planning scenarios.

## Method Summary
The edge-NN algorithm operates by querying a hierarchical AABB-tree data structure to find the closest point on roadmap edges to a given sampled configuration. Unlike traditional nearest neighbor methods that only consider vertices, edge-NN computes distances to all edges in the roadmap and returns the point of minimum distance. The AABB-tree is augmented to handle mixed Euclidean and cyclic coordinate spaces common in robotic motion planning. This enables RRT and other sampling-based planners to extend from interior points of edges, improving exploration in narrow passages where vertex-only methods struggle. The approach also enables a new cobweb-RRG variant that creates web-like structures across contact space regions for enhanced narrow passage navigation.

## Key Results
- Edge-NN reduces collision detection calls and iterations needed to solve motion planning tasks compared to vertex-only methods
- Theoretical analysis shows edge-NN produces c-space graphs with shorter expected edge lengths, with constant-factor improvement proportional to 1/d in d-dimensional spaces
- The cobweb-RRG variant demonstrates enhanced performance in narrow passage scenarios by creating web-like structures across contact space regions

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
Edge-NN improves narrow passage exploration by enabling extensions from interior points of roadmap edges rather than being limited to vertex extensions. In narrow passages, the probability of sampling configurations near existing vertices is extremely low, making exploration difficult. By considering all points along edges, the algorithm effectively increases the probability of finding valid extension directions. The hierarchical AABB-tree structure enables efficient nearest neighbor queries in O(log n) time while handling the mixed coordinate space requirements of typical robotic motion planning problems. This geometric insight transforms the sampling distribution, making it more likely to generate useful samples in critical regions of the configuration space.

## Foundational Learning
- AABB-tree data structure: Hierarchical spatial indexing for efficient nearest neighbor queries; needed for O(log n) query performance; quick check: verify tree construction and query correctness on simple geometric shapes
- Mixed Euclidean-cyclic coordinate spaces: Handling angular variables that wrap around; needed for realistic robotic joint representations; quick check: test distance calculations across boundary conditions (0° vs 360°)
- Narrow passage sampling bias: Understanding why standard methods fail in constrained regions; needed to motivate edge-NN's approach; quick check: compare vertex-only vs edge-aware sampling distributions in narrow passages
- Probabilistic completeness: Theoretical guarantee that a solution will be found if one exists given infinite time; needed for motion planning algorithm validation; quick check: verify solution existence probability increases with iterations
- Collision detection query optimization: Reducing expensive collision checks through smarter sampling; needed for practical efficiency gains; quick check: measure collision detection call reduction in benchmark scenarios

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Configuration Sampler -> Edge-NN Query Engine -> Collision Detector -> Roadmap Updater

Critical path: Configuration sampling → Edge-NN nearest neighbor search → Local planning → Collision detection → Roadmap insertion

Design tradeoffs: The edge-NN approach trades increased per-query computational complexity for reduced total collision detection calls and improved solution quality. The AABB-tree structure adds memory overhead but enables logarithmic query time. The mixed coordinate space handling adds algorithmic complexity but is necessary for realistic robotic applications.

Failure signatures: Degraded performance occurs when the roadmap becomes too dense (query time increases), when narrow passages are extremely thin (sampling still unlikely), or when the AABB-tree becomes unbalanced (worst-case query performance). Edge-NN may also struggle in high-dimensional spaces where the constant-factor improvements diminish.

First experiments: 1) Compare edge-NN vs vertex-only nearest neighbor on a simple 2D narrow passage problem with known solution; 2) Measure collision detection call reduction across multiple narrow passage scenarios; 3) Benchmark query time overhead versus total planning time improvement in high-dimensional spaces.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
The paper identifies several open questions: How does edge-NN performance scale with increasing dimensionality beyond the analyzed constant-factor improvements? Can the edge-NN concept be extended to other sampling-based planning algorithms beyond RRT and RRG? What are the theoretical limits of narrow passage exploration improvements achievable through edge-aware sampling? How does the cobweb-RRG variant perform on more diverse narrow passage geometries beyond the tested scenarios?

## Limitations
- Theoretical analysis assumes asymptotic behavior that may not fully capture practical performance differences in typical motion planning scenarios
- Empirical results focus primarily on narrow passage scenarios, leaving uncertainty about performance in other common motion planning environments
- The cobweb-RRG variant, while theoretically interesting, lacks thorough experimental validation across diverse scenarios

## Confidence
High: Core algorithmic contribution of edge-NN is well-defined and the hierarchical AABB-tree implementation appears sound
Medium: Theoretical analysis showing improved edge length distributions follows standard probabilistic analysis but makes simplifying assumptions
Medium-Low: Experimental validation is limited by narrow passage focus and lacks comprehensive benchmarking against state-of-the-art methods
