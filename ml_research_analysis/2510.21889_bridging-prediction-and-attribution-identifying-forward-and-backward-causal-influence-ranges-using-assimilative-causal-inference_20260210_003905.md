# Bridging Prediction and Attribution: Identifying Forward and Backward Causal Influence Ranges Using Assimilative Causal Inference

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2510.21889
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.21889
- Authors: Marios Andreou; Nan Chen
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Introduces framework quantifying temporal extent of causal influence using objective, threshold-free metrics for forward and backward CIR

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a mathematically rigorous framework for quantifying the temporal extent of causal influence (CIR) within the Assimilative Causal Inference (ACI) framework. The authors develop objective metrics for both forward CIR (predicting future causal impact) and backward CIR (attributing past causes to current effects), eliminating subjective parameter choices that plague existing methods. The framework is validated through three case studies demonstrating its ability to distinguish tipping mechanisms and reveal hidden causal structures in complex dynamical systems.

## Method Summary
The method computes CIR by leveraging Bayesian data assimilation to compare smoother and filter posteriors. For a system of coupled Itô diffusions, the ACI metric quantifies causal strength via relative entropy between smoother (all data) and filter (past-only) posteriors. Forward CIR measures how information deficit decays as future observations approach the horizon, while backward CIR identifies precursor emergence by examining the limit as observations approach the present. For Conditional Gaussian Nonlinear Systems (CGNS), closed-form expressions enable efficient computation through discrete-time Kalman-like filters and adaptive-lag online smoothers.

## Key Results
- Objective, threshold-free CIR metrics eliminate subjective parameter choices
- Computationally efficient approximation algorithms enable tractable analysis in high-dimensional systems
- Closed-form expressions derived for broad class of nonlinear dynamical systems
- Validated through three case studies distinguishing bifurcation-driven from noise-induced tipping points

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1: Uncertainty Reduction via Smoother-Filter Divergence
- Claim: A variable y(t) causally influences x if future observations of x reduce uncertainty about y(t) beyond what its past history provides.
- Mechanism: ACI computes relative entropy P(p_s(y|x), p_f(y|x)) between the smoother posterior (conditioned on all data, including future) and filter posterior (conditioned only on past). Positive divergence indicates that future x carries information about past y that couldn't be inferred from history alone—this is the causal signature.
- Core assumption: The system exhibits finite memory effects; causal influence decays over time rather than propagating indefinitely.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "utilizes Bayesian data assimilation to trace causes back from observed effects by quantifying the reduction in uncertainty"
  - [section 2.2] "identifying the cause-and-effect relationship is not based on running forward and extrapolating the system... This bypasses the need to impose restrictive model structures"
  - [corpus] The foundational ACI paper (2505.14825) establishes this framework; corpus evidence is thin beyond this direct predecessor.
- Break condition: If smoother and filter posteriors converge (δ ≈ 0), no causal relationship exists at that time—mechanism correctly returns null result.

### Mechanism 2: Forward CIR via Lagged Smoother Information Deficit
- Claim: The forward causal influence range can be quantified by measuring how information deficit δ(t, T') decays as T' approaches the observation horizon T.
- Mechanism: For each time t, the forward CIR metric δ_f(τ;t) measures information lost when using only observations up to T' < T instead of complete data. The objective forward CIR length averages this deficit across all threshold levels ε, eliminating the need for arbitrary cutoff selection. Approximation: τ_f^approx = (1/M_f) ∫_t^T δ(t, T') dT'.
- Core assumption: Causal influence is strongest immediately after the cause and decays monotonically (or near-monotonically) in chaotic systems.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "forward CIR quantifies the temporal impact of a cause... objective and robust metrics... eliminating the need for empirical thresholds"
  - [section 2.3.2] "In chaotic systems, M_f(t) typically occurs at τ=0 or shortly after, reflecting the fact that causal influence is strongest immediately following the cause"
  - [corpus] No direct corpus validation; this is a novel contribution of this paper.
- Break condition: If δ_f(τ;t) fails to decay (infinite memory), forward CIR becomes undefined or requires truncation at observation boundary.

### Mechanism 3: Backward CIR via Complete Information Limit
- Claim: The backward causal influence range identifies when precursors emerged by examining how the CIR metric behaves as T' → T⁻.
- Mechanism: The backward CIR metric δ_b(τ;T') = |δ(τT', T') - δ(0, T')| measures attribution relative to initial-time information deficit. In the limit of complete information incorporation, this reveals which past y(t) states contributed to x(T). For conditionally linear systems, Theorem 2.12 proves τ_b^approx(T) = Θ(1)—backward CIR remains bounded independent of T.
- Core assumption: The complete backward CIR metric is nondecreasing in τ for most practical systems; the approximation τ_b^approx becomes exact under monotonicity.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section 2.3.4] "backward CIR attributes observed effects at time T to past causes... the CIR metric is treated as a function of t, with T fixed and T' sufficiently close to it"
  - [section 2.3.5, Theorem 2.9] Provides computationally efficient upper-bound approximation with equality condition
  - [section 3.1, Figure 4-7] Numerical validation showing backward CIR oscillates between T-constant bounds as predicted
- Break condition: When M_b(T) ≈ 0 (weak causal evidence), the backward CIR may be inflated; must interpret jointly with ACI metric to avoid misattribution.

## Foundational Learning

- Concept: Bayesian Data Assimilation (Filter vs. Smoother)
  - Why needed here: ACI relies on the distinction between filter posteriors (online, past-only) and smoother posteriors (retrospective, all data). Without this, the relative entropy computation is meaningless.
  - Quick check question: Can you explain why a smoother typically has lower uncertainty than a filter for the same time point?

- Concept: Relative Entropy (Kullback-Leibler Divergence)
  - Why needed here: The paper uses relative entropy P(p, q) as the statistical divergence measuring information gain. Understanding its signal-dispersion decomposition (Eq. D.4) is essential for interpreting causal strength.
  - Quick check question: What does it mean when relative entropy equals zero between two distributions?

- Concept: Conditional Gaussian Nonlinear Systems (CGNS)
  - Why needed here: CGNS enable closed-form filter/smoother solutions, making CIR computation tractable. Not all systems qualify; recognizing when your problem fits this class determines implementation path.
  - Quick check question: In a CGNS, which variables appear linearly vs. nonlinearly in the dynamics?

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Filter update -> Smoother backward pass -> Relative entropy computation -> CIR metric evaluation -> Objective CIR integration
- Critical path: Data → Filter → Smoother → Relative Entropy → CIR Metrics → Integration. The smoother computation is the bottleneck; use adaptive-lag online smoother (Appendix D) for CGNS.
- Design tradeoffs:
  - Exact vs. approximate CIR: Exact (Eq. 2.13, 2.21) requires ε-integration; approximations (2.15, 2.23) are faster but biased (underestimate forward, overestimate backward)
  - Ensemble vs. closed-form: Non-CGNS systems require ensemble methods for posteriors—computationally expensive
  - Lag selection: Adaptive smoother lag must balance computational cost against information capture
- Failure signatures:
  - CIR ≈ 0 with high ACI: Check for numerical precision issues in relative entropy
  - Inflated CIR when M_f(t) or M_b(T) ≈ 0: Weak causal evidence; interpret jointly with ACI metric
  - Non-monotonic δ_f causing multivalued subjective CIR: Use objective formulation
- First 3 experiments:
  1. **Validate on known CGNS**: Implement the 3D tipping-point model (Eq. 3.1) with ε=0.01. Verify forward CIR is short for fast weather variable y, backward CIR oscillates between bounded constants. Compare to Figure 4-5.
  2. **Test conditional ACI**: Using the 4D multiscale model (Eq. 3.2), compare unconditional vs. conditional CIR for y₁→x₁|(x₂,y₂). Confirm that conditioning reveals hidden causal structure as shown in Figure 8.
  3. **Stress test monotonicity assumption**: Construct a system where δ_f(τ;t) is non-monotonic. Verify that τ_f^approx < τ_f (underestimate) and τ_b^approx > τ_b (overestimate) as theory predicts.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How robust is the ACI framework against model error?
- Basis: [explicit] Conclusion states assessing "robustness of the framework against model error remains crucial."
- Why unresolved: The current formulation assumes the dynamical models (Eq. 2.1) governing the state variables are known and accurate for the Bayesian update.
- Evidence: Theoretical or numerical analysis demonstrating CIR stability under structural model misspecification or perturbed parameters.

### Open Question 2
- Question: Can the framework be extended to handle noise-contaminated observations?
- Basis: [explicit] Conclusion calls for "investigating ACI under noise-contaminated observations."
- Why unresolved: The current methodology (Page 3) explicitly assumes the observed time series is "free of observational noise" for mathematical convenience.
- Evidence: Derivation of CIR metrics that explicitly incorporate observation error covariance within the likelihood function.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How can significant marginal causes be identified in high-dimensional multivariate settings?
- Basis: [explicit] Conclusion suggests "developing methods to identify significant marginal causes in multivariate settings would enhance causal discovery."
- Why unresolved: While conditional ACI handles non-target variables, systematically determining which variables are significant causes remains a distinct challenge.
- Evidence: Development of a variable selection protocol or sparsity-inducing algorithm for high-dimensional causal graphs.

## Limitations
- Framework applicability depends on meaningful divergence between smoother and filter posteriors
- Monotonicity assumptions for CIR approximations may not hold in highly non-monotonic systems
- Requires accurate knowledge of dynamical models for Bayesian updates

## Confidence

- **High confidence**: Forward CIR mechanism and basic ACI framework (well-established in prior work)
- **Medium confidence**: Closed-form CIR expressions for CGNS systems (novel but mathematically rigorous)
- **Medium confidence**: Approximation algorithms' accuracy (theoretically justified but limited empirical validation)
- **Low confidence**: Generalizability to non-CGNS systems requiring ensemble methods
