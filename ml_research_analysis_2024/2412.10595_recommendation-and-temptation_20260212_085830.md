# Recommendation and Temptation

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2412.10595
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.10595
- Authors: Md Sanzeed Anwar; Paramveer S. Dhillon; Grant Schoenebeck
- Reference count: 40
- One-line primary result: A novel recommender system that models enrichment-temptation tension outperforms baselines by maximizing consumed enrichment

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a novel recommender system that explicitly models the tension between enrichment (inherent value) and temptation (instant appeal) in user behavior. The authors propose a behavioral model incorporating both enrichment and temptation as orthogonal attributes influencing user choices, while also accounting for off-platform alternatives. They formulate a recommendation objective of maximizing consumed enrichment and prove the optimality of a locally greedy recommendation strategy. An estimation framework is presented that learns enrichment and temptation from behavioral data and explicit user feedback. Comprehensive evaluations using synthetic simulations and real-world MovieLens data demonstrate that their approach consistently outperforms competitive baselines that ignore temptation dynamics, achieving higher user enrichment. The work represents a paradigm shift toward more nuanced, user-centric recommender design that better serves long-term user interests.

## Method Summary
The authors develop a behavioral model where users choose items based on a weighted combination of enrichment (inherent value) and temptation (instant appeal). They formulate a recommendation objective to maximize consumed enrichment and prove that a locally greedy strategy is optimal when the platform knows enrichment and temptation values. The estimation framework learns these latent attributes from historical choice data and explicit feedback, leveraging the distinction that users place more weight on enrichment when providing ratings than when making consumption choices. The approach incorporates outside options by estimating their distribution and expected enrichment. The algorithm recommends items that maximize expected enrichment conditional on being chosen over outside alternatives.

## Key Results
- The locally greedy recommendation strategy maximizes consumed enrichment under the proposed behavioral model
- Explicit feedback provides more reliable enrichment signals than implicit choice data due to higher enrichment weighting
- Outside options critically influence optimal recommendations by providing context for user choices
- The proposed approach consistently outperforms competitive baselines (enrichment-only, temptation-only, ratings-based, click-based) in both synthetic simulations and MovieLens experiments

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- **Claim:** The locally greedy recommendation strategy maximizes consumed enrichment because it balances enrichment gain with selection probability.
- **Mechanism:** The recommender selects items that maximize the expected enrichment conditional on being chosen over outside options. This accounts for both the item's enrichment and its temptation (selection likelihood).
- **Core assumption:** User choice is deterministic and based on a weighted combination of enrichment and temptation.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [abstract]: "formulate a novel recommendation objective aligned with maximizing consumed enrichment and prove the optimality of a locally greedy recommendation strategy"
  - [section 3]: Theorem 1 proves the locally greedy strategy is optimal when the platform knows enrichment and temptation values
  - [corpus]: Weak - the corpus focuses on simulation frameworks and user simulators, not the theoretical optimality proof
- **Break condition:** If user choice becomes stochastic or if choice parameters vary significantly over time, the deterministic optimization may no longer be optimal.

### Mechanism 2
- **Claim:** Explicit feedback provides more reliable enrichment signals than implicit choice data.
- **Mechanism:** Users place more weight on enrichment when providing feedback (位_F > 位_C), making ratings better indicators of true enrichment preferences than click behavior.
- **Core assumption:** Users engage in more deliberate reflection when rating items compared to making consumption choices.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [section 2]: "We assume that 位_F > 位_C for all users. This means that users tend to place more weight on enrichment when providing feedback than when choosing what to consume"
  - [abstract]: "present an estimation framework that leverages the distinction between explicit user feedback and implicit choice data"
  - [corpus]: Weak - corpus papers focus on user simulators and recommendation algorithms but don't directly address the feedback vs choice distinction
- **Break condition:** If users provide ratings without reflection (e.g., when pressured or in a hurry), the assumption that 位_F > 位_C may not hold.

### Mechanism 3
- **Claim:** Outside options critically influence optimal recommendations by providing context for user choices.
- **Mechanism:** The platform must estimate the distribution of outside option choice scores and expected enrichment to properly balance on-platform recommendations against off-platform alternatives.
- **Core assumption:** Outside options follow a known distributional form (normal distribution in the paper) and enrichment/choice scores are independent.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [abstract]: "incorporating the reality of off-platform alternatives" and "estimation framework that leverages the distinction between explicit user feedback and implicit choice data while making minimal assumptions about off-platform options"
  - [section 4]: Details the estimation framework for outside options using distributional assumptions
  - [corpus]: Weak - corpus focuses on recommendation systems but doesn't specifically address outside option modeling
- **Break condition:** If outside options don't follow the assumed distribution or if enrichment and choice scores are correlated, the estimation framework may fail.

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map:**
  - User profile module -> Item embedding module -> Outside option module -> Recommendation engine -> Feedback processor

- **Critical path:**
  1. Estimate user and item parameters from historical data
  2. Compute expected enrichment for each candidate item given outside options
  3. Select item with maximum expected enrichment
  4. Update parameters based on observed choice and feedback

- **Design tradeoffs:**
  - Using normal distributions for outside options simplifies estimation but may not capture real-world complexity
  - The locally greedy strategy is computationally efficient but may miss long-term optimization opportunities
  - Separate enrichment and temptation spaces provide interpretability but increase dimensionality

- **Failure signatures:**
  - If enrichment and temptation become correlated, the estimation framework may fail to disentangle them
  - If outside options change rapidly, the distributional assumptions may become invalid
  - If users provide ratings without reflection, the assumption 位_F > 位_C breaks down

- **First 3 experiments:**
  1. **Baseline comparison:** Implement and test against purely enrichment-based, purely temptation-based, ratings-based, and click-based recommenders using the synthetic simulation setup
  2. **Estimation accuracy:** Test the estimation framework's ability to recover true enrichment and temptation values from synthetic data with known ground truth
  3. **Robustness to distribution assumptions:** Replace the normal distribution assumption for outside options with different distributions and measure impact on recommendation performance

## Limitations
- The behavioral model assumes enrichment and temptation are orthogonal attributes with linear aggregation in user choice, which may not capture complex real-world preference structures
- The normal distribution assumption for outside options and the independence between enrichment and temptation scores are critical for the estimation framework's validity but may not hold in practice
- The locally greedy strategy, while proven optimal under the model assumptions, may miss long-term optimization opportunities in dynamic environments

## Confidence
- **High confidence**: The theoretical optimality proof for the locally greedy strategy under the specified behavioral model
- **Medium confidence**: The superiority of the approach in synthetic simulations (due to controlled environment)
- **Medium confidence**: The MovieLens experimental results (real data but with simulation assumptions)
