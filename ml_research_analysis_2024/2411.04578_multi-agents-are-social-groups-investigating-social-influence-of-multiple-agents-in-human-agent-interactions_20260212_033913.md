# Multi-Agents are Social Groups: Investigating Social Influence of Multiple Agents in Human-Agent Interactions

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2411.04578
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.04578
- Authors: Tianqi Song; Yugin Tan; Zicheng Zhu; Yibin Feng; Yi-Chieh Lee
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Multiple AI agents create social influence comparable to human groups, with multi-agent systems leading to stronger opinion shifts and increased perceived normative pressure

## Executive Summary
This study investigates whether multiple AI agents can create social influence comparable to human groups. Through an experiment with 94 participants discussing social issues with one, three, or five AI agents, the research found that multi-agent systems led to stronger opinion shifts and increased perceived normative pressure compared to single-agent interactions. The 3-agent group showed the greatest opinion change when disagreeing with participants, while the 5-agent group increased polarization when agreeing. Younger participants were more susceptible to multi-agent influence.

## Method Summary
The study recruited 94 participants through CloudResearch to discuss social issues with AI agents. Participants were randomly assigned to interact with either 1, 3, or 5 agents using rule-based scripts and GPT-4 API. The experiment included pre- and post-surveys measuring opinions, mid-surveys assessing social influence perceptions, and open-ended questions for qualitative analysis. Two social topics were discussed: self-driving cars and violent video games. The methodology compared opinion change, perceived social influence, and demographic susceptibility across the different agent conditions.

## Key Results
- Multi-agent systems created stronger opinion shifts than single-agent interactions
- 3-agent group showed greatest opinion change when disagreeing with participants
- 5-agent group increased polarization when agreeing with participants
- Younger participants showed higher susceptibility to multi-agent influence

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Multiple agents create normative social pressure that shifts opinions toward consensus
- Mechanism: When agents express unified views, participants perceive them as a social group and feel pressure to conform to avoid social discord
- Core assumption: Participants attribute social group characteristics to multiple agents even though they know they are AI
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "multi-agent systems led to stronger opinion shifts and increased perceived normative pressure compared to single-agent interactions"
  - [section 4.2.2] "participants perceived group-induced social pressure as a critical factor to change their opinions as the number of agents increased"
  - [corpus] Weak - no direct corpus evidence found for AI agent group pressure
- Break condition: If agents show disagreement among themselves or if participants recognize them as identical AI systems without independent thought

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: More agents increase opinion polarization when agents agree with participants
- Mechanism: Reinforcement from multiple agreeing agents strengthens participants' existing beliefs, creating greater polarization
- Core assumption: Agreement from multiple sources amplifies existing beliefs rather than moderating them
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "the 5-agent group increased polarization when agreeing" and "participants became more polarized when agents agreed with them"
  - [section 4.1.1] "when the agents and participants agreed, the 5-agent group participants became more polarized (M=0.580, SD=0.576, Dunn's test: p<0.01) than the 3-agent participants (M=0.132, SD=0.640)"
  - [corpus] Weak - no corpus evidence found for multi-agent agreement polarization
- Break condition: If participants recognize agreement as coming from identical AI sources rather than diverse perspectives

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Psychological reactance occurs when too many agents disagree, reducing persuasion
- Mechanism: Overwhelming disagreement from five agents triggers reactance, causing participants to resist and reinforce their original position
- Core assumption: Excessive social pressure is perceived as a threat to autonomy
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "the 5-agent group led to a sense of polarization, with more participants rejecting the agents' arguments and moving further from the agents' positions"
  - [section 5.2] "unanimous disagreement from five agents may have created an overtly strong sense of pressure... triggering reactive resistance, in which participants rejected the agents' stance"
  - [corpus] Weak - no corpus evidence found for multi-agent disagreement reactance
- Break condition: If disagreement is spread across fewer agents or if agents express empathy and validation

## Foundational Learning

- Concept: Social influence theory (normative vs informational influence)
  - Why needed here: Distinguishes how group consensus affects behavior through conformity pressure versus belief in accuracy
  - Quick check question: What's the difference between conforming because you want to fit in versus conforming because you think the group is right?

- Concept: Psychological reactance theory
  - Why needed here: Explains why excessive social pressure can backfire and cause resistance rather than compliance
  - Quick check question: Why might someone dig in their heels when pressured too strongly to change their mind?

- Concept: Multi-agent system design patterns
  - Why needed here: Understanding how to structure agent interactions for desired social influence effects
  - Quick check question: How would you design agent coordination to maximize persuasion while minimizing reactance?

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Host agent + N discussion agents + survey system + conversation interface
- Critical path: User joins → Host introduces → Agents introduce → Round-robin discussion → Mid-survey → Round-robin discussion → Post-survey
- Design tradeoffs: More agents increase social pressure but also risk reactance; scripted arguments ensure consistency but may reduce perceived authenticity
- Failure signatures: No opinion change despite multiple agents, participants reporting "ganged up" feelings, high dropout rates
- First 3 experiments:
  1. Test 2-agent vs 3-agent conditions to find minimum effective group size
  2. Vary argument quality while holding agent number constant to isolate social influence effect
  3. Test empathy-expressing agents vs neutral agents to measure reactance reduction

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

- Does the effect of multi-agent systems on opinion change vary across different types of social issues, such as more contentious topics like health or politics?
  - Basis in paper: [inferred] from the limitation section noting that the study used "fairly neutral" social issues and suggesting future work explore more contentious areas
  - Why unresolved: The study only tested two relatively neutral topics, so it's unclear if the observed effects generalize to issues with stronger pre-existing opinions or higher polarization
  - What evidence would resolve it: Conducting similar experiments with more contentious or polarizing topics like healthcare policy or political issues to compare the magnitude and direction of opinion change

- What is the optimal number of agents that maximizes persuasive effectiveness without triggering psychological reactance?
  - Basis in paper: [explicit] from the discussion noting that increasing from 3 to 5 agents led to psychological reactance and reduced opinion change, but no clear threshold was identified
  - Why unresolved: The study only tested three group sizes (1, 3, and 5 agents), leaving the precise relationship between group size and persuasion unclear, particularly where reactance begins
  - What evidence would resolve it: Testing intermediate group sizes (e.g., 2 or 4 agents) and measuring both opinion change and reactance indicators to identify the tipping point

- How do cultural differences influence susceptibility to multi-agent social influence?
  - Basis in paper: [explicit] from the limitations section noting that participants were predominantly from Western, individualistic cultures and suggesting future work examine diverse cultural contexts
  - Why unresolved: The study's homogeneous sample limits generalizability, as collectivist cultures may respond differently to group pressure and social norms
  - What evidence would resolve it: Replicating the study with participants from collectivist cultures to compare susceptibility to multi-agent influence and identify cultural moderators

## Limitations
- The study used only two relatively neutral social topics, limiting generalizability to more contentious issues
- Participants were predominantly from Western, individualistic cultures, restricting cultural generalizability
- The use of scripted conversations may not reflect naturalistic agent interactions

## Confidence
- Normative pressure mechanism in 3-agent condition: Medium confidence
- 5-agent polarization finding: Medium-Low confidence
- Age-related susceptibility finding: Low-Medium confidence
