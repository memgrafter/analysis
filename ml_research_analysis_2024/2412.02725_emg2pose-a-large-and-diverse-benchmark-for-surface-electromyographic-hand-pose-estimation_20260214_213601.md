# emg2pose: A Large and Diverse Benchmark for Surface Electromyographic Hand Pose Estimation

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2412.02725
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.02725
- Authors: Sasha Salter; Richard Warren; Collin Schlager; Adrian Spurr; Shangchen Han; Rohin Bhasin; Yujun Cai; Peter Walkington; Anuoluwapo Bolarinwa; Robert Wang; Nathan Danielson; Josh Merel; Eftychios Pnevmatikakis; Jesse Marshall
- Reference count: 37
- Key outcome: Large-scale dataset with 193 users, 370 hours, and 29 stages enables generalization across users, sensor placements, and stages

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces emg2pose, a large-scale benchmark for hand pose estimation from surface electromyography (sEMG) signals. The dataset includes 193 users, 370 hours of recordings, and 29 diverse stages of hand gestures, making it one of the largest publicly available sEMG hand pose datasets. The authors provide competitive baselines, including vemg2pose, NeuroPose, and SensingDynamics, and evaluate them across challenging generalization scenarios: held-out users, sensor placements, and stages. Results show that vemg2pose outperforms other methods, achieving mean angular errors of 12.2째 and landmark distances of 15.8 mm for the held-out user scenario.

## Method Summary
The emg2pose benchmark consists of a large-scale dataset of sEMG recordings from 193 users performing 29 different hand gesture stages, captured using a 16-channel wrist band at 2kHz and a 26-camera motion capture rig. Three baseline models are provided: NeuroPose (CNN-LSTM with positional encoding), SensingDynamics (transformer-based), and vemg2pose (velocity-based CNN-LSTM). The models are trained to minimize L1 error between predicted and ground truth joint angles, with evaluation metrics including mean absolute joint angular error and mean Euclidean landmark distance. The benchmark specifically evaluates generalization across held-out users, sensor placements, and stages.

## Key Results
- vemg2pose achieves mean angular errors of 12.2째 and landmark distances of 15.8 mm for held-out user scenarios
- Increasing training users from 10 to 100 reduces error from 27.6째 to 14.9째 for held-out users
- vemg2pose outperforms NeuroPose and SensingDynamics across all generalization scenarios

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: The velocity-based architecture (vemg2pose) outperforms direct angle prediction because sEMG signals encode muscle activity which is inherently more aligned with motion derivatives than static pose.
- Mechanism: By predicting joint angular velocities and integrating them over time, the model leverages the fact that sEMG is closely related to muscle activation patterns that drive motion rather than the static configuration of the hand.
- Core assumption: Muscle activity measured by sEMG correlates more strongly with the rate of change of joint angles (velocities) than with absolute joint angles.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "sEMG is particularly well suited for kinematic inference" and "sEMG encodes muscle activity, which relates more closely to motion than the pose"
  - [section]: "vemg2pose predicts joint angular velocities, which are then integrated to produce joint angle predictions" and "sEMG is first embedded via a causal strided convolutional featurizer"
  - [corpus]: Weak - no direct evidence in corpus papers about velocity-based approaches
- Break condition: If sEMG signals contain significant phase shifts or delays that make velocity prediction less reliable than direct angle prediction for certain gesture types.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Large-scale diverse data across users, sensor placements, and stages enables generalization to held-out conditions.
- Mechanism: The dataset's scale (193 users, 370 hours, 29 stages) provides sufficient coverage of the variation space in user anatomy, sensor placement, and hand kinematics, allowing models to learn invariant features.
- Core assumption: The variation in sEMG signals across users, sensor placements, and kinematic stages follows patterns that can be learned and generalized from sufficient training data.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "This is due to sEMG sensing containing many axes of variation, primarily: user anatomy, sensor placement, and hand kinematics"
  - [section]: "We show that increasing the number of training users considerably reduces the error for held-out users" and "increasing the number of stages per-user improves performance"
  - [corpus]: Weak - corpus papers focus on smaller datasets and don't address the scale needed for generalization
- Break condition: If the variation space is too high-dimensional or discontinuous for the model to learn meaningful generalizations from the available data.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Time-Depth Separable Convolution (TDS) architecture provides effective feature extraction for sEMG signals.
- Mechanism: TDS architecture, proven effective in automatic speech recognition, efficiently captures temporal patterns in sEMG signals through strided convolutions and depthwise separable operations.
- Core assumption: The temporal patterns in sEMG signals are similar enough to speech signals that architectures proven effective for speech will also work well for sEMG.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section]: "A Time-Depth Separable Convolution (TDS) network is used for the featurizer, as it has been shown to be effective and parameter-efficient in the automatic speech recognition literature"
  - [corpus]: Weak - corpus doesn't provide evidence about TDS effectiveness for sEMG
- Break condition: If sEMG temporal patterns differ fundamentally from speech patterns in ways that make TDS less effective.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: sEMG (16-channel, 2kHz) -> TDS featurizer (temporal down-sampling to 50Hz) -> LSTM decoder (velocity prediction) -> Integration (angle prediction) -> Output (joint angles/landmarks)
- Critical path: sEMG -> TDS featurizer -> LSTM decoder -> Velocity integration -> Joint angle output
- Design tradeoffs: Velocity-based vs direct angle prediction (tradeoff between smoothness and complexity), TDS vs other CNN architectures (parameter efficiency vs representational power)
- Failure signatures: Angular drift over time, poor performance on specific fingers (pinky), difficulty with intrinsic hand muscle movements, overfitting to training users

## Limitations
- Dataset lacks coverage for real-world signal aggressors like electrode-skin contact artifacts, sweat-induced impedance changes, and electrical interference
- Model personalization techniques are not explored despite known benefits for individual anatomy differences
- The velocity-based approach's advantages need more extensive validation across diverse gesture types and scenarios

## Confidence

- **High Confidence**: The dataset's scale and diversity (193 users, 370 hours, 29 stages) are well-established facts with clear evidence in the paper. The evaluation methodology and benchmark setup are clearly specified and reproducible.

- **Medium Confidence**: The claim that vemg2pose outperforms other methods is supported by experimental results, but the magnitude of improvement and generalizability across all scenarios need further validation. The theoretical advantages of velocity-based prediction over direct angle prediction are plausible but require more extensive empirical support.

- **Low Confidence**: The effectiveness of TDS architecture specifically for sEMG signals is assumed based on speech recognition applications, but lacks direct evidence. The claim about sEMG encoding motion rather than pose is theoretically grounded but needs more physiological validation.
