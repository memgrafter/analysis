# "Global is Good, Local is Bad?": Understanding Brand Bias in LLMs

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2406.13997
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.13997
- Authors: Mahammed Kamruzzaman; Hieu Minh Nguyen; Gene Louis Kim
- Reference count: 8
- Key outcome: Systematic brand bias in LLMs, with global brands associated with positive attributes and luxury brands recommended for high-income countries

## Executive Summary
This study reveals significant brand bias in large language models, where global brands are disproportionately associated with positive attributes while local brands receive negative associations. Using a dataset spanning four brand categories (shoes, clothing, beverages, electronics) and experiments with four major LLMs (GPT-4o, Llama-3-8B, Gemma-7B, Mistral-7B), the research demonstrates consistent patterns of bias across all models. The bias persists even when using generic terms instead of specific brand names, suggesting deep-rooted associations learned from training data.

The findings show that LLMs exhibit socio-economic bias in product recommendations, favoring luxury brands for high-income countries (98.88-100% for luxury) versus low-income countries (1.11-16.05% for luxury). Additionally, models display country-of-origin effects, favoring local brands when domestic countries are specified (75-84.95% preference), except for Gemma-7B. These biases have significant implications for market competition and the perpetuation of inequalities in product recommendations.

## Method Summary
The study employs three experimental setups using fill-in-the-blank style questions with four LLMs: GPT-4o, Llama-3-8B, Gemma-7B, and Mistral-7B. Experiment 1 tests stimulus-to-attribute inference (SAI) and attribute-to-stimulus association (ASA) for brand-attribute relationships. Experiment 2 examines socio-economic bias by asking models to recommend luxury versus non-luxury gifts for high versus low-income countries. Experiment 3 investigates country-of-origin effects by prompting models to choose between local and global brands when a country is specified. The researchers use Kendall's τ test to measure statistical significance of associations, with α < 0.05 as the threshold.

## Key Results
- LLMs show significant systematic brand bias (p < 0.001 in most cases, τ values 0.037-0.490) associating global brands with positive attributes
- All models consistently recommend luxury brands for high-income countries (98.88-100%) versus low-income countries (1.11-16.05%)
- Models exhibit country-of-origin effects, favoring local brands when domestic countries are specified (75-84.95% preference), except Gemma-7B
- Bias persists even with generic terms instead of specific brand names, suggesting deep-rooted associations

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: LLMs associate global brands with positive attributes more strongly than local brands due to training data imbalances
- Mechanism: LLMs learn implicit associations from co-occurrence patterns in training data, where global brands appear more frequently and in more positive contexts
- Core assumption: Training data contains systematic overrepresentation of global brands in positive contexts
- Evidence anchors: Abstract states models "disproportionately associating global brands with positive attributes"; section shows all τ effects are positive
- Break condition: If training data is balanced or if models are trained with explicit debiasing techniques

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: LLMs exhibit socio-economic bias in product recommendations, favoring luxury brands for high-income countries
- Mechanism: Models learn correlations between product attributes and economic indicators from training data, applying these correlations when prompted with country information
- Core assumption: Training data contains strong correlations between economic indicators and product types
- Evidence anchors: Abstract mentions "disproportionately recommending luxury gifts for individuals in high-income countries"; section shows consistent luxury recommendations for high-income countries
- Break condition: If models are explicitly trained to ignore economic indicators

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: LLMs exhibit country-of-origin effects, favoring local brands when domestic country is specified
- Mechanism: Models have learned country-specific brand associations from training data where local brands are more frequently discussed in home country contexts
- Core assumption: Training data contains strong country-specific brand associations
- Evidence anchors: Abstract states models "favor local brands over global ones when the domestic country is specified"; section shows all models except Gemma suggest local brands when country name is included
- Break condition: If models are explicitly trained to ignore country information

## Limitations
- The study does not examine underlying training data composition, making it difficult to definitively attribute bias origins to specific data patterns
- Use of fill-in-the-blank prompts may not fully capture real-world usage scenarios where brand associations emerge more naturally
- While the study identifies bias existence, it does not explore potential mitigation strategies or practical impact on end users

## Confidence
- High confidence: Identifying systematic brand bias patterns across multiple LLMs with robust statistical significance
- Medium confidence: Generalizability of results due to focus on four specific brand categories and 15 countries
- Medium confidence: Connecting identified bias patterns to real-world market outcomes and consumer behavior

## Next Checks
1. Replicate the experiments with additional brand categories (e.g., food, cosmetics, automotive) to test generalizability across product types
2. Analyze the training data distribution of major LLMs to identify whether global brand overrepresentation directly correlates with observed biases
3. Conduct user studies to measure the real-world impact of these biases on consumer decision-making and market outcomes
