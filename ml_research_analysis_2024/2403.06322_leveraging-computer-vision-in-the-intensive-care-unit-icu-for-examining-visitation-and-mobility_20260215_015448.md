# Leveraging Computer Vision in the Intensive Care Unit (ICU) for Examining Visitation and Mobility

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2403.06322
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.06322
- Authors: Scott Siegel; Jiaqing Zhang; Sabyasachi Bandyopadhyay; Subhash Nerella; Brandon Silva; Tezcan Baslanti; Azra Bihorac; Parisa Rashidi
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Computer vision system using depth imaging achieves mAP of 0.79-0.88 for detecting patient postures and counting room visitors in ICU

## Executive Summary
This study developed a computer vision system using depth imaging to monitor patient mobility and hospital room visitations in the ICU, addressing the challenge of limited monitoring due to healthcare provider time constraints. The authors trained a YOLOv8 deep learning model on 30,840 annotated depth frames from 44 ICU patients to detect patient postures and count room visitors. The system achieved strong detection performance (mAP of 0.79 for lying-in-bed class and 0.88 for standing class) while preserving patient privacy through depth imaging. The analysis revealed significant correlations between hospital room visitations and patient outcomes, with higher visitations associated with lower self-reported pain scores but higher objective measures of patient acuity and delirium.

## Method Summary
The study captured depth video of ICU patients using Microsoft Kinect cameras, training a YOLOv8 model on 30,840 annotated depth frames to detect patient postures (lying in bed, standing, sitting on bed, sitting on chair) and count room visitors. The model was trained using 5-fold cross-validation, with performance evaluated using mean average precision (mAP). The authors extracted metrics including proportion of lying-in-bed frames, average and variance of visitations, then correlated these with patient outcomes including pain scores, acuity measures, and delirium instances. Temporal alignment between video frames and clinical outcomes was required for the correlation analysis.

## Key Results
- YOLOv8 achieved mAP of 0.79 for detecting patients lying in bed and 0.88 for detecting patients standing
- Higher hospital room visitation variance correlated with less stable objective patient outcomes (acuity and delirium)
- Higher visitation counts correlated with lower self-reported pain scores, suggesting social presence may bias pain reporting

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Using depth imaging with YOLOv8 reduces nurse workload while preserving patient privacy.
- Mechanism: Depth cameras capture 3D volumetric data without exposing identifiable facial features, and YOLOv8 processes these frames in real time to detect patient postures and count room visitors.
- Core assumption: Depth frames contain sufficient contrast between human bodies and background for reliable detection.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "depth video [39] of patients during their stay"
  - [section] "We captured depth frames in the ICU using Microsoft Kinect cameras [38], which captured 3D volumetric data of the ICU environment while safeguarding the physical identities of each person."
  - [corpus] Weak corpus support; related works focus on structured EHR or sensor fusion rather than depth imaging for privacy.
- Break condition: If environmental clutter or poor lighting reduces depth contrast, detection accuracy will degrade.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Higher visitation variance correlates with objective patient deterioration.
- Mechanism: Increased fluctuations in room occupancy reflect more frequent interventions or unstable clinical status, captured as higher visitation variance in video sequences.
- Core assumption: Visitation changes are driven by clinical necessity rather than random activity.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "objective measurements, including patient acuity and instances of delirium, were significantly less stable when more individuals were in the room."
  - [section] "For patient acuity, hospital room visitations average and variance were both significantly higher for unstable frame sequences compared to stable sequences"
  - [corpus] Weak corpus support; no related paper directly links visitation variance to objective deterioration metrics.
- Break condition: If visitation changes are due to non-clinical factors (e.g., family visits), the correlation may not hold.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Lower self-reported pain coincides with higher visitation counts.
- Mechanism: Social presence (more people) may bias patients to underreport pain, leading to lower self-reported pain scores.
- Core assumption: Patients modify pain reporting based on social context.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "Patients reported being in less pain when hospital rooms had higher visitations."
  - [section] "According to Boring et al., patients tend to downplay their pain when in the presence of friends or family members"
  - [corpus] Weak corpus support; related works focus on delirium or acuity, not pain reporting bias.
- Break condition: If visitation counts are dominated by clinical staff rather than family, the social bias may be minimal.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: Does the presence of family members in ICU rooms significantly affect patients' self-reported pain scores compared to healthcare providers?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The authors note that they couldn't distinguish between family members and healthcare providers in the video frames, and mention that patients tend to downplay pain in the presence of friends or family to avoid causing worry.
- Why unresolved: The study only measured total room visitation counts without identifying individual relationships to the patient, limiting the ability to differentiate between family and staff presence.
- What evidence would resolve it: Additional analysis using tracking technology to identify and classify individuals in the room, then comparing self-reported pain scores during periods when family members versus healthcare providers are present.

### Open Question 2
- Question: Is there a causal relationship between hospital room visitations and patient outcomes, or are increased visitations merely a symptom of deteriorating patient conditions?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The authors acknowledge that patients may press nurse call buttons when conditions worsen, and that emergency medical staff may be called in, suggesting visitations could be a consequence rather than a cause of poor outcomes.
- Why unresolved: The current analysis only shows correlation between visitation and outcomes, not causation. The temporal relationship between when patients' conditions deteriorate and when visitations increase hasn't been established.
- What evidence would resolve it: Longitudinal data tracking the timing of patient condition changes relative to visitations, controlling for factors like nurse call button usage and monitoring equipment alerts.

### Open Question 3
- Question: Which specific environmental factors (noise, light, or other disturbances) contribute most significantly to sleep deprivation and delirium onset in ICU patients?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The authors acknowledge that their current analysis only considers the number of people in rooms as a disturbance factor, while noting that other stimuli like light and sound also impact sleep.
- Why unresolved: The study focused solely on counting people using depth cameras, without integrating other sensor data about the ICU environment that could identify specific disturbance sources.
- What evidence would resolve it: Integration of real-time noise level monitoring, light intensity measurements, and other environmental sensors with the patient outcome data to perform multivariate analysis identifying the most significant disturbance factors.

## Limitations

- The study's generalizability is limited by its single-ICU setting and relatively small sample size (44 patients), which may not capture the full spectrum of ICU environments and patient populations.
- The depth imaging approach, while preserving privacy, may miss critical visual cues that RGB imaging would capture, potentially affecting detection accuracy for subtle patient movements.
- The observational nature of the study prevents establishing causal relationships between visitation patterns and patient outcomes, leaving open the possibility of confounding factors.

## Confidence

- **High confidence**: The technical implementation of YOLOv8 for posture detection from depth frames (mAP scores of 0.79-0.88 are consistent with published benchmarks for similar tasks).
- **Medium confidence**: The statistical correlations between visitation metrics and patient outcomes, as the sample size and single-center design limit generalizability.
- **Medium confidence**: The interpretation that visitation patterns reflect clinical instability, as alternative explanations (non-clinical visits) were not systematically controlled for.

## Next Checks

1. Replicate the study in multiple ICUs with diverse patient populations to assess generalizability of the visitation-outcome associations.
2. Conduct a controlled intervention study where visitation patterns are deliberately modified to test causality between visitations and pain reporting.
3. Compare depth-based monitoring performance against RGB-based systems on the same patient cohort to quantify any trade-offs between privacy preservation and detection accuracy.
