# Calibrating for the Future:Enhancing Calorimeter Longevity with Deep Learning

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the critical challenge of calibrating aging calorimeters in high-energy physics experiments where radiation damage degrades detector performance over time. The authors propose a novel unsupervised approach using a Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) to automatically learn aging coefficients that align damaged calorimeter data distributions with undamaged references. By leveraging the Wasserstein distance for loss calculation, this method achieves high precision calibration while requiring significantly fewer events and computational resources compared to traditional approaches.

The core innovation lies in treating calibration as a distribution alignment problem rather than a supervised regression task. The generator network learns aging coefficients as weights that transform damaged data to match the undamaged distribution, while the critic estimates the Wasserstein distance between these distributions. This approach eliminates the need for labeled aging coefficients and demonstrates effective calibration with limited data, showing promise for enhancing calorimeter longevity in future particle physics experiments.

## Method Summary
The method employs a Wasserstein GAN architecture where the generator learns aging coefficients that transform damaged calorimeter data to match undamaged distributions. The generator takes damaged calorimeter cell responses as input and outputs calibrated values, with its weights representing the predicted aging coefficients. The critic network estimates the Wasserstein distance between the damaged data and generator output, providing gradients for training. The model is trained on paired damaged/undamaged datasets using 5000 events per state, operating in an unsupervised manner without requiring labeled aging coefficients. The approach focuses on cell-by-cell calibration, treating aging effects as multiplicative coefficients applied to individual cell responses.

## Key Results
- Achieved Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 0.0074 in predicting aging coefficients using 5000 events per damaged/undamaged state
- Successfully aligned calibrated data distribution with undamaged calorimeter distribution, demonstrating effective calibration
- Demonstrated efficient calibration requiring significantly fewer events and computational resources compared to traditional methods

- Core assumption: The aging effects can be represented as multiplicative coefficients applied to individual cell responses, and these coefficients are consistent across the dataset.

- Core assumption: The damaged and undamaged datasets contain the same underlying particle interactions, just with different detector responses due to aging.

- Core assumption: The WGAN training dynamics are robust enough to handle the specific characteristics of calorimeter data distributions.
- Evidence anchors:

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Damaged calorimeter data -> Generator (aging coefficients) -> Calibrated data; Damaged calorimeter data and calibrated data -> Critic (Wasserstein distance)
- Critical path:
  1. Load paired damaged/undamaged calorimeter datasets
  2. Initialize Generator and Critic networks
  3. Train Critic to estimate Wasserstein distance
  4. Update Generator weights to minimize distance
  5. Extract aging coefficients from Generator weights
  6. Validate calibration by comparing distributions

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Unsupervised vs. supervised: Avoids need for labeled aging coefficients but requires paired datasets
  - WGAN vs. standard GAN: Better training stability but requires additional constraints
  - Cell-level vs. global calibration: More precise but increases parameter count

- Failure signatures:
  - Generator weights diverging or becoming unstable
  - Critic loss plateauing without improvement
  - MAE not decreasing over training epochs
  - Calibrated distribution not matching undamaged distribution

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Train on synthetic data with known aging coefficients to verify convergence
  2. Test on subset of cells to validate cell-level calibration capability
  3. Apply to real damaged calorimeter data to assess practical effectiveness

## Limitations
- Relies on Monte Carlo simulations rather than real damaged calorimeter data, potentially missing real-world complexities
- Assumes aging effects can be represented as simple multiplicative coefficients, which may be overly simplistic for actual radiation damage patterns
- Does not provide direct ground truth validation for predicted aging coefficients due to unsupervised nature of the approach

## Confidence
- High confidence in the theoretical foundation of using WGAN for distribution alignment
- Medium confidence in the applicability to real calorimeter systems based on simulation results
- Low confidence in the generalization to different calorimeter geometries and aging mechanisms
