# Representative Social Choice: From Learning Theory to AI Alignment

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2410.23953
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.23953
- Reference count: 29
- Primary result: Proposes a framework that generalizes social choice theory using statistical learning theory to analyze collective decision-making with large numbers of issues and individuals

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a novel framework called "representative social choice" that extends traditional social choice theory to handle situations where the number of issues and individuals is too large for all preferences to be considered directly. The framework treats collective decision-making as a statistical learning problem, where a finite sample of individual-issue pairs represents the population. By applying machine learning theory, the author establishes generalization bounds showing when social choice mechanisms can approximate population preferences, and proves impossibility theorems analogous to Arrow's theorem with new combinatorial tools.

The framework has significant applications in AI alignment, particularly for aligning language models to diverse human preferences. It provides theoretical foundations for understanding how collective preferences can be represented and aggregated in high-dimensional settings, addressing fundamental challenges in both social choice theory and machine learning. The work bridges two fields by showing how learning-theoretic tools can resolve deep questions in social choice, while social choice problems motivate new questions in learning theory.

## Method Summary
The method frames collective decision-making as a statistical learning problem where preferences are drawn from a distribution over a large space of issues and individuals. A finite sample of individual-issue pairs is collected, and a social choice mechanism selects a preference profile from a candidate space that best represents the population. The framework uses VC dimension and Rademacher complexity to establish generalization bounds, showing when mechanisms like majority vote and scoring rules can approximate population preferences. Impossibility theorems are proven using privilege graphs that capture structural dependencies between outcomes, with cyclicity identified as both necessary and sufficient for the existence of conflicting constraints.

## Key Results
- Generalization bounds show that majority vote and scoring mechanisms can approximate population preferences when candidate space complexity is controlled relative to sample size
- Impossibility theorems demonstrate fundamental trade-offs between desirable properties like fairness and utility maximization, analogous to Arrow's theorem
- The framework successfully models AI alignment scenarios, providing theoretical foundations for preference aggregation in high-dimensional settings
- Cyclicity in privilege graphs is proven to be both necessary and sufficient for impossibility results in representative social choice

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: Can we extend the representative social choice framework to handle out-of-distribution generalization to unseen outcomes or issues?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The authors note this as an open direction in the conclusion, mentioning it's vital for generative AI models
- Why unresolved: The current framework focuses on generalization bounds for known outcomes and issues. Extending to OOD scenarios requires new theoretical tools and assumptions about the structure of the outcome space
- What evidence would resolve it: A formal framework that characterizes when generalization bounds can be extended to unseen outcomes/issues, potentially using techniques from transfer learning or meta-learning

### Open Question 2
- Question: Are there stronger impossibility theorems that characterize all candidate spaces associated with a given privilege graph, rather than just constructing counterexamples for at least one?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The authors note in Theorem 4 that their "necessary" part is not as strong as hoped, only constructing counterexamples for at least one C associated with each non-cyclic privilege graph
- Why unresolved: The current proof technique only shows that some candidate space with a given privilege graph leads to impossibility, but doesn't establish that all such spaces do
- What evidence would resolve it: A proof showing that for any candidate space C whose privilege graph contains a cycle, no mechanism can satisfy the axioms, or a counterexample showing some cyclic privilege graph allows a mechanism satisfying the axioms

### Open Question 3
- Question: How can we extend the framework to handle noise and inconsistency in human preferences, which is a significant practical challenge?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The authors mention in the limitations section that their model doesn't account for noise or inconsistency in human preferences
- Why unresolved: The current framework assumes preferences are drawn from a distribution, but doesn't model noise or strategic behavior in preference elicitation
- What evidence would resolve it: A formal model incorporating preference noise, along with bounds on how this affects generalization and mechanism design, or empirical studies showing the impact of preference noise on real-world applications

### Open Question 4
- Question: What are the interactions between generalization, incentive compatibility, and computational tractability in representative social choice mechanisms?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The authors mention in the limitations section that they focused on generalization without studying other properties like incentive compatibility and computational tractability
- Why unresolved: The current framework establishes generalization bounds but doesn't explore how these interact with other desiderata for practical mechanisms
- What evidence would resolve it: Formal analysis showing trade-offs between these properties, or concrete mechanisms that achieve good performance across multiple dimensions simultaneously

## Limitations
- Theoretical gaps: Generalization bounds rely on finite VC dimension or bounded Rademacher complexity assumptions that may not hold for complex real-world candidate spaces
- Practical applicability concerns: The direct translation from theory to practice remains unclear, particularly for constructing candidate spaces for specific applications like AI alignment
- Sample complexity: The required sample sizes for achieving good generalization bounds may be prohibitive in practice, especially for high-dimensional candidate spaces

## Confidence
**High confidence**: The basic framework of modeling representative social choice as a statistical learning problem is well-established and the connection to generalization theory is sound. The impossibility theorems for binary representative social choice (Theorems 3 and 4) have rigorous proofs based on established combinatorial arguments.

**Medium confidence**: The generalization bounds for majority vote and scoring mechanisms (Theorems 1, 5, and Corollaries 5.3, 6.2) follow standard statistical learning theory arguments but rely on assumptions about candidate space complexity that may not hold in practice. The extension to non-binary cases using Rademacher complexity is theoretically sound but may be difficult to verify empirically.

**Low confidence**: The application to AI alignment and the specific construction of privilege graphs for real-world scenarios are promising directions but lack concrete validation. The trade-offs between different axioms and their real-world implications need further exploration.
