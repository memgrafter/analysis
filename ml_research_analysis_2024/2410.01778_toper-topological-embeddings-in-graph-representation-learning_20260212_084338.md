# TopER: Topological Embeddings in Graph Representation Learning

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2410.01778
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.01778
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Achieved second overall ranking among 22 SOTA models with 1.60% average deviation from best results

## Executive Summary
TopER introduces a topology-inspired graph embedding method that simplifies Persistent Homology by tracking the evolution rate of graph substructures during filtration. It computes low-dimensional embeddings through linear regression on node-edge counts across filtration graphs, yielding intuitive 2D visualizations that reveal structural patterns, clusters, and outliers. The method achieves competitive performance in graph classification and clustering while offering strong interpretability and computational efficiency, processing 100K-node graphs in under two minutes.

## Method Summary
TopER tracks the evolution rate of graph substructures during filtration by computing node-edge counts across a sequence of filtration graphs. The method applies linear regression to these counts to generate low-dimensional embeddings, producing 2D visualizations that capture structural patterns and topological features. This approach simplifies traditional Persistent Homology computation while maintaining the ability to reveal clusters, outliers, and meaningful structural relationships in graph data.

## Key Results
- Ranked second overall among 22 SOTA models with average 1.60% deviation from best results
- Processes 100K-node graphs in under two minutes
- Enables intuitive 2D visualizations revealing structural patterns, clusters, and outliers

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method works by simplifying Persistent Homology through evolution rate tracking of graph substructures during filtration. Instead of computing full PH signatures, TopER focuses on how node-edge counts change across filtration graphs. This evolution rate captures essential topological information while being computationally tractable. The linear regression on these counts extracts low-dimensional embeddings that preserve structural relationships, enabling both effective classification performance and interpretable visualizations.

## Foundational Learning
- **Persistent Homology**: A topological data analysis tool that tracks how topological features (connected components, loops, voids) appear and disappear across filtration scales. Needed to understand the theoretical foundation TopER simplifies. Quick check: Verify understanding of birth-death pairs and persistence diagrams.

- **Graph Filtration**: A sequence of graphs obtained by progressively adding edges or nodes based on some ordering criterion. ## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Graph -> Filtration Construction -> Node-Edge Counting -> Linear Regression -> 2D Embedding

**Critical Path**: The core pipeline flows from input graph through filtration construction (where edges/nodes are added progressively based on criteria like weight or degree), to counting nodes and edges at each filtration step, then applying linear regression to these counts to produce the final 2D embedding.

**Design Tradeoffs**: TopER trades full PH computation complexity for simplified evolution rate tracking, gaining computational efficiency at the potential cost of missing fine-grained topological details. The choice of linear regression assumes approximately linear relationships in evolution patterns, which may not hold for all graph types.

**Failure Signatures**: Poor performance expected on graphs with highly non-linear topological evolution patterns, or where critical structural information is encoded in features beyond node-edge counts. Dense graphs with uniform edge distributions may produce embeddings with low discriminative power.

**First Experiments**: 1) Apply to simple synthetic graphs (cycles, complete graphs) to verify expected topological patterns appear in embeddings. 2) Test on graphs with known clusters to validate clustering visualization capability. 3) Compare embeddings of structurally similar vs dissimilar graphs to assess discriminative power.
