# Multi-Excitation Projective Simulation with a Many-Body Physics Inspired Inductive Bias

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2402.10192
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.10192
- Reference count: 40
- Key outcome: Introduces MEPS framework modeling composite thoughts as multi-particle random walks on hypergraphs with quantum many-body physics-inspired inductive bias for improved interpretability and efficiency

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces Multi-Excitation Projective Simulation (MEPS), a framework that extends Projective Simulation (PS) by modeling chains of thought as random walks of multiple particles on hypergraphs rather than single particles on graphs. This enables explicit representation of composite thoughts combining several concepts simultaneously, addressing interpretability limitations in deep learning models. The authors introduce an inductive bias inspired by few-body interactions in quantum many-body physics to tackle the exponential complexity associated with naive hypergraph implementations, reducing computational complexity from exponential to polynomial scaling in the number of graph vertices.

## Method Summary
MEPS extends standard PS by representing mental states as collections of excitations (particles) on hypergraph vertices, where each excitation corresponds to a concept or percepts. These excitations undergo random walks governed by learned transition probabilities, with composite thoughts emerging from multi-excitation configurations. The quantum many-body physics-inspired inductive bias restricts interactions to few-body terms, dramatically reducing the number of trainable parameters. The framework is trained using reinforcement learning to optimize action selection in various environments. A quantum implementation approach based on quantum walks is also outlined, leveraging the formal connection between the inductive bias and quantum many-body physics.

## Key Results
- MEPS agents achieve superior performance and interpretability compared to standard PS and Q-learning baselines in three synthetic environments
- The few-body inductive bias reduces trainable parameters from exponential to polynomial scaling in graph size
- MEPS requires significantly fewer parameters and training steps to reach optimal policies compared to baseline methods
- The framework successfully models composite thoughts combining multiple concepts simultaneously

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework works by representing mental states as multi-particle configurations on hypergraphs, where each particle represents a concept or percept. Random walks of these particles generate chains of thought, with the quantum many-body physics-inspired inductive bias ensuring computational tractability. The few-body interaction assumption limits the number of trainable parameters while maintaining the ability to represent complex, composite thoughts. This structure enables both efficient learning and improved interpretability compared to black-box deep learning models.

## Foundational Learning
- **Projective Simulation**: A reinforcement learning framework using episodic memories represented as directed graphs for decision-making; needed to understand the baseline PS framework being extended
- **Quantum Many-Body Physics**: Physics of systems with many interacting particles; needed to understand the inductive bias inspiration and its computational benefits
- **Hypergraphs**: Generalization of graphs where edges can connect any number of vertices; needed to represent composite thoughts involving multiple concepts
- **Random Walks**: Stochastic processes describing paths consisting of random steps; needed to model chains of thought as particle movements
- **Quantum Walks**: Quantum mechanical generalization of random walks; needed for the proposed quantum implementation approach

## Architecture Onboarding

Component Map: Percepts/Concepts -> Excitations (Particles) -> Hypergraph Vertices -> Random Walks -> Composite Thoughts -> Action Selection

Critical Path: Input percepts trigger excitation creation → Multiple excitations undergo random walks on hypergraph → Composite thought configurations emerge → Transition probabilities guide walks toward rewarding actions → Learning updates transition probabilities based on outcomes

Design Tradeoffs: The few-body interaction assumption trades representational capacity for computational efficiency, while hypergraph representation trades simplicity for the ability to model complex, multi-concept thoughts.

Failure Signatures: Poor performance may indicate insufficient few-body interaction terms, inadequate hypergraph structure, or suboptimal learning rates. Interpretability issues may arise from overly complex excitation configurations.

## Limitations
- Evaluation restricted to three simple synthetic environments, limiting generalizability to complex real-world applications
- The few-body interaction assumption requires empirical validation across diverse problem domains
- Interpretability claims need systematic evaluation with established metrics rather than qualitative comparisons
- Quantum implementation remains theoretical with no experimental validation presented
