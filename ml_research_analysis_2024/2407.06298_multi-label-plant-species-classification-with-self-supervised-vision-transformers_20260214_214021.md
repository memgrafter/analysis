# Multi-Label Plant Species Classification with Self-Supervised Vision Transformers

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2407.06298
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.06298
- Authors: Murilo Gustineli; Anthony Miyaguchi; Ian Stalter
- Reference count: 14
- Third place ranking on PlantCLEF 2024 competition leaderboard

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a multi-label plant species classification approach using self-supervised Vision Transformers for the PlantCLEF 2024 competition. The authors leverage DINOv2, a self-supervised Vision Transformer model, for feature extraction and employ a grid-based inference strategy to handle the multi-label classification challenge. Their approach combines base and fine-tuned DINOv2 models with linear classifiers trained on single-label data, achieving competitive results on the public leaderboard with a ranking of third place.

## Method Summary
The authors propose a transfer learning approach that utilizes both base and fine-tuned DINOv2 models to extract feature embeddings from single-label training data. These embeddings are then used to train linear classifiers for multi-label prediction. To address the computational challenges of large-scale processing, they employ Spark for distributed processing and transform images into grids of tiles for classification. The grid-based inference approach with argmax logit per tile is employed to generate the final multi-label predictions.

## Key Results
- 20.77 Macro F1 Averaged Per Plot
- 47.42 Macro F1 Averaged Per Species
- 19.67 Micro F1
- Third place ranking on PlantCLEF 2024 public leaderboard

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The approach leverages the strong feature extraction capabilities of self-supervised Vision Transformers (DINOv2) to capture rich visual representations of plant species. By combining base and fine-tuned models, the system benefits from both general and domain-specific features. The grid-based inference strategy allows for more granular analysis of images, potentially capturing multiple species present in a single plot. The use of Spark for distributed processing enables efficient handling of the large-scale dataset, making the approach computationally feasible.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Image -> DINOv2 Feature Extraction -> Grid-based Inference -> Multi-label Classification

Critical path: Image preprocessing and tiling -> Feature extraction with DINOv2 -> Grid-based inference -> Final multi-label prediction

Design tradeoffs:
- Single-label training data vs. multi-label prediction: Addresses data scarcity but may limit model's ability to learn complex multi-label relationships
- Grid-based inference vs. whole-image classification: Improves species detection granularity but increases computational complexity
- Self-supervised vs. supervised pretraining: Reduces labeling requirements but may sacrifice some task-specific performance

Failure signatures:
- Low Macro F1 scores indicating imbalanced performance across species
- Potential overfitting to dominant species due to single-label training data
- Computational bottlenecks in grid-based processing for very large datasets
