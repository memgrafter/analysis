# Transfer Learning in Human Activity Recognition: A Survey

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2401.10185
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.10185
- Authors: Sourish Gunesh Dhekane; Thomas Ploetz
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Comprehensive analysis of 205 papers on transfer learning in sensor-based HAR, highlighting deep learning dominance and identifying key research gaps

## Executive Summary
This survey comprehensively analyzes 205 papers on transfer learning applications in sensor-based human activity recognition (HAR), focusing on smart home and wearable domains. The research addresses critical challenges including data scarcity, noise, and sensor heterogeneity that limit HAR deployment in real-world scenarios. The survey systematically categorizes transfer learning approaches into instance, feature, and parameter transfer methods, with neural network-based solutions emerging as the state-of-the-art. Deep learning architectures, adversarial training, and self-supervised learning represent dominant themes, while the review identifies significant gaps particularly in smart home transfer learning research.

## Method Summary
The survey methodology involved defining scope based on HAR transfer learning challenges, extracting relevant keywords, and using Google Scholar and Litmaps to find papers. Researchers manually checked for relevance and iteratively expanded searches until exhaustive coverage was achieved. The 205-paper collection was analyzed and categorized based on the challenges they address and the methods they employ. The review process focused on smart home and wearable domains, extracting insights about transfer learning approaches, performance metrics, and identified research gaps across the literature.

## Key Results
- Transfer learning bridges data scarcity gaps in HAR by leveraging annotated data from source domains to improve target domain performance
- Deep learning architectures, adversarial training, and self-supervised learning have emerged as dominant transfer learning themes in HAR
- Smart home research significantly lags behind wearables in exploring advanced transfer learning methods like neural networks
- Performance measurement under transfer settings remains underexplored, lacking standardized evaluation frameworks
- Future directions include federated learning and cross-modality transfer to address privacy and sensor heterogeneity challenges

## Foundational Learning
- Transfer Learning Theory: Understanding how knowledge transfers between domains is fundamental to HAR applications where sensor configurations and user behaviors vary
  - Why needed: HAR domains often have limited labeled data, making direct transfer from related domains essential
  - Quick check: Can the source and target domains be characterized by similar activity distributions or sensor characteristics?

- Domain Adaptation: The process of aligning source and target domains to minimize distribution differences
  - Why needed: Sensor heterogeneity and environmental variations create domain shifts that degrade model performance
  - Quick check: Are domain discrepancy measures like MMD or adversarial domain confusion applicable to the data?

- Feature Engineering for Sensors: Extracting meaningful representations from raw sensor data that capture activity patterns
  - Why needed: Raw sensor data is often high-dimensional and noisy, requiring careful feature extraction for effective transfer
  - Quick check: Do the extracted features preserve activity-relevant information while reducing dimensionality?

### Design Tradeoffs
- Instance-based vs. feature-based vs. parameter-based transfer: Different approaches suit different domain shift scenarios
- Deep learning vs. conventional methods: Tradeoff between model complexity and data requirements
- Centralized vs. federated learning: Privacy considerations vs. model performance

### Failure Signatures
- Negative transfer when source and target domains are too dissimilar
- Overfitting when transfer learning is applied to insufficient target domain data
- Domain shift not properly addressed leading to poor generalization

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: What are the most effective neural network architectures, loss functions, and training strategies for transfer learning in smart home HAR?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The survey highlights that deep learning-based solutions are state-of-the-art, but smart home research lags behind wearables in exploring these methods
- Why unresolved: While wearables research has explored various neural network architectures (e.g., AE, CNN, GRU/LSTM) and loss functions (e.g., MMD, domain discrimination), smart home research primarily relies on conventional approaches
- What evidence would resolve it: Comparative studies applying deep learning methods to smart home HAR datasets and demonstrating improved performance over conventional methods

### Open Question 2
- Question: How can federated/distributed learning be effectively implemented for privacy-preserving HAR in wearable devices?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The survey identifies data privacy and security as critical challenges, with only a few works addressing federated learning in wearables
- Why unresolved: Implementing federated learning for HAR faces challenges like resource constraints on devices, personalization requirements, and ensuring privacy without compromising model performance
- What evidence would resolve it: Real-world deployments of federated HAR systems demonstrating effective privacy preservation, model accuracy, and scalability

### Open Question 3
- Question: What are the optimal strategies for evaluating and comparing transfer learning methods in HAR?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The survey emphasizes the lack of consistent evaluation frameworks for transfer learning in HAR, hindering comparison and progress
- Why unresolved: Existing evaluation methods like k-fold cross-validation are inadequate for transfer learning settings where train and test conditions differ significantly
- What evidence would resolve it: Development and validation of standardized evaluation protocols that account for domain shifts and enable fair comparison of transfer learning methods

## Limitations
- The survey's scope is limited to sensor-based HAR in smart homes and wearables, excluding other potential applications like ambient HAR or camera-based systems
- The 205-paper analysis may have selection bias despite systematic searching, and the survey does not evaluate the actual effectiveness of transfer learning approaches across different settings
- The paper does not address potential negative transfer scenarios or provide quantitative comparisons of different transfer learning methods' performance

## Confidence
- High Confidence: The existence of data scarcity challenges in HAR and the categorization of transfer learning approaches (instance, feature, parameter transfer) are well-supported by the literature review and align with established transfer learning theory
- Medium Confidence: The identification of deep learning architectures, adversarial training, and self-supervised learning as dominant themes is supported by recent trends but may reflect publication bias toward newer methods
- Medium Confidence: The identified gaps in smart home transfer learning research are based on systematic analysis, though the survey does not quantify the severity of these gaps across different application domains
