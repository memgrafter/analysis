# Anomaly Detection in Graph Structured Data: A Survey

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2405.06172
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.06172
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Comprehensive survey categorizing graph anomaly detection methods by component type, graph type, technique, and application domain

## Executive Summary
This paper provides a comprehensive survey of anomaly detection techniques on graph-structured data, categorizing methods based on anomalous components, graph types, techniques, and application domains. The survey covers both static and dynamic graphs, including streaming and streaming edge approaches. It discusses probabilistic/statistical, matrix/tensor decomposition, distance/similarity-based, and graph neural network techniques for detecting node, edge, and (sub)graph anomalies.

## Method Summary
The paper conducts a systematic survey of anomaly detection in graph-structured data, organizing methods by the type of anomalous component (node, edge, subgraph), graph type (static, dynamic, streaming), and detection technique (probabilistic/statistical, matrix/tensor decomposition, distance/similarity-based, GNNs). The survey synthesizes existing research without implementing specific algorithms, focusing instead on classification frameworks and identifying research gaps.

## Key Results
- Comprehensive categorization of graph anomaly detection methods covering node, edge, and subgraph anomalies
- Coverage of both static and dynamic graph approaches including streaming edge methods
- Identification of key challenges including dynamic graphs, graph complexity, interpretability, and adversarial attacks

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Graph-based anomaly detection leverages inter-node dependencies and structural patterns to distinguish anomalies from normal behavior.
- Mechanism: By modeling relationships (edges) and connectivity patterns between nodes, the system can detect deviations that would be invisible in attribute-only analysis.
- Core assumption: The underlying graph structure contains discriminative patterns that separate normal and anomalous behavior.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "In short, abnormal instances are known as anomalies, and detecting such instances is called anomaly detection."
  - [section] "To detect anomalies in networks, we need to account for inter-dependencies (relations and connectivity patterns) in the graph representation."
  - [corpus] Corpus contains 25 related papers, but average citations per neighbor is 0.0, suggesting limited empirical validation yet.
- Break condition: If the graph structure is too sparse or lacks meaningful connectivity patterns, the method fails.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Different types of anomalies (node, edge, subgraph) require different detection techniques and temporal considerations.
- Mechanism: Static graphs allow snapshot-based anomaly detection, while dynamic graphs require tracking temporal evolution of nodes, edges, and subgraphs.
- Core assumption: Temporal evolution patterns are meaningful for anomaly detection in dynamic graphs.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section] "However, in dynamic graphs, one would consider the temporal aspect to detect a type of anomaly that is not present in the static graph."
  - [section] "A graph stream G is the set of infinite sequence of graphs, G = {Gt}∞ t=1, where Gt = ( Vt, Et) represents a static graph that contains the set of nodes Vt and the set of edges Et ⊆ (Vt × Vt) at timestamp t."
  - [corpus] Weak corpus evidence - average neighbor FMR is 0.461, suggesting moderate relevance but limited supporting citations.
- Break condition: If temporal patterns are too noisy or non-existent, temporal analysis becomes ineffective.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Graph neural networks (GNNs) can capture complex, nonlinear relationships in graph-structured data for anomaly detection.
- Mechanism: GNNs aggregate and transform node features through multiple layers of convolutions or attention mechanisms, learning to distinguish normal from anomalous patterns.
- Core assumption: Complex relationships in graph data can be effectively captured through deep learning architectures.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section] "DOMINANT [86] states that traditional anomaly detection techniques face challenges: Network sparsity, Data nonlinearity, Complex interactions."
  - [section] "DOMINANT presents a GCN-based autoencoder (GAE) to detect anomalies on attributed networks."
  - [corpus] Corpus shows related work exists but with no citations, suggesting emerging but not yet validated research.
- Break condition: If the graph is too large or sparse, GNNs may fail to learn meaningful representations.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How can graph-based anomaly detection methods be made more interpretable and explainable, especially for deep learning approaches like GNNs?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper discusses interpretability and explainability as a key challenge in section VIII.D, noting that many graph-based methods like GNNs are black-box models that make anomaly predictions through complex processes.
- Why unresolved: While the paper identifies the problem, it does not propose specific solutions for making these models more interpretable or explainable.
- What evidence would resolve it: Research demonstrating concrete methods to visualize learned embeddings, explain feature importance, or incorporate human validation into graph-based anomaly detection models.

### Open Question 2
- Question: How can graph-based anomaly detection methods be made more robust against adversarial attacks that manipulate graph structures?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper identifies adversarial attacks as a key challenge in section VIII.E, explaining that attackers can deliberately distort graph structures to evade detection.
- Why unresolved: While the paper recognizes the problem, it does not provide specific techniques for detecting or preventing adversarial attacks on graph-based anomaly detection systems.
- What evidence would resolve it: Research demonstrating methods to distinguish between real anomalies and adversarial ones, or techniques to evaluate and improve the robustness of graph-based anomaly detection models against adversarial perturbations.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How can graph-based anomaly detection methods be extended to handle gradual anomalies that evolve slowly over time?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper mentions gradual anomalies as a type of anomaly in section VI, noting that most existing research focuses on sudden and group anomalies, with very little research on gradual anomalies.
- Why unresolved: The paper acknowledges the existence of gradual anomalies but does not provide methods for detecting them or explain why they are challenging to identify.
- What evidence would resolve it: Research demonstrating effective techniques for detecting gradual anomalies in dynamic graphs, along with experimental results showing their effectiveness compared to existing methods.

## Limitations
