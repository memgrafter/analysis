# A biologically inspired computational trust model for open multi-agent systems which is resilient to trustor population changes

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2404.10014
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.10014
- Authors: Zoi Lygizou; Dimitris Kalles
- Reference count: 12
- Primary result: CA model resilient to trustor population changes, FIRE better handles trustee population changes and profile switches

## Executive Summary
This paper compares a biologically inspired trust model (CA) based on synaptic plasticity with FIRE under dynamic conditions in open multi-agent systems. CA allows trustees to decide task execution capability using local connection weights, while FIRE relies on trustors selecting trustees using a multi-module approach. Simulations tested both models under continuous population changes and dynamic trustee profiles, revealing that each model excels under different environmental conditions.

## Method Summary
The study uses agent-based simulations with 100 providers (10 good, 40 ordinary, 5 intermittent, 45 bad) and 500 consumers in a spherical world. Agents were replaced up to 10% per round and providers changed profiles with probability 0.02 over 500 rounds. Both models were compared using average utility gain per consumer interaction, analyzed with two-sample t-tests at 95% confidence. CA implements synaptic plasticity through connection weight updates, while FIRE integrates four trust modules: interaction trust, witness reputation, role-based trust, and certified reputation.

## Key Results
- CA demonstrates resilience to trustor population changes due to local connection weights independent of witness reputation
- FIRE outperforms CA under trustee population changes and profile switches due to its interaction trust module
- Neither model dominates across all dynamic conditions, suggesting context-dependent model selection is optimal

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: CA's resilience to trustor population changes stems from not relying on witness reputation.
- Mechanism: CA stores trustee capability knowledge locally as connection weights, independent of trustor actions. When trustors enter/exit, CA's internal model remains unchanged.
- Core assumption: Local connection weights accurately reflect trustee capability regardless of trustor turnover.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "Importantly, in CA approach, agents do not exchange information which naturally creates the expectation that CA model is immune to various kinds of disinformation"
  - [section] "CA does not rely on the opinions of other consumer-witnesses, and this is the reason for its resilience in changes in the population of consumers"
- Break condition: If connection weights become stale or misaligned with actual trustee capabilities due to lack of feedback.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: FIRE's superior performance under trustee population changes results from its interaction trust module.
- Mechanism: FIRE leverages evaluator's direct interaction history with trustees, allowing rapid adaptation to new trustees entering the system.
- Core assumption: Direct interaction history provides reliable trust assessment even with trustee turnover.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section] "Interaction trust, in which a provider's trustworthiness is mainly determined based on the evaluator's previous interactions with the target agent, is the most reliable form of trust"
  - [section] "FIRE is more resilient to changes in provider population than CA, which does not respond well to this type of environmental change"
- Break condition: If direct interactions are too sparse or infrequent to build reliable trust assessments.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: FIRE outperforms CA during trustee profile switches due to faster adaptation mechanisms.
- Mechanism: FIRE's multi-module architecture (especially interaction trust and witness reputation) allows quicker recalibration when trustee behavior changes.
- Core assumption: FIRE's modules can rapidly detect and respond to trustee performance shifts.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "When the trustees switch performance profiles FIRE clearly outperforms despite the fact that both models' performances are significantly impacted by this environmental change"
  - [section] "FIRE's performance begins higher (UG = 2.95) and gradually improves to surpass CA's performance after interaction 285"
- Break condition: If trustee behavior changes are too rapid for FIRE's modules to track effectively.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
  - CA: Local connection weight storage, task request broadcasting, weight update algorithms, threshold-based task acceptance
  - FIRE: Interaction trust module, witness reputation module, role-based trust module, certified reputation module, provider selection algorithm

- Critical path: Provider selection → Task execution → Performance feedback → Trust value update → Next provider selection
  - For CA: Trustee initiates task acceptance based on local weights
  - For FIRE: Trustor selects provider using multi-module assessment

- Design tradeoffs:
  - CA: Resilience to trustor turnover vs. vulnerability to trustee turnover
  - FIRE: Adaptability to trustee turnover vs. dependence on witness availability
  - Both: Balance between exploration (trying new providers) and exploitation (using known good providers)

- Failure signatures:
  - CA: Declining utility when trustee capabilities change but weights don't update quickly enough
  - FIRE: Performance degradation when witness population shrinks or trustee behavior becomes erratic
  - Both: Initial learning phase where utility is below optimal

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Static environment comparison: Run both models with fixed populations to establish baseline performance
  2. Trustee population change: Gradually introduce trustee turnover to test adaptation speed
  3. Trustor population change: Gradually introduce trustor turnover to test resilience mechanisms

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How can the CA algorithm be modified to handle switches of performance profiles more effectively?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper states that "Reasoning about openness and particularly agents' evolution...not only requires considering possible changes to one's capabilities, but also reasoning about changes to one's own type. A direction for future work is to investigate how CA algorithm could be modified to handle switches of performance profiles more effectively."
- Why unresolved: The paper identifies this as a limitation of the current CA model, which performs poorly when providers switch performance profiles, but does not propose specific modifications to address this issue.
- What evidence would resolve it: Experimental results showing improved performance of a modified CA algorithm when providers switch performance profiles, demonstrating that the modifications successfully address the identified limitation.

### Open Question 2
- Question: How can consumers detect the dynamic factors present in their environment and decide which trust model to employ in order to maximize utility gain?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper concludes that "Neither CA nor FIRE outperforms with any combination of varying degrees of dynamic factors in effect. Thus, a natural rich area of future research is to investigate how the consumers can detect the dynamic factors present in their environment and then decide which trust model to employ in order to maximize utility gain."
- Why unresolved: The paper identifies this as an area for future research, recognizing that different trust models perform better under different dynamic conditions, but does not propose a specific method for consumers to detect these conditions and choose the appropriate model.
- What evidence would resolve it: Development and experimental validation of a mechanism that allows consumers to accurately detect dynamic environmental factors and select the most appropriate trust model based on these factors, resulting in maximized utility gain across various dynamic conditions.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How does the performance of CA and FIRE models scale with larger and more complex multi-agent systems?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper's experiments were conducted in a relatively small-scale testbed with 100 providers and 500 consumers. The scalability of the models to larger and more complex systems is not addressed.
- Why unresolved: The paper does not provide any information on how the models would perform in larger or more complex multi-agent systems, which is a critical consideration for real-world applications.
- What evidence would resolve it: Experimental results demonstrating the performance of CA and FIRE models in larger and more complex multi-agent systems, showing how their utility gain and resilience to dynamic factors scale with system size and complexity.

## Limitations

- Agent-based simulations may not fully capture real-world trust dynamics influenced by social, emotional, or contextual factors
- Performance profiles are predetermined rather than emergent, potentially limiting ecological validity
- Testbed assumes rational agents focused solely on utility maximization, excluding other trust-influencing factors

## Confidence

- **High confidence**: CA's resilience to trustor population changes due to local connection weights
- **Medium confidence**: FIRE's superior performance under trustee population changes
- **Medium confidence**: FIRE's advantage during trustee profile switches
