# Identifying Privacy Personas

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2410.14023
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.14023
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Novel method combining qualitative and quantitative analysis to identify eight statistically distinct privacy personas

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a novel methodology for identifying privacy personas by combining qualitative coding with quantitative clustering of questionnaire responses. The approach uses open coding to extract traits from open-ended questions, which are then merged with closed-ended question data and analyzed using divisive hierarchical clustering with a custom dissimilarity measure. The method validates clusters using Boschloo's statistical test to ensure personas are meaningfully different from each other.

The research produces eight statistically distinct privacy personas: Knowledgeable Optimist, In-control Adopter, In-control Sceptic, Knowledgeable Pessimist, Helpless Protector, Occasional Protector, Adopting Protector, and Unconcerned. These personas demonstrate greater granularity and comprehensiveness compared to existing frameworks like Westin and Biselli et al., providing better support for personalized privacy assistance design.

## Method Summary
The methodology combines qualitative and quantitative analysis of questionnaire responses to identify privacy personas. Open coding through Grounded Theory extracts codes from open-ended responses, which are grouped into traits. These traits are merged with closed-ended question data to form feature vectors. Divisive hierarchical clustering with a custom dissimilarity measure groups participants based on these vectors. Boschloo's statistical test validates that clusters differ significantly on trait frequencies, ensuring personas are not arbitrary groupings.

## Key Results
- Eight statistically distinct privacy personas identified: Knowledgeable Optimist, In-control Adopter, In-control Sceptic, Knowledgeable Pessimist, Helpless Protector, Occasional Protector, Adopting Protector, and Unconcerned
- Personas demonstrate greater granularity and comprehensiveness than existing frameworks (Westin, Biselli, Dupree, Schomakers)
- Method successfully captures both knowledge and behavioral aspects of privacy attitudes
- Statistical validation confirms personas differ significantly on multiple attributes (knowledge, behavior, self-efficacy, privacy protection importance)

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- **Claim:** Combining qualitative coding with quantitative clustering produces more granular privacy personas than purely quantitative or qualitative approaches.
- **Mechanism:** Open coding extracts fine-grained traits from open-ended responses, which are then merged with closed-ended question data. Divisive hierarchical clustering with a custom dissimilarity measure groups participants based on these rich trait vectors, ensuring clusters differ statistically (Boschloo's test).
- **Core assumption:** Traits derived from open-ended questions are mutually exclusive or can be grouped into Likert-scale categories, allowing valid distance calculations between participants.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [abstract] "We propose eight personas that we derive by combining qualitative and quantitative analysis of the responses to an interactive educational questionnaire."
  - [section] "We use open coding through Grounded Theory [16] to extract codes, which are sentences or phrases that propagate one idea [4]. We then group codes into traits..."
- **Break condition:** If trait extraction is inconsistent or mutual exclusivity assumption fails, distance calculations become unreliable and clusters may not reflect true persona differences.

### Mechanism 2
- **Claim:** The custom dissimilarity measure accurately captures differences between participants while accounting for the nature of question types.
- **Mechanism:** For Likert-scale explanatory variables, L1 distance normalized by the maximum possible range is used. For binary variables from open-ended traits, dot product is used. The measure combines these parts to avoid penalizing absence of expressed opinions.
- **Core assumption:** Zero values for binary traits indicate absence of expression, not disagreement, and thus should not contribute negatively to distance.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [abstract] "Additionally, we propose a new measure for calculating distances between questionnaire responses, that accounts for the type of the question (closed- vs open-ended) used to derive traits."
  - [section] "We merge the approaches used by Dupree et al. [11] and Biselli et al. [2] by introducing a new dissimilarity measure..."
- **Break condition:** If the distinction between absence of expression and disagreement is invalid for certain traits, the dissimilarity measure may incorrectly treat similar participants as different.

### Mechanism 3
- **Claim:** Statistical validation of cluster splits ensures personas are meaningfully different from each other.
- **Mechanism:** Boschloo's test of homogeneity of proportions is applied to each trait when comparing clusters. Only splits where at least one trait shows significant difference (p < 0.05) are accepted. A two-step pruning process removes statistically similar sub-clusters.
- **Core assumption:** Boschloo's test is appropriate for small sample sizes and binary/proportion data derived from trait frequencies.
- **Evidence anchors:**
  - [abstract] "We design an analysis pipeline that uses divisive hierarchical clustering and Boschloo's statistical test of homogeneity of proportions to ensure that the elicited clusters differ from each other based on a statistical measure."
  - [section] "We consider clusters Uð‘£ð‘˜ and Uð‘›ð‘š to be significantly different if there exists at least one trait for which the difference in proportions is p < 0.05."
- **Break condition:** If the sample size per cluster is too small, statistical tests lose power and may fail to detect real differences or falsely accept splits.

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map:**
  Data collection -> Code extraction -> Trait formation -> Annotation -> Feature vector construction -> Dissimilarity measure -> Clustering -> Validation -> Persona description

- **Design tradeoffs:**
  - Manual coding and annotation vs automated methods: Manual ensures nuanced understanding but is labor-intensive and may have inter-annotator variability.
  - Custom dissimilarity measure vs standard metrics: Custom measure accounts for question type but requires careful validation.
  - Statistical validation vs purely algorithmic clustering: Ensures meaningful personas but may reject valid splits if sample size is small.

- **Failure signatures:**
  - Inconsistent coding leading to noisy trait vectors
  - Statistical tests failing to find differences even when clusters appear visually distinct
  - Dendrogram pruning removing too many clusters, resulting in overly coarse personas
  - Dissimilarity measure producing negative or inflated distances due to incorrect handling of trait types

- **First 3 experiments:**
  1. Validate trait extraction consistency by having two coders code the same subset of responses and compute inter-coder agreement.
  2. Test the custom dissimilarity measure on a synthetic dataset with known differences to ensure it correctly identifies similar vs dissimilar participants.
  3. Apply Boschloo's test on a small sample with known group differences to confirm it can detect significant splits with limited data.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How would privacy personas identified in this study differ across different age groups or cultural backgrounds?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper acknowledges that the dataset used was from participants aged 25-35 in the UK, and that cultural differences and age could influence privacy perception.
- Why unresolved: The study only validated the personas on a subset of the same dataset, not on a new dataset with different demographics.
- What evidence would resolve it: Collecting and analyzing data from participants of various ages and cultural backgrounds using the same methodology would provide evidence of how personas differ across these groups.

### Open Question 2
- Question: How do the privacy personas identified in this study relate to actual privacy behaviors, as opposed to self-reported behaviors?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper mentions that the questionnaire measured self-reported behaviors and future decisions, which might not correspond to actual behavior.
- Why unresolved: The study relied on self-reported data and did not record user interactions with applications or observe actual privacy behaviors.
- What evidence would resolve it: Conducting a study that tracks user interactions with privacy settings and applications, and comparing this data with the identified personas, would provide evidence of the relationship between personas and actual behaviors.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How can the identified privacy personas be used to design personalized privacy settings and interventions?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper suggests that the personas can be used to support the design of online privacy settings and to facilitate persona-tailored privacy support.
- Why unresolved: The paper does not provide specific examples or guidelines on how to use the personas to design personalized privacy settings or interventions.
- What evidence would resolve it: Developing and testing personalized privacy settings and interventions based on the identified personas, and evaluating their effectiveness in improving privacy outcomes, would provide evidence of how the personas can be used in practice.

## Limitations
- Manual annotation burden introduces potential inter-coder variability and scalability limitations
- Sample representativeness may not capture full diversity of privacy attitudes across demographics
- Statistical power constraints with small cluster sizes during divisive clustering may limit test effectiveness

## Confidence
- **High confidence**: Methodology for combining qualitative and quantitative analysis is sound and well-explained
- **Medium confidence**: Custom dissimilarity measure appropriately accounts for different question types
- **Low confidence**: Generalizability to populations beyond specific questionnaire sample is uncertain

## Next Checks
1. **Cross-validation study**: Apply the same methodology to an independent dataset of privacy questionnaire responses to assess whether the eight personas replicate or if different patterns emerge.
2. **Inter-annotator reliability assessment**: Calculate Cohen's kappa or similar agreement metrics across annotators for a subset of responses to quantify coding consistency and identify problematic traits.
3. **Dissimilarity measure benchmarking**: Compare the proposed custom dissimilarity measure against standard clustering metrics (Euclidean, Manhattan, cosine similarity) on synthetic datasets with known ground truth to evaluate its discriminative power.
