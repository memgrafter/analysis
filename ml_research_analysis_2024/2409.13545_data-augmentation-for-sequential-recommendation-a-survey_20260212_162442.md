# Data Augmentation for Sequential Recommendation: A Survey

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2409.13545
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.13545
- Reference count: 7
- Primary result: Systematic review of data augmentation methods for sequential recommendation addressing data sparsity through heuristic-based and model-based approaches

## Executive Summary
This survey comprehensively reviews data augmentation methods for sequential recommendation (SR), addressing the critical data sparsity problem that limits model performance. The authors categorize existing DA methods into heuristic-based and model-based approaches, further distinguishing them by augmentation objects (data-level vs. representation-level) and purposes. The paper provides comparative analysis of these approaches and presents experimental results demonstrating that model-based methods generally outperform heuristic-based ones, while also identifying key future research directions in the field.

## Method Summary
The survey systematically organizes data augmentation methods for sequential recommendation by categorizing them into heuristic-based and model-based approaches. Heuristic-based methods employ simple operations like sliding windows, masking, and substitution, while model-based methods use learnable modules for sequence extension, denoising, and generation through techniques like diffusion models and large language models. The authors conducted experiments on three datasets (Beauty, Sports, Yelp) using SASRec as the backbone model, comparing various DA methods including FEARec, RepPad, and others. The analysis highlights the advantages and limitations of each approach while identifying research gaps and future directions for improving DA in sequential recommendation.

## Key Results
- Model-based DA methods generally outperform heuristic-based methods, with FEARec achieving 0.0432 H@10 on Beauty and 0.0803 N@10 on Yelp
- Some heuristic methods like RepPad show competitive performance despite being simpler approaches
- The survey identifies key future directions including developing theoretical foundations for DA and improving evaluation metrics for augmented data quality

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
Data augmentation addresses the fundamental data sparsity problem in sequential recommendation by artificially expanding limited user interaction sequences. By generating additional training samples through various augmentation strategies, SR models can learn more robust representations and improve their generalization ability. The effectiveness stems from exposing models to diverse variations of existing data, helping them capture underlying patterns and relationships that might not be apparent from sparse original data alone.

## Architecture Onboarding

Component map: Data -> Augmentation Module -> Enhanced Data -> SR Model -> Recommendations

Critical path: User interaction data flows through augmentation module, which generates enhanced sequences that improve SR model training and ultimately produce better recommendations.

Design tradeoffs:
- Complexity vs. performance: Model-based methods offer better results but require more computational resources
- Relevance vs. diversity: Augmentation must balance realistic variations with novel patterns
- Generality vs. specificity: Methods need to work across different recommendation contexts while maintaining effectiveness

Failure signatures:
- Over-augmentation leading to unrealistic sequences that confuse the model
- Under-augmentation providing insufficient additional information
- Augmentation bias introducing systematic distortions in recommendation patterns

First experiments:
1. Implement sliding window augmentation on a small dataset and compare performance with original data
2. Test masking augmentation with different masking rates to find optimal configuration
3. Compare heuristic augmentation (masking) versus simple model-based augmentation on a single dataset

## Limitations
- Categorization framework may not capture all nuances of rapidly evolving DA techniques
- Experimental results limited to three datasets and one backbone model (SASRec), constraining generalizability
- Claims about model-based methods outperforming heuristic-based ones come from limited experimental setup
- Survey lacks quantitative comparisons across all methods, making definitive ranking difficult
