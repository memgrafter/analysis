# Syn2Real Domain Generalization for Underwater Mine-like Object Detection Using Side-Scan Sonar

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2410.12953
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.12953
- Authors: Aayush Agrawal; Aniruddh Sikdar; Rajini Makam; Suresh Sundaram; Suresh Kumar Besai; Mahesh Gopi
- Reference count: 26
- One-line primary result: DDIM-generated synthetic SSS images with residual noise improved Mask R-CNN AP by ~60% compared to training on real data alone

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of underwater mine detection using deep learning, which is hindered by the scarcity of real-world data. The proposed Syn2Real (Synthetic to Real) domain generalization approach uses diffusion models to generate synthetic data that can effectively augment real-world samples for training. The method leverages the residual noise in the final sampled images to improve the model's ability to generalize to real-world data with inherent noise and high variation. The baseline Mask-RCNN model, when trained on a combination of synthetic and original training datasets, exhibited approximately a 60% increase in Average Precision (AP) compared to being trained solely on the original training data. The results demonstrate the potential of Syn2Real domain generalization for underwater mine detection tasks, with the DDIM sampling method producing the most effective synthetic SSS images for domain generalization.

## Method Summary
The method uses diffusion models (DDPM and DDIM) to generate synthetic side-scan sonar images containing mine-like objects, then trains a Mask R-CNN object detection model on combinations of synthetic and real datasets. The key innovation is leveraging the residual noise in DDIM-generated images as a regularizer that helps the model generalize from synthetic to real domains. The approach was evaluated on a dataset of 461 side-scan sonar images containing cylindrical and conical mines, with performance measured using AP and AUPC metrics.

## Key Results
- Mask R-CNN trained on combined synthetic+real datasets showed ~60% AP improvement over real data alone
- DDIM-generated synthetic images outperformed DDPM-generated images for domain generalization
- Optimal performance achieved with combination of DDPM, DDIM, and original datasets
- Synthetic data generation successfully addressed the data scarcity challenge in underwater mine detection

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Synthetic data generated with residual noise improves domain generalization by exposing the model to more realistic variations.
- Mechanism: The noise inherent in DDIM-generated images mimics the variability found in real-world SSS data, preventing overfitting to clean synthetic samples and improving the model's ability to handle real-world noise.
- Core assumption: The noise patterns in synthetic data are sufficiently similar to real-world noise distributions to act as a regularizer.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "The residual noise in the final sampled images improves the model's ability to generalize to real-world data with inherent noise and high variation."
  - [section] "When compared to DDPM-generated images, the DDIM-generated images exhibit higher levels of noise... This noise addition blocks overfitting caused by regularization."
- Break condition: If real-world noise distribution differs significantly from synthetic noise patterns, the regularizing effect diminishes.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Combining synthetic and real datasets yields better performance than either alone.
- Mechanism: Synthetic data augments the training set size and diversity, while real data provides ground truth quality and domain-specific characteristics. The combination balances generalization with accurate representation.
- Core assumption: The synthetic data captures enough of the real data's statistical properties to be useful while adding beneficial diversity.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "The baseline Mask-RCNN model when trained on a combination of synthetic and original training datasets, exhibited approximately a 60% increase in Average Precision (AP) compared to being trained solely on the original training data."
  - [section] "We see an almost 2x increase in performance when trained on just synthetic images and when trained on a combination of Original + Synthetic, we see an increase of roughly 14%."
- Break condition: If synthetic data quality degrades significantly or fails to capture essential domain features, the combination may not improve performance.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: DDIM sampling produces more effective synthetic SSS images for domain generalization than DDPM.
- Mechanism: DDIM's faster sampling introduces more noise variation in final images, creating synthetic samples that better represent the noise characteristics of real SSS data, leading to improved generalization.
- Core assumption: The noise introduced by DDIM sampling is beneficial for generalization rather than just being random corruption.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "DDIM sampling method producing the most effective synthetic SSS images for domain generalization."
  - [section] "DDIM-generated image datasets outperform DDPM for domain generalization from synthetic to real... The DDIM model's infusion of noise into the sampled images is partially responsible for the observed variability in the results."
- Break condition: If the noise becomes excessive and obscures essential features, performance may degrade despite the regularization effect.

- Critical path: Diffusion model -> Synthetic dataset -> Mask R-CNN training -> AP calculation
- Design tradeoffs: DDPM offers higher image quality but slower sampling; DDIM is faster but introduces more noise. The tradeoff is between sample quality and the beneficial noise for generalization.
- Failure signatures: Poor FID/KID scores indicate synthetic data quality issues; low AP scores suggest the model fails to generalize from synthetic to real data.
- First 3 experiments:

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How does the noise introduced by DDIM diffusion models affect the generalization performance of semantic segmentation models in underwater mine detection compared to other noise-injection methods?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper states that DDIM-generated images exhibit higher levels of noise compared to DDPM-generated images, and this noise is partially responsible for the observed variability in the results, with DDIM datasets outperforming DDPM datasets for domain generalization.
- Why unresolved: The paper does not provide a detailed analysis of how the specific type and level of noise introduced by DDIM affects the model's ability to generalize. It also does not compare DDIM noise to other noise-injection methods.
- What evidence would resolve it: A comparative study of DDIM noise against other noise-injection techniques (e.g., Gaussian noise, dropout) on semantic segmentation performance for underwater mine detection, measuring metrics like AP and AUPC.

### Open Question 2
- Question: Can the synthetic data generation approach be extended to other underwater objects beyond mine-like objects, and how would the performance vary across different object types?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper focuses on generating synthetic data for mine-like objects (MLOs) using diffusion models. While the approach shows promise for MLOs, it is unclear if the same methodology would be effective for other underwater objects with different shapes, textures, or detection challenges.
- Why unresolved: The study is limited to MLOs, and there is no exploration of the method's applicability to other underwater object categories. Different objects may require different synthetic data generation strategies or model architectures.
- What evidence would resolve it: Applying the Syn2Real approach to datasets of other underwater objects (e.g., fish, wrecks, plants) and comparing the performance of synthetic data generation and domain generalization across these object types.

### Open Question 3
- Question: What is the optimal balance between synthetic and real data for training semantic segmentation models in underwater mine detection, and how does this balance impact model performance?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper experiments with different combinations of synthetic and real data (e.g., DDPM+DDIM+Original) and finds that combining all three datasets yields the most favorable outcomes. However, it does not explore the optimal ratio of synthetic to real data.
- Why unresolved: While the paper demonstrates that combining synthetic and real data improves performance, it does not investigate the optimal ratio or provide insights into how different ratios affect model accuracy and generalization.
- What evidence would resolve it: Conducting experiments with varying ratios of synthetic to real data (e.g., 25%:75%, 50%:50%, 75%:25%) and analyzing the impact on segmentation metrics like AP and AUPC to determine the optimal balance for underwater mine detection.

## Limitations

- The exact impact of DDIM's noise characteristics versus DDPM's quality on final performance is not fully quantified; visual inspection alone cannot determine which aspect drives generalization
- The paper lacks comparison with other domain generalization techniques (e.g., adversarial training, meta-learning) that might yield different performance profiles
- Real-world noise patterns in SSS data are not characterized in detail, making it difficult to assess whether synthetic noise is truly representative
