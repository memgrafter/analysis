# Generative VS non-Generative Models in Engineering Shape Optimization

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2402.08540
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.08540
- Authors: Muhammad Usama; Zahid Masood; Shahroz Khan; Konstantinos Kostas; Panagiotis Kaklis
- Reference count: 40
- Key outcome: SSV-KLE approach achieves 0.01% invalid designs vs 1.01% for PaDGAN and 7.91% for conventional GAN

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a systematic comparison of generative and non-generative models for constructing design spaces in airfoil/hydrofoil shape optimization. The authors compare a conventional GAN, a performance-augmented GAN (PaDGAN), and a non-generative model based on Karhunen-Loève expansion with physics-informed shape signature vectors (SSV-KLE). The comparison is performed on two large datasets containing real-world and artificial airfoil designs. The SSV-KLE approach achieves the best results in terms of design validity, generating a highly robust latent space with only 0.01% invalid designs for one dataset and 0.46% for the other, compared to 1.01% for PaDGAN and 7.91% for the conventional GAN.

## Method Summary
The study compares three approaches for engineering shape optimization: a conventional GAN, a performance-augmented GAN (PaDGAN) incorporating determinantal point processes, and a non-generative SSV-KLE approach. The non-generative model encodes geometric and physics-informed features into shape signature vectors, applies Karhunen-Loève expansion for dimensionality reduction, and sets latent space bounds based on eigenvalues. The generative models use deep neural networks with specialized loss functions to learn the data distribution. All approaches are tested with different discretization methods (uniform parametric, cosine, curvature-based, uniform point spacing) on two large airfoil datasets enriched with integral properties and physics-informed parameters.

## Key Results
- SSV-KLE approach achieves only 0.01% invalid designs vs 1.01% for PaDGAN and 7.91% for conventional GAN
- Non-generative model shows comparable performance in design diversity and aerodynamic efficiency to generative models
- Different discretization methods significantly impact latent space quality for all model types

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
SSV-KLE with geometric moment augmentation produces latent spaces with minimal invalid designs. The non-generative SSV-KLE approach encodes both geometric and physics-informed features (via geometric moments) into the shape signature vector, ensuring the resulting latent space excludes invalid shapes like self-intersections and undulations. By incorporating performance-informed quantities without direct simulation, the latent space remains valid while capturing key design characteristics.

### Mechanism 2
PaDGAN achieves higher design diversity through determinantal point processes. The performance-augmented DPP loss in PaDGAN explicitly penalizes similar designs while promoting high-performance ones, creating a latent space that covers the design space more broadly than conventional GANs. The kernel matrix LB captures both similarity and performance simultaneously.

### Mechanism 3
Discretization method significantly impacts latent space quality for both generative and non-generative models. Different point distribution schemes (uniform parametric, cosine, curvature-based, uniform point spacing) affect how well the shape is encoded into the SSV, which in turn determines latent space validity and performance. Curvature-based spacing concentrates points where design changes matter most.

## Foundational Learning

- **Karhunen-Loève Expansion (KLE)**: KLE provides the mathematical foundation for extracting principal components from the shape signature vectors, creating an orthonormal basis for the latent space that captures maximum variance. - **Geometric moment invariants**: Geometric moments provide physics-informed features that augment the shape signature vector without requiring computationally expensive performance simulations, improving latent space quality. ## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map**: Shape Signature Vector (SSV) construction → Karhunen-Loève Expansion → Latent space bounds → Design generation/validation
- **Critical path**: 
  1. Generate SSV with appropriate augmentation (geometry + physics-informed features)
  2. Apply KLE to extract principal components
  3. Set latent space bounds based on eigenvalues
  4. Generate designs and validate against geometric constraints
  5. Evaluate performance metrics (CL/CD ratio)
- **Design tradeoffs**: SSV-KLE is linear and computationally efficient but requires careful augmentation; PaDGAN captures nonlinearities but is computationally expensive and sensitive to hyperparameters; discretization choice affects all models differently
- **Failure signatures**: High percentage of invalid designs (>1%) indicates problems with SSV construction or latent space bounds; low diversity scores suggest insufficient augmentation; performance below dataset average indicates issues with feature selection
- **First 3 experiments**: 
  1. Generate latent spaces using different discretization methods (uniform parametric vs curvature-based) with SSV-KLE
  2. Compare PaDGAN with conventional GAN on the same dataset using identical discretization
  3. Test different SSV augmentations (moments vs direct performance metrics)

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
How do generative and non-generative models compare in terms of computational efficiency when applied to complex 3D functional surfaces like wings and turbine blades? The paper suggests this as a future research direction, indicating it remains unexplored.

### Open Question 2
What is the optimal balance between shape discretization resolution and model performance for different types of generative and non-generative models? The paper shows discretization significantly affects model performance but doesn't establish optimal trade-offs.

### Open Question 3
How do physics-informed augmentation methods compare to other approaches for improving latent space quality in engineering design applications? The paper uses geometric moments for augmentation but doesn't compare this approach to alternatives.

## Limitations
- Limited statistical validation across multiple datasets
- Reliance on specific airfoil/hydrofoil applications
- Results based on comparisons with only two datasets

## Confidence
- Medium: The primary claims are supported by the presented results but require validation across diverse problem domains and larger sample sizes

## Next Checks

1. **Cross-Dataset Validation**: Test the SSV-KLE approach across 10+ diverse engineering shape datasets (aerodynamic, structural, thermal) to assess generalizability of the validity improvements beyond airfoils/hydrofoils.

2. **Ablation Study on SSV Augmentation**: Systematically compare geometric moment augmentation against direct performance metric inclusion in the SSV to quantify which physics-informed features contribute most to latent space quality.

3. **Computational Efficiency Benchmarking**: Conduct runtime analysis comparing SSV-KLE with PaDGAN across identical hardware configurations, measuring both training time and design generation speed to validate the "computationally inexpensive" claim.
