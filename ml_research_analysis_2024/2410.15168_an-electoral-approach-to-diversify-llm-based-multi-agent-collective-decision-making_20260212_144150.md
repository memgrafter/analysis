# An Electoral Approach to Diversify LLM-based Multi-Agent Collective Decision-Making

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2410.15168
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.15168
- Authors: Xiutian Zhao; Ke Wang; Wei Peng
- Reference count: 31
- Key outcome: GEDI introduces ordinal preferential voting methods to LLM-based multi-agent systems, showing improved reasoning capabilities and robustness compared to simple voting approaches

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the limited diversity in collective decision-making (CDM) methods used in LLM-based multi-agent systems, which typically rely on simple dictatorial or plurality voting. The authors introduce GEDI, an electoral CDM module that incorporates various ordinal preferential voting mechanisms including Borda count, ranked pairs, and instant-runoff voting. Through extensive empirical evaluation across three MCQA benchmarks using diverse LLM backbones, the study demonstrates that certain voting methods can significantly improve reasoning capabilities and robustness compared to single-agent or simple voting approaches, with as few as three agents achieving positive synergies.

## Method Summary
The study evaluates various collective decision-making methods in LLM-based multi-agent systems on MCQA benchmarks using the GEDI module. The module implements 10 CDM methods (dictatorial, range voting, plurality, Borda count, Bucklin, IRV, minimax, ranked pairs) with ensemble voting agents built on 8 backbone models. Experiments run on MMLU (5,700 questions), MMLU-Pro (1,400 questions), and ARC-Challenge (1,172 questions) using 5-shot prompting, measuring overall accuracy, hit-rate@k, subject-wise accuracy improvements, and robustness against unreliable agents.

## Key Results
- Certain voting methods (Borda count, ranked pairs, IRV) significantly improve reasoning capabilities compared to simple plurality or dictatorial methods
- Most CDM methods show positive synergies even with as few as three agents, surpassing blind dictatorial baselines
- Voting methods demonstrate robustness against unreliable agents, maintaining integrity until approximately 4 unreliable agents are present
- Different CDM methods show varying hit-rate@k and subject-wise impacts across disciplines

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
Applying ordinal preferential voting methods (e.g., Borda Count, Ranked Pairs, IRV) improves LLM-agent decision accuracy compared to simple plurality or dictatorial methods. These methods aggregate ranked preferences across multiple agents, capturing richer preference information than first-choice only voting, which reduces noise and bias from individual agents. This works under the assumption that LLM agents can reliably rank all available choices, and these rankings are meaningful for collective decisions. Break condition: If agents cannot produce consistent rankings, or if choice sets are too large for reliable ranking.

### Mechanism 2
Ordinal voting methods provide robustness against unreliable agents compared to dictatorial methods. When some agents provide random or incorrect rankings, ordinal aggregation methods dilute the impact of these outliers, whereas dictatorial methods fail completely if the dictator is unreliable. This holds as long as the majority of agents remain reliable, but breaks when most agents become unreliable, causing all methods to degrade to random performance.

### Mechanism 3
Even small voting quorums (3-5 agents) can produce meaningful improvements over single-agent decisions. With ordinal voting, even a small group can aggregate complementary strengths and weaknesses of individual agents, producing better collective judgments than any single agent. This requires agents to have diverse capabilities, but provides little benefit if agents are too similar or correlated.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Backbone LLMs -> GEDI module -> Voting rules -> Profile processor -> Collective decision
- Critical path: 1. Generate individual agent rankings 2. Collect and validate ranking profiles 3. Apply selected voting method 4. Output collective preference ranking
- Design tradeoffs: Simple methods (plurality) are fast but less robust; Complex methods (ranked pairs) are more robust but computationally heavier; More agents improve robustness but increase cost
- Failure signatures: Low profile validity rates; Minimal accuracy improvement over single agent; High variance across different voting methods; Sensitivity to unreliable agents
- First 3 experiments: 1. Compare plurality voting vs. Borda Count on MMLU with 3 agents 2. Test robustness by gradually replacing reliable agents with random voters 3. Vary quorum size (1-10 agents) to find minimum effective number

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
What is the optimal number of agents needed for different LLM-based collective decision-making (CDM) methods to achieve maximum performance gains? The paper shows that most CDM methods start exhibiting significant improvements and surpass the blind dictatorial baseline with more than two agents, but the extent of improvement varies depending on the specific model and CDM method. A comprehensive study testing different CDM methods with varying numbers of agents and models would identify the optimal number of agents for each combination.

### Open Question 2
How do different CDM methods impact the hit-rate@k for LLM-based multi-agent systems? The paper shows that different CDM methods have varying levels of hit-rate@k, with some methods like plurality falling short in scenarios where the elimination of the worst choices is of higher priority than the selection of the best. A comprehensive study testing different CDM methods with varying hit-rate@k would identify the optimal method for each scenario.

### Open Question 3
How do different CDM methods impact the subject-wise performance of LLM-based multi-agent systems? The paper shows that under the same voting method, the performance gains are not evenly distributed across disciplines, and for the same discipline, the impacts under different CDM methods vary as well. A comprehensive study testing different CDM methods with varying subjects would identify the optimal method for each subject.

## Limitations

- The study relies on synthetic ranking data rather than observed human or agent preferences, which may not reflect real-world preference structures
- Computational overhead of complex voting methods like Ranked Pairs versus their accuracy gains is not quantified
- Robustness testing assumes random agent failures rather than systematic biases or coordinated adversarial behavior

## Confidence

- **High Confidence**: The demonstration that ordinal voting methods outperform simple plurality and dictatorial approaches in the tested MCQA scenarios, given the consistent empirical results across multiple benchmarks and agent configurations
- **Medium Confidence**: The generalizability of these findings to domains beyond MCQA, as the study focuses on a specific problem type with well-defined answer spaces
- **Medium Confidence**: The claimed robustness against unreliable agents, as the testing methodology uses simulated random failures rather than modeling realistic failure modes

## Next Checks

1. **Domain Transfer Test**: Apply the GEDI module with multiple voting methods to a non-MCQA domain (e.g., multi-criteria decision making or preference aggregation tasks) to assess generalizability beyond the current benchmarks
2. **Efficiency Analysis**: Measure and compare the computational overhead of each voting method relative to their accuracy improvements, particularly focusing on the trade-off between complex methods like Ranked Pairs and simpler alternatives
3. **Realistic Failure Simulation**: Replace random agent failure testing with scenarios involving systematic biases, coordinated adversarial behavior, or model drift to better understand real-world robustness limitations
