# Active Diffusion Subsampling

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2406.14388
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.14388

## Executive Summary
Active Diffusion Subsampling (ADS) addresses the challenge of designing intelligent subsampling masks for inverse imaging problems by leveraging diffusion models. The method employs a maximum entropy sampling policy that selects measurement locations maximizing information gain about the target signal. ADS can be applied using pre-trained diffusion models for any subsampling rate without task-specific retraining, requiring only the specification of a measurement model.

The approach demonstrates significant improvements in reconstruction quality compared to fixed sampling strategies on benchmark datasets, while maintaining interpretability in acquisition scheme design. ADS achieves competitive performance with supervised methods in MRI acceleration tasks, offering a promising alternative for medical imaging applications.

## Method Summary
ADS utilizes a pre-trained diffusion model to guide the subsampling process through maximum entropy sampling. The method tracks a distribution of beliefs over the true signal state throughout the reverse diffusion process, using the Tweedie estimate of the posterior distribution as an approximate posterior. This allows ADS to select measurement locations that maximize information gain about the target signal. The approach is task-agnostic and can be applied to various subsampling rates without requiring retraining, making it flexible for different imaging applications.

## Key Results
- ADS with 200 measurements achieves higher PSNR than random-mask DPS with 300 measurements on CelebA dataset
- ADS achieves 91.26 SSIM on fastMRI knee test set with 4x acceleration, outperforming fixed-mask diffusion-based approach (90.13 SSIM)
- Demonstrates significant improvement in reconstruction quality metrics compared to fixed sampling strategies on MNIST and CelebA datasets

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
ADS leverages the generative prior learned by diffusion models to guide subsampling decisions. By tracking the posterior distribution through the reverse diffusion process using Tweedie estimation, the method can quantify uncertainty and select measurements that maximize information gain. This adaptive sampling strategy exploits the structure captured by the pre-trained model to focus on the most informative regions of the signal space, resulting in improved reconstruction quality compared to fixed sampling patterns.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Pre-trained Diffusion Model -> Tweedie Posterior Tracking -> Maximum Entropy Sampler -> Subsampling Mask

**Critical Path:** The algorithm iteratively selects measurement locations by evaluating information gain at each step, using the Tweedie-estimated posterior to guide sampling decisions through the reverse diffusion process.

**Design Tradeoffs:** The method balances computational complexity of posterior tracking against sampling quality, with Tweedie approximation providing efficiency at the cost of some accuracy in complex scenarios.

**Failure Signatures:** Poor reconstruction quality may indicate:
- Inadequate Tweedie approximation accuracy
- Insufficient sampling budget for complex image structures
- Suboptimal pre-trained diffusion model for the target domain

## Limitations
- Tweedie approximation errors may accumulate during reverse diffusion, affecting sampling quality for complex image structures
- Performance on diverse real-world datasets beyond synthetic benchmarks remains untested
- Computational overhead of tracking belief distributions may limit real-time applicability in clinical settings
