## Executive Summary
This survey provides a comprehensive review of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) in epidemic modeling, addressing the limitations of traditional mechanistic models by leveraging GNNs' ability to capture complex relational information. The authors categorize epidemiological tasks into four main groups: Detection, Surveillance, Prediction, and Projection, and methodologies into Neural Models and Hybrid Models. Neural Models focus on data-driven approaches using GNNs, while Hybrid Models integrate mechanistic models with GNNs for enhanced interpretability and accuracy. The survey highlights various graph construction techniques and data sources, emphasizing the importance of multi-scale and multi-modal data.

## Method Summary
The authors systematically review existing literature on GNNs in epidemic modeling, categorizing studies based on their epidemiological tasks (Detection, Surveillance, Prediction, Projection) and methodologies (Neural Models, Hybrid Models). They analyze the graph construction techniques, data sources, and model architectures employed in these studies. The review also identifies future directions and challenges in the field, such as scalability, cross-modality, and explainable predictions.

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
GNNs excel in epidemic modeling due to their ability to capture complex relational information and interactions between individuals or regions. By representing the population as a graph, where nodes represent individuals or regions and edges represent their interactions, GNNs can effectively model the spread of diseases through these networks. The convolution operations in GNNs allow for the aggregation of information from neighboring nodes, enabling the model to capture local and global patterns in disease transmission. This relational learning capability is particularly useful in scenarios where traditional mechanistic models struggle to account for complex interactions and heterogeneity in the population.
