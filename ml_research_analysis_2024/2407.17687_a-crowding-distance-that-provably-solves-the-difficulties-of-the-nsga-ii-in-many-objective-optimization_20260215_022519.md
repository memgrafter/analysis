# A Crowding Distance That Provably Solves the Difficulties of the NSGA-II in Many-Objective Optimization

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2407.17687
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.17687
- Reference count: 10
- This paper proposes a new truthful crowding distance that resolves NSGA-II's inefficiency in many-objective optimization

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the known inefficiency of NSGA-II in solving many-objective optimization problems by modifying its crowding distance calculation. The core issue stems from NSGA-II's independent treatment of objectives in crowding distance, which allows distant points to unduly influence a solution's crowding distance. The authors propose a "truthful crowding distance" that considers the overall L1 distance between solutions with respect to all objectives, combined with sequential survival selection that updates crowding distances after each removal. Theoretical runtime analyses demonstrate that this approach efficiently covers the full Pareto front for many-objective versions of benchmark problems in polynomial time, contrasting the exponential lower bounds for the classic NSGA-II.

## Method Summary
The method replaces NSGA-II's classic crowding distance with a "truthful crowding distance" that uses normalized L1 distance between solutions considering all objectives simultaneously. This prevents distant points from having undue influence on a solution's crowding distance. The NSGA-II-T algorithm employs sequential survival selection, which updates crowding distances after each solution removal. The approach maintains the elitism property of keeping reached Pareto front points when the population size is at least the size of the maximal set of incomparable solutions. The implementation uses standard bit-wise mutation, fair or random selection, and optional crossover.

## Key Results
- NSGA-II-T covers the full Pareto front for many-objective versions of OneMinMax, OJZJ, and other benchmarks in polynomial time (O(nM) for mOneMinMax), contrasting exponential lower bounds for classic NSGA-II
- For bi-objective OneMinMax, NSGA-II-T achieves MEI ≤ 2n/N - 1 within O(n log n) iterations, slightly better than the classic NSGA-II's approximation guarantee
- The truthful crowding distance admits at most one individual with infinite crowding distance contribution per objective, compared to two in the classic version
- Empirical results on DLTB problem demonstrate the approach's effectiveness on combinatorial problems beyond bit-string representations

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: The NSGA-II-T algorithm overcomes the inefficiency of NSGA-II in many-objective optimization by using a "truthful crowding distance" that considers the overall L1 distance between solutions with respect to all objectives.
- Mechanism: The truthful crowding distance calculates the smallest normalized L1 distance between a solution and all solutions that are better than it in the considered objective. This ensures that points far away from a considered solution do not have undue influence on its crowding distance.
- Core assumption: The overall L1 distance is a more appropriate measure of similarity between solutions than independent per-objective distances.
- Evidence anchors:

For easy calculation, we adopt the L1 norm to reflect the distance."

- Claim: The NSGA-II-T algorithm retains the classic NSGA-II's good performance in two objectives while resolving its severe inefficiency in many-objective settings.
- Mechanism: By replacing the original crowding distance with the truthful crowding distance and using sequential survival selection, the NSGA-II-T algorithm maintains the elitism property of keeping any reached Pareto front point if the population size is at least the size of the maximal set of incomparable solutions.
- Core assumption: The elitism property is crucial for efficient optimization, and the truthful crowding distance combined with sequential survival selection preserves this property.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: The NSGA-II-T algorithm achieves a slightly better approximation of the bi-objective OneMinMax problem compared to the original NSGA-II with sequential survival selection.
- Mechanism: The truthful crowding distance admits at most one individual with infinite crowding distance contribution per objective, whereas the classic crowding distance admits two. This leads to a more precise approximation of the Pareto front.
- Core assumption: A more precise approximation of the Pareto front is desirable and achievable through the truthful crowding distance.

## Foundational Learning

- Concept: Non-dominated sorting
  - Why needed here: Non-dominated sorting is the primary selection criterion in NSGA-II and its variants, used to rank solutions based on their dominance relationships.
  - Quick check question: In a minimization problem, if solution A is dominated by solution B, which one has a better non-dominated sorting rank?

- Concept: Crowding distance
  - Why needed here: Crowding distance is the secondary selection criterion in NSGA-II, used to maintain diversity in the population by preferring solutions in less crowded regions of the objective space.
  - Quick check question: How does the classic crowding distance calculation treat objectives, and why is this problematic in many-objective optimization?

- Concept: Pareto front
  - Why needed here: The Pareto front is the set of non-dominated solutions in the objective space, and the goal of multi-objective optimization is to find a representative sample of this front.
  - Quick check question: What is the difference between the Pareto front and the Pareto optimal set?

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
  - Parent population (Pt) -> Offspring population (Qt) -> Combined population (Rt = Pt ∪ Qt) -> Non-dominated fronts (F1, F2, ...) -> Truthful crowding distance (tCD) -> Sequential survival selection -> Updated parent population (Pt+1)

- Critical path:
  1. Generate initial population Pt0
  2. Generate offspring Qt using variation operators
  3. Combine parent and offspring populations: Rt = Pt ∪ Qt
  4. Perform non-dominated sorting on Rt
  5. Identify the set of fronts to be selected for the next generation
  6. Calculate truthful crowding distances for solutions in the last front
  7. Sequentially remove solutions with the smallest tCD until the population size is reached
  8. Update Pt+1 and repeat from step 2

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Population size: Larger populations can better approximate the Pareto front but increase computational cost
  - Variation operators: Different crossover and mutation operators can affect the exploration and exploitation balance
  - Termination criterion: The number of generations or a convergence metric can be used to stop the algorithm

- Failure signatures:
  - Slow convergence: The algorithm may struggle to find good solutions if the population size is too small or the variation operators are not effective
  - Premature convergence: The algorithm may converge to a suboptimal solution if the population lacks diversity or the selection pressure is too high
  - Inaccurate approximation: The algorithm may not provide a good approximation of the Pareto front if the population size is too small or the termination criterion is not met

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Implement the NSGA-II-T algorithm with a small population size (e.g., 10) and run it on a simple bi-objective problem (e.g., OneMinMax) to verify the basic functionality
  2. Increase the population size and compare the runtime and approximation quality with the original NSGA-II on the same problem
  3. Test the algorithm on a many-objective problem (e.g., mOneMinMax with m > 2) and verify that it can efficiently cover the full Pareto front, unlike the original NSGA-II

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
The paper invites researchers to test the proposed NSGA-II-T on practical applications, suggesting interest in empirical validation on real-world many-objective optimization problems.

## Limitations
- The analysis is limited to specific benchmark problems (OneMinMax, OJZJ, COCZ, LOTZ variants) with uniform 0/1 bit strings, though DLTB results show promise for combinatorial problems
- The approach assumes population size N ≥ M (where M is the size of the maximal incomparable solution set), which may not hold in practical scenarios
- The theoretical runtime guarantees are proven only for the benchmark problems, with no theoretical analysis provided for the DLTB combinatorial problem despite empirical results

## Confidence
- **High confidence**: The polynomial runtime guarantees for benchmark problems are well-supported by rigorous proofs. The mechanism of truthful crowding distance preventing distant points from influencing selection is clearly explained and theoretically validated.
- **Medium confidence**: The extension to DLTB shows promising empirical results but lacks theoretical runtime analysis. The improvement for bi-objective problems (MEI ≤ 2n/N - 1) is demonstrated but represents a relatively minor enhancement over existing results.
- **Low confidence**: Generalization to arbitrary combinatorial problems beyond the tested benchmarks remains unproven. The practical impact of the correlated tie-breaking rule implementation details is not fully explored.

## Next Checks
