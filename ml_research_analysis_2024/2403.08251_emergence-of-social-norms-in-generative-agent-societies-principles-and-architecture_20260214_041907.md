# Emergence of Social Norms in Generative Agent Societies: Principles and Architecture

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2403.08251
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.08251
- Authors: Siyue Ren; Zhiyao Cui; Ruiqi Song; Zhen Wang; Shuyue Hu
- Reference count: 25
- One-line primary result: CRSEC architecture enables 100% norm acceptance and compliance in generative agent societies

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces CRSEC, a novel architecture for enabling social norm emergence in generative multi-agent systems. The architecture consists of four interconnected modules: Creation & Representation, Spreading, Evaluation, and Compliance, which together address the fundamental questions of norm origins, representation, dissemination, assessment, and behavioral incorporation. Experiments in the Smallville environment demonstrate that social norms successfully emerge in all simulation runs, resulting in complete agent acceptance and compliance while virtually eliminating social conflicts. Human evaluators assessed the system positively with an average score of 5.63/7.

## Method Summary
The research employs the Smallville sandbox environment with 10 generative agents (3 norm entrepreneurs, 7 ordinary agents) to test the CRSEC architecture. The method involves implementing four modules: Creation & Representation generates personal norms from agent descriptions, Spreading propagates norms through communication and observation, Evaluation assesses and synthesizes norms over time, and Compliance ensures agents incorporate norms into their planning and actions. The simulation runs for 2 days while collecting data on norm emergence, conflict reduction, and compliance metrics.

## Key Results
- Social norms emerged in all simulation runs with 100% agent acceptance and compliance
- Social conflicts virtually vanished as norms became established
- Human evaluation yielded an overall positive score of 5.63/7
- The norm emergence process followed a predictable pattern from initial conflicts to stable compliance

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: The CRSEC architecture enables social norms to emerge through a cyclic process of creation, spreading, evaluation, and compliance.
- Mechanism: Agents initially create personal norms based on their descriptions, spread them through communication and observation, evaluate and refine them, and comply with them in planning and actions, reinforcing the norm through behavior.
- Core assumption: LLM agents can generate and evaluate normative information in natural language and store it in a structured database.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "Our architecture consists of four modules: Creation & Representation, Spreading, Evaluation, and Compliance."
  - [section 2.4] "The Compliance module raises agents' awareness of personal norms in their behaviors."
  - [corpus] Weak evidence; this is a novel architecture not directly validated in prior work.
- Break condition: If LLMs fail to generate consistent normative information or agents do not comply with norms in actions.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Social conflicts drive the initial spread of norms, which are then resolved through norm adoption.
- Mechanism: When agents observe behaviors conflicting with their personal norms, they communicate about the conflict, leading to norm identification and evaluation. As norms are adopted, conflicts decrease.
- Core assumption: Agents detect conflicts between observations and personal norms and initiate conversations to influence others.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section 2.2] "The desire to resolve social conflicts has driven the emergence of numerous social norms [Nyla R. Branscombe, 2022]."
  - [section 3.2] "The initial surge in social conflicts... triggered numerous conversations among agents as well as their observations about these conflicts."
  - [corpus] Weak evidence; this mechanism is hypothesized based on human society observations.
- Break condition: If agents do not detect conflicts or do not initiate conversations to resolve them.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Long-term synthesis of personal norms keeps the norm set compact and concise, facilitating easier compliance.
- Mechanism: When the sum of utilities of qualified personal norms exceeds a threshold, agents synthesize them into more abstract norms, deactivating the specific ones.
- Core assumption: Agents can categorize norms, generate abstract norms, and evaluate them for consistency and conflicts.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section 2.3] "Morales et al. [2013; 2015 ] suggested that for better agent liberty, it would be beneficial to synthesize norms into a compact and concise set of possibly more abstract ones."
  - [section 3.2] "Sometimes there exists a slight decrease in the proportion of agents accepting a norm in databases. This occurs because qualified personal norms are synthesized into abstract ones."
  - [corpus] Weak evidence; this mechanism is inspired by human society but not directly validated in prior agent-based norm emergence work.
- Break condition: If agents fail to synthesize norms or the abstract norms are not more general or easier to comply with.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Creation & Representation -> Spreading -> Evaluation -> Compliance -> Reinforcement of Spreading
- Critical path: Creation → Spreading → Evaluation → Compliance → Reinforcement of Spreading
- Design tradeoffs:
  - Granularity of norms vs. ease of compliance (synthesis module)
  - Autonomy of agents vs. norm enforcement (compliance module)
  - Communication overhead vs. norm spread efficiency (spreading module)
- Failure signatures:
  - Norms not emerging: Agents not creating or spreading norms effectively
  - Conflicts persisting: Agents not evaluating or complying with norms correctly
  - Norms becoming too complex: Synthesis module not functioning properly
- First 3 experiments:
  1. Test norm creation and representation with different agent descriptions
  2. Test norm spread through communication and observation in a small agent society
  3. Test norm evaluation and synthesis with varying norm sets and thresholds

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: What are the key factors that influence the emergence and acceptance of social norms in generative multi-agent systems?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper identifies several factors, including norm entrepreneurs, communication, observation, and the evaluation and synthesis of norms. It also mentions the role of conflicts and the importance of descriptive vs. injunctive norms.
- Why unresolved: While the paper provides insights into these factors, it does not fully explore their relative importance or the specific mechanisms by which they influence norm emergence. Further research is needed to understand how these factors interact and contribute to the overall process.
- What evidence would resolve it: Experiments varying the presence or absence of specific factors, such as norm entrepreneurs or communication channels, could help determine their impact on norm emergence. Additionally, analyzing the dynamics of norm acceptance and adherence over time could provide insights into the key drivers of norm evolution.

### Open Question 2
- Question: How can generative multi-agent systems be designed to prevent the emergence of negative social norms?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper mentions the potential for negative norms to arise in generative agent societies, but does not explore strategies for preventing their emergence.
- Why unresolved: The paper focuses on the emergence of positive norms, but does not address the potential for negative norms to emerge and the consequences of their existence. Further research is needed to develop mechanisms for preventing the emergence of harmful norms and ensuring that generative agents adhere to positive social standards.
- What evidence would resolve it: Experiments introducing scenarios where negative norms could potentially emerge, followed by the implementation of prevention mechanisms, could help evaluate the effectiveness of different strategies. Additionally, analyzing the long-term impact of negative norms on agent behavior and system performance could provide insights into the importance of prevention.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How can generative multi-agent systems be designed to handle conflicts and disagreements between agents with different cultural backgrounds or values?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper acknowledges the potential for conflicts to arise in generative agent societies due to differences in values and preferences. It also mentions the importance of norm entrepreneurs in shaping the emergence of descriptive norms.
- Why unresolved: The paper does not explore specific strategies for resolving conflicts between agents with diverse backgrounds or values. Further research is needed to develop mechanisms for facilitating dialogue, negotiation, and compromise between agents with conflicting perspectives.
- What evidence would resolve it: Experiments introducing scenarios where agents with different cultural backgrounds or values interact and encounter conflicts could help evaluate the effectiveness of different conflict resolution strategies. Additionally, analyzing the impact of conflict resolution mechanisms on norm emergence and agent cooperation could provide insights into the importance of addressing cultural differences in generative agent societies.

## Limitations

- The paper lacks explicit experimental controls and comparison with baseline architectures
- The internal decision-making processes of LLM agents cannot be fully verified
- Experiments were limited to 10 agents in a controlled sandbox environment, limiting scalability assessment

## Confidence

- **High Confidence**: The architectural framework and module descriptions are clearly specified and internally consistent
- **Medium Confidence**: The claimed 100% norm compliance and conflict reduction is based on simulation data without statistical validation
- **Low Confidence**: The scalability of CRSEC to larger agent populations or more complex social environments is speculative
