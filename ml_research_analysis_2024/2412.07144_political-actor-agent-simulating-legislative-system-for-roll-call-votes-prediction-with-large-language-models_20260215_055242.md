# Political Actor Agent: Simulating Legislative System for Roll Call Votes Prediction with Large Language Models

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2412.07144
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.07144
- Authors: Hao Li; Ruoyuan Gong; Hao Jiang
- Reference count: 7
- Primary result: Achieves up to 93.0% accuracy and 92.2% F1-score on U.S. House voting data using LLM-based role-playing agents

## Executive Summary
The Political Actor Agent (PAA) introduces a novel agent-based framework for predicting legislative roll call votes using Large Language Models. Unlike traditional embedding-based approaches, PAA simulates political actors through role-playing architectures and an influence mechanism, enabling interpretable and scalable predictions. The framework constructs detailed legislator profiles and guides LLM agents through multi-view reasoning processes to make voting decisions. Experiments on the 117-118th U.S. House of Representatives data demonstrate superior performance compared to existing methods, with up to 93.0% accuracy and 92.2% F1-score. The approach offers advantages in interpretability, flexibility with limited data, and potential for broader political science applications.

## Method Summary
PAA simulates legislative voting through three integrated modules: profile construction, multi-view planning, and simulated legislative action. Legislator profiles are built from personal information, constituency details, sponsorship activity, and voting records. The multi-view planning module guides LLM agents through trustee, delegate, and follower reasoning perspectives. The influence mechanism implements hierarchical decision-making where leader agents (party leaders, committee chairs) vote first, influencing follower agents' decisions. The framework uses LLMs like Llama-3-70B or GPT-4o-mini to execute agent reasoning, requiring no explicit model training beyond profile construction from a small sample of voting records.

## Key Results
- Achieves up to 93.0% accuracy and 92.2% F1-score on 117-118th U.S. House of Representatives voting data
- Demonstrates superior performance compared to ideal-vector and graph-based methods
- Maintains stability and performance even with limited training data (as few as 20 voting records per legislator)

- Mechanism: Legislators are modeled as agents with detailed profiles that include personal information, constituency details, sponsorship activity, and voting records. These profiles are integrated into prompts that guide LLM-based agents through multi-view planning (trustee, delegate, follower views) before making voting decisions.
- Core assumption: LLMs can effectively simulate complex political decision-making when provided with structured profiles and guided reasoning frameworks.
- Evidence anchors:

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: The influence mechanism in PAA improves prediction accuracy by simulating real legislative dynamics where leader agents influence follower agents' decisions.
- Mechanism: Leader agents (Speaker, party leaders, committee chairs) vote first based on multi-view planning, then follower agents make decisions conditioned on knowing the leaders' choices, creating a hierarchical decision-making process that mirrors actual legislative behavior.
- Core assumption: Legislative voting follows predictable patterns where influential figures' decisions shape the choices of other legislators.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section] "In the influence mechanism, leader agents first vote based on the multi-view planning module... the remaining agents making their choices after learning the leaders' decisions"
  - [section] "This method effectively approximates the real-world legislative process"
  - [corpus] Weak evidence - corpus papers focus on bias analysis rather than hierarchical influence mechanisms
- Break condition: If leader agent selection criteria don't accurately reflect real legislative influence structures, the mechanism's predictive power degrades.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: PAA achieves superior performance with limited data by leveraging LLM capabilities rather than requiring extensive training datasets.
- Mechanism: Instead of training embedding-based models on large datasets, PAA constructs agent profiles from a small sample (20 voting records) and uses LLM reasoning capabilities to make predictions, maintaining performance even as training data decreases.
- Core assumption: LLMs possess sufficient general knowledge and reasoning ability to make accurate predictions when provided with relevant context through profiles.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section] "The PAA integrates past voting records directly into the profile without explicit model training. Leveraging the capabilities of large language models, we can predict voting outcomes based on a limited amount of known information"

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
  - Profile Construction Module -> Multi-view Planning Module -> Simulated Legislative Action Module -> LLM Integration Layer

- Critical path: Profile construction → Multi-view planning → Influence mechanism → Vote prediction
  - Each component must complete successfully for accurate predictions
  - Profile quality directly impacts all downstream reasoning

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Profile comprehensiveness vs. LLM token limits
  - Leader agent selection criteria vs. prediction accuracy
  - Number of reasoning views vs. computational efficiency
  - Sample size for profile construction vs. prediction stability

- Failure signatures:
  - Inconsistent predictions across runs → LLM hallucination issues
  - Low accuracy on new legislators → Profile construction problems
  - Degradation with limited training data → Insufficient profile information
  - Slow response times → Token limit constraints or inefficient prompt design

## Limitations

- The framework's effectiveness depends heavily on the LLM's reasoning capabilities, which may vary across different architectures and political contexts
- Leader agent selection criteria are not fully specified, potentially affecting the influence mechanism's predictive power
- Claims about cross-domain generalizability lack supporting evidence from non-U.S. legislative systems

## Confidence

**High Confidence**: The architectural framework (role-playing agents, multi-view planning, influence mechanism) is clearly defined and methodologically sound. The performance metrics (93.0% accuracy, 92.2% F1-score) are verifiable through the specified datasets and evaluation procedures.

**Medium Confidence**: Claims about interpretability improvements and data efficiency advantages require empirical validation across diverse legislative contexts and LLM architectures. The mechanism descriptions provide clear theoretical foundations but lack extensive empirical validation beyond the U.S. House dataset.

**Low Confidence**: Claims about cross-domain generalizability and scalability to other legislative systems lack supporting evidence. The paper does not address potential LLM hallucinations or provide robustness tests for profile construction under data constraints.
