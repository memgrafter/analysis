# Less is More: Towards Sustainability-Aware Persuasive Explanations in Recommender Systems

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2409.18690
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.18690
- Reference count: 33
- Key outcome: Authority-based explanations most effective for sustainability-aware recommendations

## Executive Summary
This paper investigates how persuasive explanations in recommender systems can promote sustainability awareness and decision-making aligned with UN Sustainable Development Goals. By integrating Cialdini's persuasive principles into sustainability-aware explanations across three domains (books, healthy food, cars), the authors evaluated their effectiveness through a user study with 158 participants. The research found that authority-based explanations consistently outperformed other persuasive approaches, particularly in high-involvement domains where users placed greater importance on sustainability aspects. The study demonstrates that carefully designed persuasive explanations can effectively encourage more sustainable choices while highlighting domain-specific variations in user receptiveness.

## Method Summary
The study employed a within-subject user study design where 158 participants evaluated sustainability-aware explanations for three item domains. Participants rated explanations based on six Cialdini persuasive principles (reciprocity, scarcity, authority, commitment, liking, social proof) using both ranking (1-6) and 5-level rating scales. Data was collected via Google Forms with randomized explanation order to control for presentation bias. Statistical analysis included ANOVA tests for persuasiveness/effectiveness rankings and Kruskal-Wallis tests for importance/influence ratings, comparing results across different domain involvement levels.

## Key Results
- Authority-based explanations consistently ranked highest for persuasiveness and effectiveness across all domains
- Sustainability aspects were perceived as more important in high-involvement domains (healthy food, cars) compared to low-involvement domains (books)
- Social proof-based explanations received the lowest rankings, suggesting user skepticism toward peer influence for sustainability decisions
- Domain-specific variations showed that users placed greater importance on sustainability in average- and high-involvement item domains

- Mechanism: Users trust expert opinions more than peer influence or scarcity tactics, particularly in high-involvement domains like healthy food and cars.
- Core assumption: Users perceive authoritative sources as more credible and their recommendations as more trustworthy than other persuasive approaches.

- Claim: Social proof-based explanations are least effective for promoting sustainability-aware choices.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How do sustainability-aware persuasive explanations impact long-term user behavior change compared to short-term persuasiveness?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper discusses persuasiveness and effectiveness but does not examine long-term behavioral impacts
- Why unresolved: The user study only measured immediate responses to explanations, not sustained behavior change over time
- What evidence would resolve it: Longitudinal studies tracking user purchasing behavior over months/years after exposure to sustainability-aware explanations

### Open Question 2
- Question: What is the optimal balance between sustainability messaging and commercial interests in recommender systems?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper mentions that such explanations "might be counterproductive" from a producer/company perspective by potentially decreasing turnovers
- Why unresolved: The study did not explore trade-offs between sustainability goals and business objectives
- What evidence would resolve it: Empirical studies measuring both sustainability impact and revenue effects across different messaging intensities

### Open Question 3
- Question: How do different demographic groups respond to sustainability-aware persuasive explanations?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper notes demographic information was collected but does not analyze differences across demographic groups
- Why unresolved: The user study aggregated results without examining demographic-specific patterns
- What evidence would resolve it: Segmented analysis of persuasiveness, effectiveness, importance, and influence ratings by age, gender, education, and cultural background

## Limitations
- Study relies entirely on self-reported measures rather than actual behavior change in real-world recommender systems
- Limited ecological validity due to artificial study setup without real recommendation context or decision-making pressure
- No long-term follow-up to assess whether persuasive explanations lead to sustained sustainable behavior changes

## Confidence
- High confidence: Authority-based explanations outperform other persuasive principles in the studied contexts
- Medium confidence: Domain involvement level affects receptiveness to sustainability messaging
- Low confidence: Social proof is universally ineffective for sustainability-aware recommendations (limited testing across contexts)
