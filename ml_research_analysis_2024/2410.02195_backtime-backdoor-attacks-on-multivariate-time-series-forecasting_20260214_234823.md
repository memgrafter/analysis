# BACKTIME: Backdoor Attacks on Multivariate Time Series Forecasting

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2410.02195
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.02195
- Authors: Xiao Lin; Zhining Liu; Dongqi Fu; Ruizhong Qiu; Hanghang Tong
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: First backdoor attack framework for multivariate time series forecasting achieving state-of-the-art effectiveness

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces BACKTIME, a novel backdoor attack framework designed specifically for multivariate time series (MTS) forecasting. The framework addresses the unique challenges of attacking forecasting models by generating stealthy and sparse triggers that manipulate model predictions according to attacker intent. BACKTIME identifies vulnerable timestamps using clean model prediction errors, then adaptively generates triggers using a GNN-based trigger generator that considers inter-variable correlations. The framework solves a bi-level optimization problem with constraints on trigger amplitude and target pattern similarity to historical data.

## Method Summary
BACKTIME operates through a bi-level optimization framework where the upper level minimizes attack loss between the surrogate model's output and the target pattern, while the lower level maximizes the surrogate model's forecasting ability on the poisoned dataset. The trigger generator uses a GCN to create stealthy and effective triggers based on inter-variable correlations learned from historical data. A soft identification mechanism controls trigger activation based on the proportion of target patterns within the future to be forecasted, ensuring the backdoor effect gradually diminishes over time. The framework is evaluated across five real-world datasets and three different forecasting model architectures.

## Key Results
- Achieves state-of-the-art attack effectiveness while maintaining competitive clean forecasting performance
- Demonstrates effectiveness across different model architectures (TimesNet, FEDformer, Autoformer)
- Shows high stealthiness with anomaly detection methods failing to identify poisoned timestamps
- Effective across different target pattern shapes and spatial injection rates

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
The bi-level optimization framework ensures the surrogate model learns to produce outputs resembling the target pattern when triggers are present, while the trigger generator learns to create effective and stealthy triggers. The upper-level optimization minimizes attack loss between the surrogate model's output and the target pattern, while the lower-level optimization maximizes forecasting ability on the poisoned dataset. This adversarial training allows the trigger generator to produce triggers that are both effective and stealthy.

### Mechanism 2
The GNN-based trigger generator leverages inter-variable correlations to create more effective and sparse triggers. It learns a weighted graph measuring inter-variable correlations within target variables, then uses a GCN to generate triggers based on sliced historical data and the learned graph. This approach creates triggers effective for multiple correlated variables, increasing sparsity.

### Mechanism 3
The soft identification mechanism ensures the backdoor activates only when inputs contain all trigger components, with the effect gradually diminishing over time. It defines poisoning degree based on the proportion of target patterns within the future to be forecasted, ensuring the backdoor's influence is most pronounced at activation and gradually diminishes as time progresses.

## Limitations

- Trigger patterns used in experiments not fully specified
- Attack robustness not evaluated against comprehensive range of defense mechanisms
- Limited testing of transferability across different model architectures
