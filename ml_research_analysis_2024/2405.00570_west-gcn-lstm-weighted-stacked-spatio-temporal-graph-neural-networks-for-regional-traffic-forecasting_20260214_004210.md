# WEST GCN-LSTM: Weighted Stacked Spatio-Temporal Graph Neural Networks for Regional Traffic Forecasting

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2405.00570
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.00570
- Authors: Theodoros Theodoropoulos; Angelos-Christos Maroudis; Antonios Makris; Konstantinos Tserpes
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Introduces weighted stacked graph convolutions with shared border lengths and adjustable hops for regional traffic forecasting

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces WEST GCN-LSTM, a novel spatio-temporal graph neural network for regional traffic forecasting that incorporates both spatial and temporal aspects of traffic systems. The key innovation is the use of weighted stacked graph convolutions, where the adjacency matrix is constructed based on shared border lengths between regions (Shared Borders Policy), and the number of convolution layers is determined by the average speed of populations traversing the regions (Adjustable Hops Policy). This approach enables the model to capture both the spatial topology of regions and the temporal dynamics of population movement. Experimental evaluation on real and synthetic datasets demonstrates significant performance improvements over 19 competing models.

## Method Summary
WEST GCN-LSTM combines weighted stacked graph convolutional layers with LSTM layers to forecast regional traffic. The model uses the Shared Borders Policy to weight edges by border length between regions, ensuring neighboring regions influence each other proportionally to their physical connection. The Adjustable Hops Policy calculates the number of convolution layers (K) based on population speed, allowing faster populations to aggregate information over longer distances per time-step. The architecture takes regional traffic values over multiple time-steps as input and outputs predictions for future time periods, with the weighted adjacency matrix and K value determined by the proposed policies.

## Key Results
- WEST GCN-LSTM achieves up to 46% better MAE compared to the best linear regression model across different datasets
- The model achieves 22% better MAE compared to the best GCN-LSTM variant across different datasets
- The ablation study confirms that both Shared Borders Policy and Adjustable Hops Policy contribute to the overall performance enhancement

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Weighted stacked graph convolutions with adjustable hop counts better capture both spatial topology and temporal dynamics of regional traffic.
- Mechanism: The model uses the Shared Borders Policy to weight edges by border length, ensuring only neighboring regions influence each other proportionally to their physical connection. The Adjustable Hops Policy calculates K based on population speed, allowing faster populations to aggregate information over longer distances per time-step.
- Core assumption: Traffic flow between regions is primarily determined by physical border length and population movement speed, not just adjacency or distance.
- Evidence anchors: [abstract], [section IV-B]
- Break condition: If border length doesn't correlate with actual traffic flow (e.g., due to physical barriers, tolls, or one-way restrictions), the weighting becomes misleading.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Multi-region forecasting requires models that can handle both major and minor regional traffic inequality.
- Mechanism: The model uses graph convolutions to filter and aggregate regional traffic information, reducing the impact of regions that monopolize traffic volume.
- Core assumption: Graph convolutions can effectively mitigate the impact of regional traffic inequality by focusing on local spatial relationships rather than raw traffic volume differences.
- Evidence anchors: [section V-D]
- Break condition: If regional traffic inequality is so extreme that even local spatial relationships cannot compensate, the model's performance degrades significantly.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: The combination of weighted adjacency matrices and adjustable K creates a more refined encapsulation of spatial dependencies than binary or distance-based approaches.
- Mechanism: By weighting edges based on shared border length rather than simple binary adjacency, the model captures nuanced spatial relationships. By adjusting K based on population speed, it ensures that faster-moving populations can influence predictions over appropriate spatial scales.
- Core assumption: Simple binary adjacency matrices or distance-based weighting are insufficient for capturing the true spatial dependencies in regional traffic systems.
- Evidence anchors: [section II], [section V-D]
- Break condition: If the relationship between border length and traffic flow is not monotonic or if population speeds vary too widely within the same region, the weighting and K adjustment may become counterproductive.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Input → Shared Borders Policy → Weighted Adjacency Matrix → Adjustable Hops Policy → K → Weighted Stacked GCN → Aggregated Representation → LSTM Decoder → Output

- Critical path: The critical path flows from input data through the two policy components to determine the graph structure, then through the weighted stacked GCN layers for spatial modeling, followed by LSTM layers for temporal modeling, and finally to the output predictions.

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Using border length weighting vs. binary adjacency: More nuanced spatial relationships but requires accurate border data
  - Adjustable K vs. fixed K: Better temporal modeling but increased model complexity and potential overfitting
  - Stacked GCN layers vs. single layer: Better spatial modeling but increased computational cost

- Failure signatures:
  - Poor performance on datasets with non-monotonic relationship between border length and traffic flow
  - Overfitting when K is too large relative to available data
  - Underperformance compared to simpler models when regional traffic inequality is minimal

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Train WEST GCN-LSTM on Berlin dataset with varying K values to find optimal number of convolution layers
  2. Compare WEST GCN-LSTM performance with and without Shared Borders Policy on Central Park dataset
  3. Test model sensitivity to border length accuracy by introducing noise to border length measurements and measuring performance degradation

## Limitations
- The effectiveness of the Shared Borders Policy relies heavily on the assumption that border length correlates with traffic flow, which may not hold in all scenarios
- The Adjustable Hops Policy's reliance on average population speed as a proxy for optimal convolution depth introduces potential sensitivity to speed estimation errors
- The model's performance on datasets with extreme regional traffic inequality remains unclear

## Confidence
- **High Confidence**: The overall methodology of using weighted stacked graph convolutions for spatio-temporal modeling is well-established and the experimental setup is appropriately rigorous.
- **Medium Confidence**: The specific policies (Shared Borders and Adjustable Hops) are novel and their effectiveness is demonstrated, but the theoretical justification could be strengthened with more diverse real-world validation.
- **Low Confidence**: The model's robustness to varying levels of regional traffic inequality and its generalizability to larger, more complex traffic networks remain uncertain.
