# Lost in Recursion: Mining Rich Event Semantics in Knowledge Graphs

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2404.16405
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.16405
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Introduces recursive narrative construction and binding to ECKGs for mining viewpoint-specific narratives

## Executive Summary
This paper presents a novel approach to mining and representing narratives from multiple viewpoints on complex real-world events. The authors formalize narratives using recursive nodes to represent different levels of detail and bind them to event-centric knowledge graphs (ECKGs). An algorithm based on incremental prompting techniques mines such narratives from text sources to account for different perspectives. A proof-of-concept case study on the Iraq War demonstrates the approach, mining 105 event labels across three viewpoints (Russia, UK, US) and successfully binding 50 events to Wikidata.

## Method Summary
The approach combines recursive narrative representation with event-centric knowledge graphs to capture multiple viewpoints on complex events. The method uses a large language model with incremental prompting to mine narratives from document collections, employing pairwise matching and synthesis to create viewpoint-specific event sequences. The recursive structure allows for hierarchical representation of events, while binding to ECKGs provides disambiguation and contextualization of event labels.

## Key Results
- Successfully mined 105 event labels across three viewpoints (Russia, UK, US) on the Iraq War
- Bound 50 events to Wikidata, demonstrating effective narrative-ECKG integration
- Captured viewpoint-specific narratives with distinct event selections and temporal orderings
- Demonstrated the ability to handle compound event labels and indirect binding cases

- Component map:
  Input: Event, viewpoint, document collection -> Processing: Event detection, timeline extraction, pairwise matching, synthesis, recursion -> Output: Narrative with recursive structure and bindings to ECKG

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Using open-domain web data provides rich, diverse narratives but introduces noise and requires careful event disambiguation
  - Recursive narrative structure enables detailed representation but increases complexity in event binding and mining
  - LLM-based mining is flexible and domain-independent but may struggle with precise temporal relationships and event extraction

- Failure signatures:
  - Incomplete or incorrect event timelines due to poor document selection or LLM hallucinations
  - Failure to bind events to ECKG due to ambiguous event labels or lack of corresponding ECKG entries
  - Infinite recursion or excessive depth due to improper termination conditions

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Test event extraction accuracy on a small, controlled document set with known events
  2. Evaluate event binding success rate on a sample of extracted events with a small ECKG
  3. Assess narrative quality and viewpoint differences on a limited set of viewpoints and documents

## Limitations

- The recursive narrative representation introduces significant computational and semantic complexity that may limit scalability to very large events
- The binding mechanism to ECKGs is conceptually sound but empirically validated only partially (50/105 events successfully bound)
- The proof-of-concept case study focuses on a single event across three viewpoints, limiting generalizability to other complex events or larger numbers of perspectives

## Confidence

- High confidence: The formal definitions of narratives, viewpoints, and binding mechanisms (ùúÇ and ùúá functions) are clearly specified and internally consistent. The overall architecture and methodology description are coherent and implementable.
- Medium confidence: The incremental prompting algorithm and its recursive nature are well-described, but the effectiveness depends heavily on prompt engineering quality which isn't fully specified. The pairwise matching approach using SBERT and HDBSCAN is standard but may struggle with semantic similarity in event descriptions.
- Low confidence: The claims about viewpoint-specific narrative capture are based on qualitative observations rather than quantitative analysis. The paper doesn't provide systematic evaluation of narrative quality, viewpoint differences, or comparison with baseline methods.
