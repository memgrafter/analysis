# Interactions with Generative Information Retrieval Systems

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2407.11605
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.11605
- Reference count: 0
- Primary result: Survey of user interactions with generative IR systems, highlighting new interaction modalities enabled by LLMs

## Executive Summary
This paper explores how generative information retrieval systems are transforming user interactions, moving beyond traditional keyword-based search to support richer, natural language-based interactions. The authors examine how large language models enable users to express information needs at all four levels of Taylor's model and discuss various interaction types including feedback, result refinement, clarification, proactive interactions, explanation, and multi-modal interactions. The paper identifies key technical challenges and opportunities for future development in generative IR systems.

## Method Summary
This is a survey paper that reviews and synthesizes existing literature on user interactions with generative information retrieval systems. Rather than presenting original experiments, the authors analyze frameworks and mechanisms from the literature, organizing them around different types of user interactions and their associated challenges. The paper draws on concepts from information retrieval, human-computer interaction, and natural language processing to provide a comprehensive overview of the field.

## Key Results
- Generative IR systems can support all four levels of information needs (visceral, conscious, formalized, compromised) as proposed by Robert Taylor
- Multi-modal interactions expand beyond text to include images, videos, gestures, and sensor data
- Proactive interactions represent a significant shift from user-initiated to system-initiated assistance
- The paper identifies key technical challenges including algorithmic issues in result refinement and planning challenges in clarification

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: LLMs enable users to express information needs at all four levels of Taylor's model (visceral, conscious, formalized, compromised).
- Mechanism: Natural language processing and contextual understanding allow LLMs to interpret vague, partially formed, or flexible queries and progressively refine them through dialogue.
- Core assumption: LLMs can maintain context over multiple interactions and adapt responses based on perceived information need level.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract]: "users can express themselves freely through natural language" and "LLMs-based information access systems have the potential to support all four levels."
  - [section 2]: Describes how LLMs can assist at each of Taylor's four levels through exploratory interactions, prompt suggestions, guided conversations, etc.
  - [corpus]: Weak/no direct evidence for this specific mechanism in corpus papers.
- Break condition: If LLMs fail to maintain context across turns or misinterpret the user's intent, they cannot effectively support the lower levels of information need.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Generative IR systems can proactively initiate interactions to assist users.
- Mechanism: The system monitors user context and information streams, generates proactive instances (conversation purpose, content, context), and selects appropriate moments to initiate helpful interactions like recommendations or clarifications.
- Core assumption: The system can accurately identify opportune moments for proactive interaction and generate contextually appropriate utterances.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section 6.1]: Describes the generic pipeline for conversation initiation including monitoring context, producing instances, and selecting when/how to initiate.
  - [section 6.3]: Details technical challenges in producing system-initiative instances and developing an initiator model.
  - [corpus]: Weak/no direct evidence for this specific mechanism in corpus papers.
- Break condition: If the initiator model fails to predict good timing or generates irrelevant proactive interactions, user satisfaction and trust will decrease.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Multi-modal interactions expand the ways users can express information needs beyond text.
- Mechanism: Multi-modal LLMs can process and respond to combinations of text, images, videos, gestures, and sensor data, enabling richer expression of needs in complex scenarios.
- Core assumption: Multi-modal foundation models can effectively align and fuse different modality information into meaningful representations.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [section 8]: Describes how multi-modal signals have been used in search/recommendation and how generative IR systems with multi-modal interactions can handle complex scenarios.
  - [section 2]: Mentions that interactions can go "beyond natural language, through images, videos, gestures, and sensors."
  - [corpus]: Weak/no direct evidence for this specific mechanism in corpus papers.
- Break condition: If the multi-modal alignment and fusion techniques are inadequate, the system cannot properly interpret cross-modal user inputs.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
  User Interface Layer -> Interaction Manager -> Information Need Expressor -> Grounding Layer -> Retriever -> Generator -> User Interface

- Critical path:
  User query → Information Need Expressor → Grounding Layer → Retriever → Generator → User Interface

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Proactivity vs. user control: Too many proactive interactions annoy users; too few miss opportunities
  - Complexity vs. usability: Rich multi-modal interactions are powerful but can overwhelm users
  - Grounding vs. fluency: Strict grounding may limit response quality; loose grounding risks hallucinations
  - Refinement granularity: Fine-grained attribute refinement is powerful but increases interaction complexity

- Failure signatures:
  - User abandons sessions frequently (proactive interactions too intrusive)
  - Users struggle to formulate effective prompts (Gulf of Envisioning gap)
  - Generated responses contain factual errors (grounding failures)
  - System fails to understand multi-modal inputs (alignment/fusion issues)

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Implement basic Taylor-level query processing: Test how well the system handles visceral vs. formalized queries using a controlled user study.
  2. Build simple proactive clarification: Implement a clarifying question generator and test its effect on retrieval performance and user satisfaction.
  3. Create multi-modal query interface: Add image/video input capability and measure how it changes user behavior and information need expression.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How can we effectively evaluate the quality and usefulness of proactive interactions in generative IR systems?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper discusses evaluation challenges for proactive systems, mentioning both modular and end-to-end evaluation approaches but noting significant challenges in both.
- Why unresolved: The paper states that evaluating proactive interactions is challenging because traditional evaluation methods based on predefined needs or observations don't readily apply. It mentions two evaluation approaches but doesn't provide concrete solutions.
- What evidence would resolve it: Development and validation of new evaluation metrics specifically designed for proactive interactions, including both offline and online evaluation strategies with concrete examples and case studies.

### Open Question 2
- Question: What are the optimal moments and contexts for initiating proactive interactions in generative IR systems?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper identifies this as a key technical challenge, stating "the primary challenge in this component stems from our limited understanding of the optimal moment to initiate a conversation."
- Why unresolved: While the paper discusses various purposes for proactive interactions, it doesn't provide concrete guidelines or models for determining when to initiate them.
- What evidence would resolve it: User studies that identify patterns in optimal conversation initiation timing, machine learning models that predict effective initiation moments, and empirical validation of these approaches.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How can we effectively interpret and utilize implicit feedback in generative IR systems?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper highlights that "Research in understanding and modeling implicit (negative) feedback is relatively sparse and future technologies can greatly benefit from further research in this space."
- Why unresolved: The paper notes that implicit feedback in generative IR systems is more challenging than in traditional search due to higher abandonment rates and different user behaviors.
- What evidence would resolve it: Development of models that can effectively interpret implicit feedback signals, validation of these models through user studies, and demonstration of their impact on system performance.

## Limitations
- The work is primarily theoretical and conceptual with limited empirical validation of proposed interaction mechanisms
- Discussion of multi-modal interactions and proactive systems remains largely speculative with few concrete implementation details
- Limited evidence provided for how well current systems actually support all four levels of Taylor's information need model

## Confidence

- **High Confidence**: The characterization of current interaction patterns (feedback, refinement, clarification) and their technical challenges is well-grounded in existing literature.
- **Medium Confidence**: The application of Taylor's information need model to generative IR is conceptually sound but lacks empirical validation of how well current systems actually support all four levels.
- **Low Confidence**: The proposed proactive interaction framework and multi-modal processing mechanisms are forward-looking but lack concrete implementation evidence or evaluation metrics.
