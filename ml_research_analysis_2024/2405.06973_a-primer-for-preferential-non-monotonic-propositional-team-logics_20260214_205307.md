# A Primer for Preferential Non-Monotonic Propositional Team Logics

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2405.06973
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.06973
- Authors: Kai Sauerwald; Juha Kontinen
- Reference count: 9
- Primary result: Establishes framework for preferential non-monotonic reasoning in propositional team semantics

## Executive Summary
This paper develops a foundational framework for preferential non-monotonic reasoning within propositional team semantics. The authors demonstrate that team-based propositional logics naturally induce cumulative non-monotonic entailment relations, providing a systematic approach to understanding non-monotonic reasoning in this context. The work precisely characterizes preferential models for propositional dependence logic that satisfy System P axioms, showing how non-trivial preferential models can express both classical entailment and dependence logic entailment.

A significant finding is that preferential team logics satisfying System P are fundamentally equivalent to their flat counterparts in classical propositional logic. This equivalence suggests that System P's (Or) rule is too restrictive for non-monotonic team logics, limiting their expressive power. The paper also reveals a divergence between preferential model-based and axiomatic characterizations of non-monotonic inference in team semantics, opening new avenues for understanding the relationship between different approaches to non-monotonic reasoning.

## Method Summary
The authors employ a systematic approach to establishing preferential non-monotonic reasoning in propositional team semantics. They begin by defining preferential models for propositional dependence logic and examining their properties under System P axioms. Through careful analysis of the relationship between preferential models and classical entailment, they demonstrate the equivalence between preferential and flat team logics. The methodology involves rigorous mathematical proofs and counterexamples to establish the limitations of System P for team-based non-monotonic logics.

## Key Results
- Team-based propositional logics naturally induce cumulative non-monotonic entailment relations
- Preferential team logics satisfying System P are equivalent to flat counterparts in classical propositional logic
- A divergence exists between preferential model-based and axiomatic characterizations of non-monotonic inference in team semantics

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The mechanism underlying this work rests on the careful construction of preferential models that capture the semantics of team-based logics. By imposing System P axioms on these models, the authors establish a framework where non-monotonic reasoning can be systematically analyzed. The equivalence between preferential and flat team logics emerges from the constraints imposed by System P's (Or) rule, which effectively reduces the complexity of team-based reasoning to classical propositional logic.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component map**: Preferential models -> System P satisfaction -> Non-monotonic entailment -> Equivalence to flat logic

**Critical path**: Define preferential models → Apply System P axioms → Establish entailment relation → Prove equivalence to classical logic

**Design tradeoffs**: 
- Using System P provides well-understood axiomatic properties but may be too restrictive
- Team semantics allows multi-assignment reasoning but is constrained by the (Or) rule
- Preferential models enable non-monotonic reasoning but may not capture all team-based patterns

**Failure signatures**: 
- When System P axioms fail to capture intended non-monotonic patterns
- When team semantics cannot express certain dependencies
- When preferential models cannot distinguish between different non-monotonic behaviors

**3 first experiments**:
1. Verify that preferential models satisfying System P axioms indeed produce cumulative non-monotonic entailment
2. Test the equivalence claim by comparing preferential and flat team logic entailments on benchmark problems

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
The paper identifies several open questions, including whether alternative axiom systems beyond System P can better characterize non-monotonic reasoning in team semantics, and how the divergence between preferential model-based and axiomatic characterizations affects practical implementations of these logics.

## Limitations
- The equivalence between preferential and flat team logics may limit expressive power for complex non-monotonic patterns
- System P's (Or) rule appears too restrictive, but alternative axiom systems remain unexplored
- The practical implications of the divergence between model-based and axiomatic characterizations are unclear
