# IC3M: In-Car Multimodal Multi-object Monitoring for Abnormal Status of Both Driver and Passengers

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2410.02592
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.02592
- Reference count: 40

## Executive Summary
This paper proposes IC3M, an in-car multimodal monitoring framework that simultaneously tracks abnormal status of both drivers and passengers. The framework addresses two key challenges: limited labeled data with class imbalance, and missing modalities due to environmental constraints like rotating camera setups. IC3M introduces adaptive threshold pseudo-labeling to generate balanced pseudo labels and incorporates low-confidence data via contrastive learning. Additionally, it employs a missing modality reconstruction module that restores lost modality features through a meta-learning framework. Experiments on Stressors and Drive&Act datasets demonstrate superior performance over state-of-the-art baselines.

## Method Summary
IC3M addresses the dual challenges of limited labeled data and missing modalities in in-car monitoring through two core modules. The adaptive threshold pseudo-labeling scheme dynamically adjusts classification thresholds based on class distribution to generate balanced pseudo labels, while incorporating low-confidence samples using contrastive learning. The missing modality reconstruction module restores lost modality features by learning cross-modality distribution mappings through meta-learning. This approach enables effective monitoring of both driver and passenger abnormal states despite data limitations and sensor constraints.

## Key Results
- Achieves up to 75.37% accuracy and 89.13% precision on Stressors dataset
- Demonstrates superior performance compared to state-of-the-art baselines
- Shows fast convergence and robustness under severe modality loss conditions
- Validated on two public datasets: Stressors and Drive&Act

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The method works by addressing the fundamental challenges of in-car monitoring through intelligent data augmentation and feature recovery. The adaptive threshold pseudo-labeling compensates for class imbalance by dynamically adjusting confidence thresholds, ensuring that minority classes receive adequate representation in the training process. The contrastive learning component leverages low-confidence samples that would otherwise be discarded, extracting useful information from uncertain predictions. The meta-learning-based missing modality reconstruction learns robust cross-modal mappings that can effectively restore lost features, making the system resilient to sensor failures or environmental constraints.

## Limitations
- Performance shows variability across datasets (75.37% accuracy on Stressors vs 70.28% on Drive&Act), suggesting potential dataset-specific constraints
- Claims about "fast convergence" lack quantitative comparison against baselines
- Adaptive threshold pseudo-labeling may be sensitive to hyperparameter tuning
- Meta-learning framework's effectiveness with multiple simultaneous modality losses not thoroughly analyzed

## Confidence
- High: Core methodology for pseudo-labeling and missing modality reconstruction is technically sound
- Medium: Performance improvements over baselines are demonstrated but may be partially dataset-dependent
- Low: Claims about generalization to diverse in-car environments and severe modality loss scenarios
