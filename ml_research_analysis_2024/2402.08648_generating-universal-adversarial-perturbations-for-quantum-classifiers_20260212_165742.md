# Generating Universal Adversarial Perturbations for Quantum Classifiers

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2402.08648
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.08648
- Authors: Gautham Anil; Vishnu Vinod; Apurva Narayan
- Reference count: 19
- Primary result: Introduces QuGAP framework generating universal adversarial perturbations (UAPs) for quantum classifiers with state-of-the-art misclassification rates

## Executive Summary
This work introduces QuGAP, a framework for generating Universal Adversarial Perturbations targeting quantum classifiers. It presents two attack strategies: QuGAP-A for additive UAPs in the classical domain (applied before amplitude encoding) and QuGAP-U for unitary UAPs acting directly on quantum states. The paper theoretically proves the existence of additive UAPs for quantum classifiers under amplitude encoding and provides a generative model-based method for their creation. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed framework achieves state-of-the-art misclassification rates while maintaining high fidelity between legitimate and adversarial samples.

## Method Summary
The QuGAP framework introduces two approaches for generating universal adversarial perturbations against quantum classifiers. QuGAP-A uses classical generative models to produce additive perturbations applied in the classical domain before amplitude encoding, while QuGAP-U employs quantum generative models (PQCs) to generate unitary perturbations acting directly on quantum states. Both methods incorporate fidelity constraints to control perturbation strength. The framework is evaluated on various datasets including MNIST and a synthetic Transverse-field Ising Model dataset, demonstrating effectiveness across different quantum classifier architectures.

## Key Results
- Theoretically proves existence of additive UAPs for quantum classifiers under amplitude encoding
- Achieves state-of-the-art misclassification rates while maintaining high fidelity between legitimate and adversarial samples
- Introduces novel loss function combining fooling loss with fidelity constraints for unitary UAPs
- Demonstrates vulnerability of quantum classifiers to adversarial attacks

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- **Claim:** Additive UAPs exist for quantum classifiers because normalization in amplitude encoding maps perturbed vectors into new decision regions.
- **Mechanism:** Adding a large perturbation δ to a normalized input x (yielding x+δ), then re-normalizing to get y, shifts the amplitude encoding such that the classifier's decision boundary is crossed. Lemma 2 provides a bound on the change in classification probability when ∥x-y∥ is small.
- **Core assumption:** The quantum classifier uses amplitude encoding and the decision boundary is non-trivial in the normalized space.
- **Evidence anchors:** [abstract]: "theoretically proves the existence of additive UAPs for quantum classifiers under amplitude encoding"; [section 4.1]: Lemmas 2 and 3 formalize the perturbation-to-decision mapping; Theorem 1 gives sufficient condition for misclassification.

### Mechanism 2
- **Claim:** Unitary UAPs can be learned by optimizing a PQC generator to minimize fooling loss while constraining fidelity.
- **Mechanism:** The generator GQ maps |ψ⟩→|ϕ⟩=UGQ|ψ⟩; the total loss LU = Lfool + αLfid balances misclassification rate and closeness (fidelity). Larger α keeps |ϕ⟩ near |ψ⟩, smaller α increases misclassification.
- **Core assumption:** The generator's expressivity (number of layers/parameters) is sufficient to span useful unitary transformations.
- **Evidence anchors:** [abstract]: "introduce a novel loss function combining fooling loss with fidelity constraints"; [section 5.2]: Loss formulation and parameter-shift gradient computation; depth requirement O(d²) gates.

### Mechanism 3
- **Claim:** The proposed classical generator (QuGAP-A) can produce effective additive perturbations without needing quantum resources at inference.
- **Mechanism:** A classical neural network G(z) learns to map random noise z to perturbations; backpropagation through a simulated quantum classifier updates G. After training, G can generate perturbations offline.
- **Core assumption:** The quantum classifier's unitary U is known or can be simulated classically for gradient computation.
- **Evidence anchors:** [abstract]: "utilize generative models (QuGAP-A) to craft additive UAPs"; [section 4.2]: Training loop with fooling loss Lfool and classical backpropagation; simulation of U.

## Foundational Learning

- **Concept:** Amplitude encoding of classical data into quantum states.
  - Why needed here: The theoretical existence proofs and QuGAP-A both rely on the specific properties of amplitude encoding (normalization, real-valued amplitudes).
  - Quick check question: How is a d-dimensional real vector x normalized and encoded into a quantum state? (Answer: |ψx⟩ = Σ x_i/√Σx_j² |i⟩)

- **Concept:** Parametrized Quantum Circuits (PQCs) as quantum classifiers.
  - Why needed here: All experiments use PQCs with rotation + entanglement layers; understanding their structure is essential for designing generators and interpreting results.
  - Quick check question: What is the effect of setting all PQC parameters to zero? (Answer: Identity transformation, baseline fidelity)

- **Concept:** Fidelity as a distance measure between quantum states.
  - Why needed here: The unitary UAP framework explicitly constrains fidelity to control perturbation strength; correct implementation of fidelity (via SWAP test or closed form for pure states) is critical.
  - Quick check question: How is fidelity F(|ψ⟩,|ϕ⟩) computed for pure states? (Answer: F = |⟨ψ|ϕ⟩|²)

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map:** Target quantum classifier Q (PQCs of varying depth) -> Additive generator G (classical NN) for QuGAP-A / Unitary generator GQ (quantum PQC) for QuGAP-U -> Fidelity/loss modules (fooling loss Lfool, fidelity loss Lfid) -> Classical simulator of quantum circuits (for training, unless on real hardware)

- **Critical path:**
  - QuGAP-A: Sample z → G(z) → scale to L∞ bound → add to x → amplitude encode → Q → compute Lfool → backprop to G
  - QuGAP-U: Sample |ψ⟩ → GQ(|ψ⟩)=|ϕ⟩ → compute Lfool + αLfid → backprop to GQ parameters

- **Design tradeoffs:**
  - QuGAP-A: Generator depth vs. overfitting; L∞ vs. L₂ norm constraints
  - QuGAP-U: Generator depth vs. expressivity; α value vs. fidelity/misclassification balance; real vs. simulated quantum hardware

- **Failure signatures:**
  - QuGAP-A: Generator produces perturbations that are clipped to zero or fail to fool Q; high variance across runs
  - QuGAP-U: Misclassification plateaus below 100%; fidelity drops sharply as depth increases (insufficient expressivity)

- **First 3 experiments:**
  1. Train a depth-10 PQC on 16×16 binary MNIST, verify >95% accuracy
  2. Run QuGAP-A with L∞ bound 0.1 on the trained PQC, check misclassification >50%
  3. Train QuGAP-U on 8×8 MNIST with fidelity constraint 0.9, compare misclassification to baseline qBIM

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- **Question:** What is the theoretical upper bound on misclassification rates achievable by additive UAPs for quantum classifiers under amplitude encoding?
- **Basis in paper:** [explicit] The paper theoretically proves the existence of additive UAPs and provides conditions under which they can cause misclassification, but does not establish a definitive upper bound on achievable misclassification rates.
- **Why unresolved:** The paper focuses on proving existence conditions and generating methods rather than establishing performance limits. The effectiveness depends on dataset characteristics and perturbation strength in ways that are not fully characterized.
- **What evidence would resolve it:** Theoretical analysis deriving maximum achievable misclassification rates as a function of dataset properties, perturbation constraints, and classifier architecture would establish definitive bounds.

### Open Question 2
- **Question:** How do quantum noise and hardware limitations affect the effectiveness of unitary UAPs generated by QuGAP-U in real quantum computing environments?
- **Basis in paper:** [explicit] The paper notes that while the framework is designed with practical implementation considerations, all experiments are limited to simulations and real-world implementation remains unexplored.
- **Why unresolved:** The paper demonstrates effectiveness in ideal simulation conditions but does not test the approach on actual quantum hardware where noise and decoherence would be present.
- **What evidence would resolve it:** Experimental validation of QuGAP-U-generated attacks on real quantum processors across different noise levels and hardware platforms would quantify the impact of realistic conditions.

### Open Question 3
- **Question:** What defense mechanisms are most effective against additive and unitary UAPs in quantum classifiers?
- **Basis in paper:** [explicit] The paper concludes by noting that studying and mitigating adversarial attacks is necessary, and mentions that defense strategies could be centered around the two types of UAPs conceptualized, but does not propose or evaluate any specific defenses.
- **Why unresolved:** The paper focuses entirely on attack generation and effectiveness without exploring defensive countermeasures or their relative performance.
- **What evidence would resolve it:** Systematic evaluation of various defense strategies (e.g., adversarial training, noise injection, architectural modifications) against both additive and unitary UAPs would identify the most effective approaches.

## Limitations
- Theoretical guarantees for additive UAPs rely heavily on amplitude encoding properties that may not generalize to other encoding schemes
- All experimental validation is limited to classical simulations without real quantum hardware demonstrations
- The depth requirements for unitary generators (O(d²) gates) may be prohibitive for high-dimensional datasets on near-term quantum devices

## Confidence
- **High confidence**: The mathematical framework for defining and optimizing UAPs (both additive and unitary variants) is well-established and rigorously formulated. The theoretical proofs for additive UAP existence under amplitude encoding are sound.
- **Medium confidence**: The experimental results showing effectiveness of both QuGAP-A and QuGAP-U approaches, though limited in scale and hardware. The framework's vulnerability claims are supported but could benefit from broader testing.
- **Low confidence**: The practical feasibility of unitary UAP generation on real quantum hardware, given the depth requirements and current NISQ constraints.

## Next Checks
1. **Hardware validation**: Implement QuGAP-U on actual quantum processors (e.g., superconducting or trapped-ion systems) for small-scale datasets to verify theoretical predictions about unitary UAP effectiveness.
2. **Encoding generalization**: Test the additive UAP framework with alternative encoding schemes (angle/phase encoding) to determine if the theoretical guarantees extend beyond amplitude encoding.
3. **Scalability analysis**: Evaluate both frameworks on larger quantum classifiers (20+ layers) and higher-dimensional datasets to assess practical limitations and identify performance bottlenecks.
