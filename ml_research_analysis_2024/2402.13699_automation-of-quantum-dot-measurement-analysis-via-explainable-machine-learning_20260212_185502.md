# Automation of Quantum Dot Measurement Analysis via Explainable Machine Learning

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2402.13699
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.13699
- Authors: Daniel Schug; Tyler J. Kovach; M. A. Wolfe; Jared Benson; Sanghyeok Park; J. P. Dodson; J. Corrigan; M. A. Eriksson; Justyna P. Zwolak
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: EBM models achieve 92.5% accuracy (Gabor filterbank) and 90.5% accuracy (synthetic data modeling) in classifying good vs. bad quantum dot triangle plots

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of automating quantum dot (QD) device tuning for quantum computing applications by analyzing triangle plot images that indicate proper current channel formation. The authors propose using explainable boosting machines (EBMs) with two vectorization approaches - Gabor filterbank and synthetic data modeling - to classify good vs. bad triangle plots while maintaining interpretability. The core contribution is demonstrating that EBMs can achieve high accuracy while providing insights into which features drive predictions, enabling experimentalists to understand and adjust device parameters based on model outputs.

## Method Summary
The method involves converting 64×64 pixel triangle plot images into feature vectors using two approaches: Gabor filterbank (6 filters at specific scales/orientations capturing line-like features at ±45°) and synthetic data modeling (mathematical modeling of triangle regions using sigmoid functions). These features are then fed into EBM classifiers for training with five six-fold stratified cross-validation. The hybrid approach combines the most important Gabor feature with synthetic parameters to balance accuracy (91.7%) and interpretability. The trained models can explain predictions by showing how individual features contribute to classification decisions.

## Key Results
- EBM models achieve 92.5% accuracy using Gabor filterbank vectorization
- Synthetic data modeling approach achieves 90.5% accuracy with superior interpretability
- Hybrid approach combining top Gabor feature with synthetic parameters achieves 91.7% accuracy
- Model predictions provide interpretable features that map back to physical device parameters for experimental guidance

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Gabor filterbank vectorization extracts discriminative features for classifying good vs. bad triangle plots.
- Mechanism: Gabor wavelets at specific scales and orientations (especially ±45°) capture the line-like features in triangle plots corresponding to gate interactions. These oriented filters create compact, interpretable feature vectors that EBMs can process.
- Core assumption: The triangle plot structure contains consistent diagonal patterns at ±45° that correlate with device quality.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "image vectorization approach that relies on the Gabor wavelet transform"
  - [section 2.3] "the main factor allowing the discrimination of good and bad triangles is the presence of the pattern of line-like features at approximately 45° orientation"
  - [corpus] Weak evidence - no direct corpus citations for Gabor effectiveness in this specific domain
- Break condition: If triangle plot patterns vary significantly across devices or if the ±45° orientation assumption fails, the Gabor filters may not capture the relevant features.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: Synthetic triangle modeling creates interpretable features that directly map to physical device parameters.
- Mechanism: Mathematical modeling of synthetic triangles using sigmoid functions approximates experimental data. Optimization finds parameters that best fit real measurements, creating features like boundary locations and magnitudes that correspond to physical gate voltages.
- Core assumption: Synthetic triangle structure sufficiently resembles experimental triangle plots to enable meaningful feature extraction.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "alternative vectorization method that involves mathematical modeling of synthetic triangles to mimic the experimental data"
  - [section 2.5] "The synthetic triangles are used to model the experimental triangle plots by deriving from experimental data parameters defining a most similar synthetic triangle"
  - [corpus] Weak evidence - no direct corpus citations for synthetic modeling in this domain
- Break condition: If experimental triangle plots deviate too much from synthetic model assumptions, the feature extraction will fail to capture meaningful physical parameters.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: EBM models provide both high accuracy and interpretability for quantum dot measurement analysis.
- Mechanism: EBMs extend generalized additive models with pairwise interactions, allowing direct interpretation of feature contributions to predictions. This enables understanding why a triangle plot is classified as good or bad.
- Core assumption: The relationship between triangle plot features and device quality can be modeled as additive functions with limited interactions.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "explainable boosting machines (EBMs) for classification tasks, which offers better interpretability compared to traditional black-box models"
  - [section 1] "EBMs are a glass-box method that enables the model to be directly interpretable rather than relying on surrogate explanations"
  - [corpus] Weak evidence - no direct corpus citations for EBM effectiveness in this specific domain
- Break condition: If the true relationship between features and device quality is highly non-additive or requires many interactions, EBM accuracy will suffer.

- Critical path: Image preprocessing → Vectorization → EBM training → Model evaluation → Feature interpretation

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

- Question: What specific threshold values for synthetic triangle parameters (DB, Dθ, F) reliably distinguish good from bad triangle plots across different quantum dot devices?
  - Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper discusses using synthetic triangle parameters like DB (diagonal boundary component), Dθ (diagonal orientation), and F (fit fitness) for classification, noting their importance in determining good vs. bad plots
  - Why unresolved: The paper shows these features are important discriminators but doesn't provide specific threshold values or ranges that could be universally applied
  - What evidence would resolve it: Systematic testing across multiple quantum dot devices to establish optimal threshold ranges for these parameters

- Question: How does the presence of multiple current channels (as mentioned in the paper's discussion of misclassification cases) affect the reliability of the synthetic triangle fitting approach?
  - Basis in paper: [explicit] The discussion section notes that when multiple channels are present, the ohmic potential can drift dynamically, affecting current distribution
  - Why unresolved: The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't explore how to compensate for or model this multi-channel behavior
  - What evidence would resolve it: Experimental data from devices with varying numbers of current channels to test how the fitting approach performs under these conditions

- Question: Can the sinusoidal term mentioned for capturing the ridged pattern in the triangle region be incorporated into the synthetic triangle model without compromising interpretability?
  - Basis in paper: [explicit] The conclusion suggests this as a potential improvement to better capture the ridged pattern in good triangle plots
  - Why unresolved: The paper doesn't explore this modification or test whether it maintains the interpretability advantages of the current approach
  - What evidence would resolve it: Implementation and testing of the modified model with sinusoidal terms to verify both improved accuracy and maintained interpretability

## Limitations
- The synthetic triangle modeling approach relies on assumptions about triangle plot structure that may not generalize across different quantum dot architectures
- The Gabor filterbank method, while achieving higher accuracy, offers limited interpretability regarding physical device parameters
- The dataset size (902 samples) is relatively small for deep learning approaches, though appropriate for EBM methods

## Confidence
- High confidence: EBM framework's ability to provide interpretable classifications and general methodology of using vectorization approaches
- Medium confidence: Specific accuracy metrics due to limited testing on diverse datasets and assumption of consistent triangle plot patterns
- Low confidence: Long-term generalizability of synthetic modeling approach without validation on broader range of quantum dot architectures
