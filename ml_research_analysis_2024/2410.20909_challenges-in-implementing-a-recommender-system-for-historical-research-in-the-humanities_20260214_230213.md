# Challenges in Implementing a Recommender System for Historical Research in the Humanities

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2410.20909
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.20909
- Reference count: 11
- This paper identifies three key challenges in implementing recommender systems for digital archives in the humanities using Monasterium.net as a case study

## Executive Summary
This paper examines the implementation challenges of recommender systems for historical research in the humanities, focusing on Monasterium.net, a platform for historical legal documents. The authors identify three interconnected challenges: the unique characteristics of historical charters as recommendation items with complex, sparse metadata; a multi-stakeholder environment with diverse and sometimes conflicting objectives; and the distinct exploratory information-seeking behavior of humanities scholars that differs from typical consumer patterns. The paper argues that addressing these challenges requires specialized algorithms, evaluation frameworks that reflect multiple stakeholder values, and interfaces that support long-term, non-linear research processes.

## Method Summary
The paper presents a theoretical analysis of challenges in implementing recommender systems for historical research, using Monasterium.net as a case study. Rather than proposing specific algorithms, the authors analyze the unique characteristics of historical charters, the multi-stakeholder environment of digital humanities platforms, and the exploratory information-seeking behavior of humanities scholars. The analysis draws on literature from recommender systems, digital humanities, and information science to identify fundamental challenges that differ from standard commercial RecSys applications. The authors propose future directions including specialized algorithms for handling sparse metadata, multistakeholder evaluation frameworks, and interfaces supporting exploratory research.

## Key Results
- Historical charters present unique algorithmic challenges due to complex, sparse, and high-dimensional metadata that traditional RecSys struggle to process
- Digital humanities platforms operate in a multi-stakeholder environment where researchers, content creators, platform owners, and funding agencies have diverse and sometimes conflicting objectives
- Humanities scholars engage in exploratory, non-linear information-seeking behavior that requires fundamentally different recommendation approaches than typical consumer RecSys

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: The unique characteristics of historical charters as recommendation items create fundamental algorithmic challenges that differ from standard RecSys applications.
- Mechanism: Historical charters possess complex, sparse, and multi-dimensional metadata that traditional recommendation algorithms struggle to process effectively. The documents span temporal ranges, contain multilingual content, and have varying completeness levels, requiring specialized handling beyond standard collaborative or content-based filtering approaches.
- Core assumption: The metadata complexity and sparsity of historical charters is significantly higher than typical commercial products or entertainment content.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "unique characteristics of so-called charters as recommendation items, which include complex metadata, varying completeness, multilingual content, and temporal considerations"
  - [section 2] "Charters in Monasterium.net are represented as digitized scans, scholarly editions, and semi-structured data using specialized schemas like the Charter Encoding Initiative (CEI) [3]. This representation includes diverse metadata such as material properties, production details, authentication methods, and scholarly annotations. The high dimensionality and often sparse population of this metadata pose significant challenges for traditional recommendation algorithms."
- Break condition: If standard RecSys algorithms can be easily adapted to handle the metadata complexity without significant performance degradation.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: The multi-stakeholder environment creates competing objectives that complicate system design and evaluation.
- Mechanism: Different stakeholders (researchers, content creators, platform owners, funding agencies) have distinct and sometimes conflicting values. The system must balance visibility for content creators, relevance for researchers, engagement metrics for platform owners, and usage metrics for funding agencies. This creates a complex optimization problem where satisfying all stakeholders simultaneously is challenging.
- Core assumption: Stakeholder objectives are genuinely conflicting rather than complementary.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "a multi-stakeholder environment involving researchers, content creators, platform owners, and funding agencies with diverse and sometimes conflicting objectives"
  - [section 3] "Balancing these diverse and sometimes conflicting interests presents a significant challenge. For example, recommendations that prioritize popular or well-documented charters might satisfy casual users but could limit the exposure of lesser-known documents that might be crucial for specialized research."
- Break condition: If stakeholder objectives can be aligned through a single unified metric or if one stakeholder group's needs dominate the others.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Humanities scholars' exploratory information-seeking behavior requires fundamentally different recommendation approaches than typical consumer RecSys.
- Mechanism: Humanities researchers engage in non-linear, iterative research processes where relevance is context-dependent and may only emerge over extended periods. Traditional RecSys assume immediate user feedback and clear relevance signals, but humanities research often involves serendipitous discovery and long-term value assessment that doesn't fit standard evaluation metrics.
- Core assumption: Humanities research processes are genuinely non-linear and context-dependent in ways that invalidate standard RecSys assumptions.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "the distinct exploratory information-seeking behavior of humanities scholars, characterized by non-linear research processes and context-dependent relevance assessments"
  - [section 4] "The information-seeking behavior of humanities scholars differs significantly from the user behavior in commercial RecSys. For instance, historians often engage in exploratory searches, seeking to uncover unexpected connections and generate new research questions [6, 7]. Their research process is typically non-linear, involving iterative refinement of queries and exploration of diverse paths"
- Break condition: If humanities research can be effectively modeled using standard consumer RecSys patterns with minor modifications.

- Critical path: Charter document ingestion → Metadata extraction and processing → Feature engineering for recommendation → Algorithm execution → Stakeholder preference integration → Result presentation → User feedback collection → Long-term relevance assessment

- Failure signatures: Recommendations that are technically relevant but contextually inappropriate for humanities research; algorithms that perform well on standard metrics but fail to surface unexpected connections valued by historians; systems that optimize for one stakeholder group at the expense of others; interfaces that don't support exploratory browsing patterns

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- Question: How can recommender systems be designed to effectively handle the complex, sparse, and high-dimensional metadata associated with historical charters?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper discusses the challenges of traditional recommendation algorithms in handling the high dimensionality and sparse population of charter metadata.
- Why unresolved: Current recommendation algorithms are primarily designed for dense, well-structured data, and there is a lack of specialized approaches for handling the unique characteristics of historical charter metadata.
- What evidence would resolve it: Development and evaluation of novel recommendation algorithms specifically tailored to handle sparse, high-dimensional metadata from historical documents, demonstrating improved performance over traditional approaches.

### Open Question 2
- Question: What evaluation frameworks can adequately capture the diverse values and objectives of multiple stakeholders in digital humanities archives?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper highlights the need for evaluation frameworks that reflect diverse stakeholder values, given the complex multi-stakeholder environment in digital archives.
- Why unresolved: Existing evaluation metrics for recommender systems are often designed for single-stakeholder scenarios and may not adequately represent the varied objectives of researchers, content creators, platform owners, and funding agencies.
- What evidence would resolve it: Development and validation of multi-stakeholder evaluation frameworks that balance the diverse objectives of different stakeholders in digital humanities archives.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How can recommender systems be designed to support the exploratory and non-linear information-seeking behavior characteristic of humanities research?
- Basis in paper: [explicit] The paper discusses the distinct exploratory information-seeking behavior of humanities scholars, which differs significantly from user behavior in commercial recommender systems.
- Why unresolved: Current recommender systems are often optimized for task-oriented, linear search patterns and may not adequately support the iterative, exploratory nature of humanities research.
- What evidence would resolve it: Development and user studies of recommendation interfaces that support exploratory search, serendipity, and long-term research processes in humanities research contexts.

## Limitations
- The paper presents theoretical challenges rather than empirical validation with quantitative performance data
- Analysis is based on observations from a single platform (Monasterium.net) without broader validation across different digital humanities archives
- The authors acknowledge lack of specific algorithm proposals or implementation details for addressing the identified challenges

## Confidence
- High Confidence: The identification of charter metadata complexity as a challenge - well-supported by description of CEI schema and nature of historical documents
- Medium Confidence: The multistakeholder environment challenge - logically sound but lacks concrete examples of conflicts or their severity
- Medium Confidence: The exploratory information-seeking behavior characterization - based on literature references but not validated with actual humanities researchers in this study
