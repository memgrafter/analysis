# Decentralised Variational Inference Frameworks for Multi-object Tracking on Sensor Networks: Additional Notes

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2408.13689
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.13689
- **Reference count**: 32
- **Primary result**: Decentralised VI achieves centralised sensor fusion performance with only local message exchanges among neighbouring sensors

## Executive Summary
This paper proposes decentralised Variational Inference (VI) schemes for multi-sensor multi-object tracking that match centralised sensor fusion performance while significantly reducing communication requirements. The key innovation is the introduction of a locally maximised evidence lower bound (LM-ELBO), which reduces the information needed for communication compared to standard ELBO-based approaches. The framework employs decentralised natural gradient descent with gradient tracking strategy, optimising LM-ELBO instead of ELBO to reduce parameter search space and enable faster convergence.

## Method Summary
The proposed framework addresses multi-object tracking in sensor networks by developing decentralised VI methods that enable sensors to perform local inference while maintaining global tracking accuracy. The approach introduces LM-ELBO as a communication-efficient alternative to standard ELBO, reducing the information exchanged between sensors. The method employs decentralised natural gradient descent with gradient tracking to optimise the LM-ELBO, allowing sensors to converge to solutions equivalent to centralised fusion without sharing raw data. This is achieved through local message exchanges among neighbouring sensors, where each sensor maintains its own local approximation of the global posterior distribution.

## Key Results
- Decentralised natural gradient VI method achieves tracking accuracy empirically equivalent to centralised fusion
- LM-ELBO approach requires significantly less communication compared to consensus-based methods while maintaining comparable costs to suboptimal fusion techniques
- The framework demonstrates robust performance across multiple tracking scenarios with only local message exchanges

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The framework works by leveraging the structure of variational inference in a decentralised setting. By optimising a locally maximised ELBO (LM-ELBO) instead of the global ELBO, sensors can perform inference using only local information while still achieving global optimality. The natural gradient descent with gradient tracking ensures that sensors can coordinate their updates without sharing raw measurements, preserving privacy and reducing communication overhead. The LM-ELBO formulation inherently reduces the parameter search space, leading to faster convergence compared to standard VI approaches.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: Sensors -> Local VI Computation -> LM-ELBO Optimisation -> Natural Gradient Descent -> Gradient Tracking -> Neighbouring Sensors

**Critical Path**: Sensor measurements → Local VI computation → LM-ELBO evaluation → Natural gradient update → Gradient tracking → Neighbour communication → Parameter update

**Design Tradeoffs**: 
- Communication efficiency vs. tracking accuracy (LM-ELBO reduces communication but may introduce approximation errors)
- Local computation vs. global optimality (decentralised approach trades some coordination overhead for scalability)
- Gaussian approximations vs. complex distributions (simplifies computation but may limit expressiveness)

**Failure Signatures**:
- Divergence when communication links fail or become unreliable
- Convergence to suboptimal solutions if initial conditions are poor
- Performance degradation in highly non-linear or non-Gaussian environments

## Limitations
- Assumes reliable local communication between neighbouring sensors, which may not hold in real-world scenarios with intermittent connectivity or bandwidth constraints
- Relies on Gaussian approximations that could introduce systematic biases in highly non-linear dynamics or non-Gaussian measurement noise environments
- Scalability analysis is limited to specific network topologies, with unclear performance boundaries in highly dynamic or heterogeneous sensor networks
