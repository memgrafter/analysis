# Prompt-based Unifying Inference Attack on Graph Neural Networks

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2412.15735
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.15735
- Reference count: 40
- Key outcome: ProIA improves attack accuracy by up to 18.54% over baseline methods

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces ProIA, a prompt-based unifying inference attack framework for graph neural networks that enhances attack capabilities through pre-training and downstream adaptation. The framework retains crucial topological information during pre-training to build background knowledge, then uses unified prompts with disentanglement factors for task-specific attacks. Extensive experiments demonstrate improved attack accuracy and adaptability across various inference attack scenarios.

## Method Summary
ProIA operates through a two-phase approach: first, it pre-trains an attack model on graph data to capture essential topological features and build background knowledge. Second, it employs a unified prompt mechanism with additional disentanglement factors during downstream attacks to adapt to specific attack objectives. This design allows the framework to maintain strong attack performance while being flexible enough to handle different attack scenarios.

## Key Results
- ProIA achieves up to 18.54% improvement in attack accuracy compared to vanilla methods
- The framework demonstrates strong adaptability across various inference attack scenarios
- Experimental results show consistent performance improvements on benchmark datasets (Cora, CiteSeer, PubMed)

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
ProIA's effectiveness stems from its two-stage design that first captures general graph structural knowledge through pre-training, then adapts this knowledge to specific attack objectives using prompts and disentanglement factors. By retaining topological information during pre-training, the model develops robust background knowledge that can be fine-tuned for various downstream attacks. The unified prompt mechanism allows for consistent attack strategies across different scenarios, while disentanglement factors enable task-specific optimization.
