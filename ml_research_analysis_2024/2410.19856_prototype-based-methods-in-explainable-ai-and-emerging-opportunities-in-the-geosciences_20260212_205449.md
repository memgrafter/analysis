## Executive Summary
This review paper explores prototype-based explainable AI (XAI) methods and their potential applications in geosciences. Prototype-based methods are inherently interpretable, using learned prototypical examples from training data to make predictions and provide explanations. The paper categorizes prototype-based XAI literature into three themes: development and visualization of prototypes, types of prototypes, and their use in various learning tasks. Key findings include the development of ProtoPNet for patch-based prototypes, ProtoTree for decision tree integration, and NP-ProtoPNet for negative reasoning. The authors discuss how these methods can be adapted for geoscientific data, which has unique characteristics like temporal components, spatial autocorrelations, and high dimensionality. Potential applications include climate mode variability, extreme event prediction, and multi-spectral imagery classification. The paper also addresses challenges in evaluating the robustness and reliability of prototype-based explanations, emphasizing the importance of domain expert involvement in interpretation and validation.

## Method Summary

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
Prototype-based methods work by learning prototypical examples from training data that represent the key features of different classes. During inference, new inputs are compared to these prototypes, and predictions are made based on similarity to relevant prototypes. This mechanism provides interpretable explanations because users can directly examine which prototypes influenced the prediction and why. The methods leverage similarity measures to quantify the relationship between inputs and prototypes, creating a transparent decision-making process. For geoscientific applications, this approach can capture complex patterns in spatiotemporal data by learning prototypes that represent characteristic features of different phenomena. The interpretability comes from the ability to visualize and understand the prototypical examples that drive predictions, making these methods particularly valuable for scientific domains where understanding the reasoning behind predictions is crucial.
