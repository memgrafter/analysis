# Optimistic Games for Combinatorial Bayesian Optimization with Application to Protein Design

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2409.18582
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.18582
- Authors: Melis Ilayda Bal; Pier Giuseppe Sessa; Mojmir Mutny; Andreas Krause
- Reference count: 40
- One-line primary result: GameOpt achieves sample complexity bounds and faster convergence in protein design through game-theoretical Bayesian optimization

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces GameOpt, a novel game-theoretical approach for Bayesian optimization in combinatorial spaces, specifically applied to protein design. The method addresses the challenge of optimizing expensive-to-evaluate functions over large combinatorial spaces by defining a cooperative game among decision variables, where equilibria are selected as evaluation points. By breaking down the complexity of combinatorial domains into individual decision sets, GameOpt achieves scalability while maintaining theoretical guarantees on sample complexity.

The approach uses Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) acquisition functions to balance exploration and exploitation, validated on four real-world protein datasets. Results show faster convergence and discovery of highly active protein variants compared to baseline methods. The theoretical foundation provides sample complexity bounds, returning ϵ-approximate equilibria after T = Ω(γT ϵ−2) iterations, where γT is the kernel-dependent maximum information gain.

## Method Summary
GameOpt formulates combinatorial Bayesian optimization as a cooperative game among decision variables, where each variable's strategy space represents possible choices (e.g., amino acids at specific positions). The method identifies and evaluates equilibria of this game, which represent promising protein variants. By decomposing the complex combinatorial space into individual decision sets, the approach achieves scalability while maintaining theoretical guarantees. UCB acquisition functions guide the selection of equilibria, balancing exploration of uncertain regions with exploitation of promising areas. The cooperative game formulation allows the method to navigate the high-dimensional protein design space efficiently while providing sample complexity bounds that ensure theoretical performance guarantees.

## Key Results
- Achieves sample complexity bounds of T = Ω(γT ϵ−2) iterations for returning ϵ-approximate equilibria
- Demonstrates faster convergence compared to baseline methods on four real-world protein datasets
- Discovers highly active protein variants through game-based optimization that mimics natural evolution
- Shows scalability by decomposing combinatorial domains into individual decision sets

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
GameOpt works by transforming the combinatorial optimization problem into a cooperative game where decision variables (amino acid positions) interact strategically. The equilibria of this game represent protein variants that balance individual position contributions with overall protein function. By using UCB acquisition functions within this game-theoretic framework, the method systematically explores the protein space while focusing on regions with high potential. The decomposition of the combinatorial space allows the algorithm to handle the exponential growth of possibilities by treating each position independently within the game structure, making the optimization tractable while maintaining theoretical guarantees.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**
GameOpt consists of: Decision Variables (amino acid positions) -> Cooperative Game Formulation -> Equilibrium Selection -> UCB Acquisition -> Evaluation -> Gaussian Process Update -> Repeat

**Critical Path**
The critical path flows from defining the cooperative game structure, selecting equilibria using UCB acquisition, evaluating the selected protein variants, and updating the Gaussian process model. This cycle repeats until convergence or budget exhaustion.

**Design Tradeoffs**
The method trades computational complexity for theoretical guarantees by decomposing the problem into individual decision sets. While this enables scalability, it may miss some global interactions between positions. The choice of cooperative game formulation affects both computational efficiency and solution quality.

**Failure Signatures**
The method may fail when the cooperative game structure poorly represents true protein interactions, when the kernel function fails to capture relevant sequence similarities, or when UCB acquisition becomes too greedy and misses optimal regions. Poor scalability may emerge when the number of decision variables becomes very large.

## Limitations
- Computational scaling challenges for very large combinatorial spaces with many decision variables
- Performance heavily depends on the quality of the cooperative game formulation and equilibrium selection
- Assumes bounded domain and specific kernel functions, potentially limiting applicability to diverse protein design scenarios

## Confidence

**High Confidence**: Theoretical sample complexity bounds (T = Ω(γT ϵ−2)) and empirical validation on four real-world protein datasets are well-supported. Comparison against baselines showing faster convergence and discovery of highly active protein variants is convincing.

**Medium Confidence**: Scalability claims through decomposition into individual decision sets are reasonable but lack extensive validation for extremely large combinatorial spaces. Choice of cooperative game formulation and equilibrium selection mechanisms, while theoretically sound, may have practical limitations not fully explored.

**Low Confidence**: Assumption that UCB acquisition functions always provide optimal exploration-exploitation trade-offs in protein design contexts is not thoroughly validated. Method's performance in scenarios with noisy or incomplete protein activity data is not explicitly addressed.

## Next Checks

1. **Scalability Test**: Validate GameOpt's performance on protein design problems with significantly larger combinatorial spaces (e.g., proteins with more than 50 amino acids) to empirically confirm scalability claims.

2. **Robustness to Noise**: Test the method's performance on protein datasets with varying levels of noise and uncertainty in activity measurements to assess its robustness in real-world scenarios.

3. **Kernel Sensitivity Analysis**: Conduct experiments using different kernel functions (e.g., Matérn, linear) to evaluate how sensitive GameOpt's performance is to the choice of kernel and whether the theoretical bounds hold across different kernels.
