# Talos: A More Effective and Efficient Adversarial Defense for GNN Models Based on the Global Homophily of Graphs

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2406.03833
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.03833
- **Reference count**: 38
- **Primary result**: Talos outperforms state-of-the-art GNN defenses in accuracy and speed while requiring only a single preprocessing step.

## Executive Summary
Talos is a novel adversarial defense method for graph neural networks (GNNs) that leverages global homophily to identify and remove adversarial edges. Unlike existing approaches that focus on local neighborhood similarity or expensive structure learning, Talos computes a global homophily index using the entire graph structure. The method is efficient, requiring only a single preprocessing step, and demonstrates superior performance across multiple datasets and attack types. Experiments show Talos achieves higher accuracy and faster execution compared to state-of-the-art defenses while maintaining robustness under high attack rates.

## Method Summary
Talos preprocesses the graph by computing a global homophily index that captures homophilous information propagated across all edges and orders in the graph. It removes edges that, when eliminated, result in the largest decrease in this index. The method requires two hyperparameters: the number of edges to remove (nr) and a retention rate (α). Talos operates in a single preprocessing step, leaving model training unchanged, which makes it efficient and applicable to large-scale graphs.

## Key Results
- Outperforms state-of-the-art defense approaches (GCN-Jaccard, GCN-SVD, RobustGCN, GNNGuard, Pro-GNN) in node classification accuracy under adversarial attacks
- Requires only a single preprocessing step, imposing minimal computational overhead compared to iterative structure learning methods
- Demonstrates strong robustness under high attack rates (5-25% perturbation) across multiple datasets (Cora, Amazon Photo, Amazon Computers)

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- Claim: Talos improves GNN robustness by focusing on global homophily instead of local neighborhood similarity.
- Mechanism: The method computes a global homophily index that captures the total amount of homophilous information propagated across all edges and all orders in the graph. It then removes edges that, when eliminated, result in the largest decrease in this index.
- Core assumption: Adversarial edges carry less homophilous information globally than clean edges, so removing those with the largest negative impact on global homophily will remove most adversarial edges.

  - [section] "Talos preprocesses the graph by enhancing its homophily and leaves the model training unchanged, making it more efficient and applicable to large-scale graphs."
  - [corpus] "No direct evidence in corpus comparing Talos to other global methods; related works focus on local or model-based defenses."
- Break condition: If adversarial edges are strategically placed to preserve or enhance global homophily, the index will not effectively identify them.

### Mechanism 2
- Claim: By removing edges with the greatest negative impact on the global homophily index, Talos effectively eliminates most adversarial perturbations.
- Mechanism: For each edge, Talos computes the change in the global homophily index (∆Hom) if that edge were removed. The edges with the largest negative ∆Hom are removed, as their removal most improves global homophily.
- Core assumption: The adversarial edges added by attackers are typically between dissimilar nodes and thus have a large negative impact on global homophily.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "Talos computes a global homophily index using the entire graph structure to identify and remove adversarial edges."
  - [section] "Talos preprocesses the graph by enhancing its homophily and leaves the model training unchanged."
  - [corpus] "No direct experimental evidence in corpus on ∆Hom metric's ability to isolate adversarial edges; relies on theoretical derivation."
- Break condition: If attackers can add edges that maintain high global homophily (e.g., by mimicking homophilous patterns), Talos will not distinguish them from clean edges.

### Mechanism 3
- Claim: Talos's efficiency comes from computing the global homophily index once and using approximations to rank edges for removal.
- Mechanism: Talos uses the formula M = (I - αA)^(-1) to compute the global homophily matrix once, then ranks edges by the value of M'JM at their positions. Approximations allow fast identification of the top n edges to remove without recomputing for each edge.
- Core assumption: The change in global homophily from removing an edge can be approximated by its contribution to M'JM without full recomputation.
- Evidence anchors:
  - [abstract] "Talos is efficient, requiring only a single preprocessing step."
  - [section] "Talos preprocesses the graph only once and can be computed efficiently."
  - [corpus] "No corpus evidence on runtime or memory scaling; related methods mention computational cost but not Talos's specific optimizations."
- Break condition: If the graph is extremely large or sparse, the matrix operations required for M'JM may become computationally prohibitive.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
  Input -> Jaccard similarity matrix -> Global homophily matrix M'JM -> Edge ranking -> Cleaned graph

- Critical path:
  1. Compute Jaccard similarity matrix J from feature matrix X
  2. Compute M = (I - αA)^(-1) (global homophily operator)
  3. Compute M'JM (edge importance scores)
  4. Select and remove n edges with smallest scores
  5. Return cleaned graph for training

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Removing too few edges leaves many adversarial edges; removing too many may destroy true graph structure.
  - Higher α in M = (I - αA)^(-1) increases sensitivity to long-range homophily but may amplify noise.
  - Single preprocessing step is fast but may miss adaptive attacks that target global structure.

- Failure signatures:
  - Low classification accuracy after defense: may indicate over-purification or ineffective edge selection.
  - High runtime on large graphs: may indicate need to optimize matrix operations or reduce α.
  - No improvement over baseline: may indicate low homophily in the graph or highly adaptive attacks.

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Run Talos on a small synthetic graph with known adversarial edges; verify that edges between dissimilar nodes are removed.
  2. Compare Talos's edge removal pattern to GCN-Jaccard on a benchmark dataset; check if Talos removes more adversarial edges.
  3. Measure runtime and memory usage of Talos on graphs of increasing size; identify scaling bottlenecks.

## Limitations
- Performance degradation on graphs with weak homophily, as Talos's defense mechanism relies on global homophily
- No empirical evidence provided on computational efficiency for large-scale graphs beyond the tested datasets
- Lack of testing against adaptive attacks that specifically target Talos's global homophily-based defense strategy

## Confidence
- **High confidence**: The theoretical framework and formula for computing the global homophily index (M'JM) are clearly specified and mathematically sound.
- **Medium confidence**: Experimental results show improved accuracy and efficiency over baselines, but limited ablation studies leave some uncertainty about the relative contribution of each component.
- **Low confidence**: Claims about computational efficiency on large graphs and robustness against adaptive attacks are not empirically supported in the provided evidence.
