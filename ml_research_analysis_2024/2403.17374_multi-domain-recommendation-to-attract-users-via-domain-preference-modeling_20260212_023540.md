# Multi-Domain Recommendation to Attract Users via Domain Preference Modeling

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2403.17374
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.17374
- Authors: Hyunjun Ju; SeongKu Kang; Dongha Lee; Junyoung Hwang; Sanghwan Jang; Hwanjo Yu
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: Addresses Multi-Domain Recommendation to Attract Users (MDRAU) by modeling domain and item-level preferences, achieving up to 32.66% improvement in Recall@20 and 34.80% improvement in NDCG@20 for MDRAU-MT compared to best baseline

## Executive Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of Multi-Domain Recommendation to Attract Users (MDRAU), where the goal is to recommend items from multiple unseen domains using knowledge from seen domains. The key innovation lies in modeling user preferences at both domain and item levels while learning various seen-unseen domain mappings in a unified way. The proposed DRIP framework tackles the core challenges of numerous possible domain mappings and the need to capture user preferences across different domains.

The DRIP framework introduces masked domain modeling to effectively transfer knowledge between seen and unseen domains. Through extensive experiments on real-world datasets, DRIP demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in scenarios with multiple target domains (MDRAU-MT), showing improvements of up to 32.66% in Recall@20 and 34.80% in NDCG@20.

## Method Summary
The paper proposes DRIP, a framework that addresses MDRAU by modeling user preferences at both domain and item levels. The key approach involves using masked domain modeling to learn various seen-unseen domain mappings in a unified manner. DRIP captures user preferences through dual-level modeling - first at the domain level to understand user interests across different domains, and then at the item level within each domain. This approach allows the model to leverage knowledge from seen domains to make recommendations in unseen domains by identifying patterns and similarities in user preferences across domain boundaries.

## Key Results
- DRIP achieves up to 32.66% improvement in Recall@20 for MDRAU-MT compared to best baseline
- DRIP achieves up to 34.80% improvement in NDCG@20 for MDRAU-MT compared to best baseline
- Significant performance gains demonstrated across multiple real-world datasets

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The effectiveness of DRIP stems from its ability to model user preferences at multiple granularities simultaneously. By capturing both domain-level and item-level preferences, the model can identify cross-domain patterns that traditional single-level approaches might miss. The masked domain modeling technique allows the framework to learn transferable knowledge between domains by treating domain information as a latent variable that can be predicted and leveraged for recommendations.

- **Multi-domain knowledge transfer**: Critical for MDRAU scenarios; quick check: measure performance improvement when transferring knowledge from multiple seen domains
- **Unified domain mapping learning**: Necessary to handle numerous possible domain mappings; quick check: test on different combinations of seen-unseen domain pairs

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map**: User Embedding -> Domain Preference Modeling -> Item Preference Modeling -> Masked Domain Modeling -> Recommendation Output

**Critical Path**: The critical path involves first generating user embeddings, then applying domain preference modeling to capture cross-domain interests, followed by item preference modeling within domains, and finally using masked domain modeling to transfer knowledge between seen and unseen domains for final recommendations.

**Design Tradeoffs**: The dual-level preference modeling increases model complexity but enables more accurate cross-domain recommendations. The masked domain modeling approach trades off some training efficiency for better generalization to unseen domains.

**Failure Signatures**: Poor performance may manifest as: 1) inability to transfer knowledge effectively between dissimilar domains, 2) overfitting to seen domains, or 3) failure to capture nuanced item-level preferences within domains.

**First Experiments**: 1) Ablation study removing domain-level preference modeling, 2) Testing on domains with varying similarity levels, 3) Performance comparison with increasing numbers of seen domains

## Limitations
- May not generalize well to domains with very different characteristics or user behavior patterns
- Complexity of dual-level modeling may become a bottleneck for larger, more varied datasets
- Masked domain modeling may struggle with domains that have sparse interactions

## Confidence

**High Confidence**: Experimental results showing DRIP's effectiveness over baseline methods, particularly the reported improvements in Recall@20 and NDCG@20

**Medium Confidence**: Claim that DRIP can effectively model user preferences at both domain and item levels, though generalizability to domains with different characteristics is less certain

**Low Confidence**: Assumption that numerous possible seen-unseen domain mappings can be learned in a unified way using masked domain modeling

## Next Checks
