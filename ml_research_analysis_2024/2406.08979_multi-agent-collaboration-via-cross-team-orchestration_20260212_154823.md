# Multi-Agent Collaboration via Cross-Team Orchestration

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2406.08979
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.08979
- Reference count: 9
- One-line primary result: Multi-team collaboration improves software task quality from 0.779 to 0.840 and executability from 0.813 to 0.928

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces Cross-Team Orchestration (Croto), a multi-team framework that enables autonomous agents to collaboratively generate solutions by allowing different teams to propose and refine task-oriented outcomes through structured interactions. By leveraging diversity across teams and introducing mechanisms like greedy pruning and hierarchical partitioning, the framework effectively synthesizes multiple decision paths into superior solutions. Experiments on software development tasks show significant improvements in quality and executability compared to state-of-the-art baselines.

## Method Summary
The Croto framework orchestrates multiple independent teams working on the same task, each team executing phases of work until key interaction points trigger cross-team collaboration. Solutions are extracted, pruned for quality, partitioned hierarchically, and aggregated through iterative refinement. The framework uses temperature variation across teams to maintain solution diversity, while greedy pruning and hierarchical partitioning manage aggregation complexity. The aggregated solution is then disseminated back to all teams for continued execution.

## Key Results
- Software development quality improved from 0.779 to 0.840 compared to single-team approaches
- Executability of solutions increased from 0.813 to 0.928, indicating more practical outcomes
- Framework demonstrates generalization to story generation tasks with similar performance gains

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
Multi-team collaboration enables exploration of diverse decision paths and reduces the risk of repetitive errors. By running multiple teams in parallel with different configurations, the framework captures a broader solution space. Key phases trigger cross-team interactions where teams exchange insights, allowing mutual correction and refinement. If interactions are too frequent or poorly timed, teams may converge prematurely, reducing diversity and negating the benefit of multiple parallel paths.

### Mechanism 2
Greedy pruning and hierarchical partitioning improve scalability and solution quality by managing aggregation complexity. Pruning removes low-quality solutions before aggregation, reducing the burden on the aggregate agent. Hierarchical partitioning groups solutions into manageable subsets, enabling iterative refinement rather than a single overwhelming aggregation step. If pruning is too aggressive, potentially valuable but initially weak solutions may be discarded before they can be improved through interaction.

### Mechanism 3
Temperature variation across teams creates diversity in solution generation, enhancing the pool of ideas for aggregation. Different temperature settings allow teams to balance creativity and compliance differently, producing solutions with varying degrees of novelty and requirement adherence. Cross-team interactions then integrate the best aspects of both creative and compliant approaches. If temperature settings are poorly calibrated, teams may generate either too much noise or too little innovation, reducing the value of cross-team interactions.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map:
  Multiple independent teams (each a chain of phases) -> Key phase detectors -> Solution extraction and grouping -> Greedy pruning module -> Hierarchical partitioning module -> Role-assigned aggregate agent -> Dissemination module

- Critical path:
  1. Initialize multiple teams with same task
  2. Execute phases until a key phase is reached
  3. Extract and group solutions from all teams
  4. Apply greedy pruning to remove low-quality solutions
  5. Partition solutions hierarchically
  6. Iteratively aggregate solutions within groups
  7. Disseminate final superior solution to all teams
  8. Resume execution with updated solutions

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Number of teams vs. aggregation complexity: More teams increase diversity but also increase the burden on the aggregation process
  - Pruning aggressiveness vs. solution diversity: Aggressive pruning improves efficiency but risks discarding potentially valuable ideas
  - Partitioning granularity vs. aggregation effectiveness: Finer partitions make aggregation easier but may slow down the overall process
  - Temperature variation vs. solution coherence: Higher variation increases diversity but may produce less immediately usable solutions

- Failure signatures:
  - Teams converge to similar solutions (lack of diversity)
  - Aggregation produces incoherent or low-quality results (pruning or partitioning misconfiguration)
  - Key phases are missed or too frequent (poor timing of interactions)
  - Solutions degrade after cross-team interaction (aggregation agent fails to properly synthesize features)

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Baseline single-team vs. 2-team Croto on a simple software task to verify basic cross-team interaction improves quality
  2. Vary temperature configurations (uniform vs. balanced) with 4 teams to test the impact of diversity on solution quality
  3. Test hierarchical partitioning with different group sizes (2, 4, 8) on an 8-team configuration to find optimal aggregation granularity

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
- How does the framework handle task-specific customization requirements when scaling to new domains beyond software development and story generation?
- What is the optimal balance between maintaining team independence and enabling cross-team collaboration to maximize solution quality?
- How does the greedy pruning mechanism affect the diversity of solutions, and what is the optimal pruning threshold?

## Limitations
- The paper lacks precise details on the greedy pruning mechanism's evaluation criteria for solution quality
- Temperature parameter configurations for balancing creativity and compliance across teams are not specified
- The criteria for detecting "key phases" where cross-team interactions should occur remain underspecified

## Confidence
- **High confidence**: The overall framework architecture and its core mechanisms (hierarchical partitioning, cross-team aggregation) are well-described and logically sound
- **Medium confidence**: The experimental results showing improvements over baselines are promising, but the lack of implementation details limits full verification
- **Medium confidence**: The claim about generalization to story generation tasks is supported by results, but the comparison is limited to a single domain

## Next Checks
1. Reproduce the temperature sensitivity experiments by systematically varying team temperature configurations (uniform, balanced, random) and measuring solution quality to confirm the optimal diversity sweet spot
2. Implement ablation studies that remove individual components (pruning, partitioning, temperature variation) to quantify each mechanism's contribution to the overall performance gains
3. Test key phase detection by comparing performance when interactions occur at different frequencies (every phase vs. every 2-3 phases) to determine optimal timing for cross-team communication
