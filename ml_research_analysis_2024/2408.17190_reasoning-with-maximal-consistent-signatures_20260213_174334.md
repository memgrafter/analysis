# Reasoning with maximal consistent signatures

## Quick Facts
- **arXiv ID**: 2408.17190
- **Source URL**: https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.17190
- **Reference count**: 26
- **Key outcome**: Proposes reasoning with maximal consistent subsignatures to handle inconsistency-tolerant reasoning, showing hitting set duality with minimal inconsistent subsignatures and satisfying rationality postulates.

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach to inconsistency-tolerant reasoning based on maximal consistent subsignatures. The framework defines maximal consistent subsignatures as maximal sets of propositions whose forgetting restores consistency to a knowledge base. The authors establish that minimal inconsistent and maximal consistent subsignatures obey the hitting set duality, define inference relations that satisfy key rationality postulates from non-monotonic reasoning, and analyze the computational complexity of reasoning tasks. The work connects to paraconsistent logics and inconsistency measurement while providing a structured framework for handling inconsistent knowledge bases.

## Method Summary
The approach centers on defining forgetting operators and maximal consistent subsignatures as a way to handle inconsistency in knowledge bases. The method involves identifying maximal subsets of propositions whose projection (via forgetting) yields a consistent knowledge base, then defining inference relations based on these subsignatures. The framework is analyzed for its theoretical properties including hitting set duality, satisfaction of rationality postulates, and computational complexity. Two specific forgetting operators (⊟ve and ⊟na) are examined in detail, with the analysis showing that reasoning with naive forgetting coincides with Priest's paraconsistent 3-valued logic.

## Key Results
- Minimal inconsistent and maximal consistent subsignatures obey the hitting set duality, enabling structural characterization of inconsistency
- The induced inference relations satisfy reflexivity, left logical equivalence, right weakening, cut, and cautious monotonicity postulates
- Computational complexity of basic reasoning tasks is in the second level of the polynomial hierarchy (ΠP₂-complete for inference, DP-complete for MCSig)
- Using naive forgetting leads to reasoning that coincides with Priest's paraconsistent 3-valued logic

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
- **Claim**: Reasoning with maximal consistent subsignatures restores consistency by removing "conflicting" propositions while retaining logical structure.
- **Mechanism**: The framework identifies maximal subsets of propositions whose projection (via forgetting) yields a consistent knowledge base, enabling inconsistency-tolerant reasoning.
- **Core assumption**: Forgetting can be defined such that consistency is restored without preserving all original inferences.
- **Evidence anchors**:
  - [abstract]: "maximal consistent subsignature is a maximal set of propositions such that forgetting the remaining propositions restores consistency"
  - [section 2]: "We define a maximal consistent subsignature as a maximal set of propositions, such that forgetting... the remaining propositions from the knowledge base makes it consistent again"
  - [corpus]: Weak—no direct evidence for forgetting mechanics; inferred from the definition of ⊟ operator.
- **Break condition**: If forgetting does not truly restore consistency, the approach fails; e.g., if all projections remain inconsistent.

### Mechanism 2
- **Claim**: Minimal inconsistent and maximal consistent subsignatures obey the hitting set duality.
- **Mechanism**: A minimal hitting set of minimal inconsistent subsignatures yields a maximal consistent subsignature, and vice versa.
- **Core assumption**: The duality from classical subset-based reasoning carries over to subsignature-based reasoning.
- **Evidence anchors**:
  - [section 4]: "we obtain a technical framework that is quite similar to the framework of maximal consistent subsets and minimal inconsistent subsets, but also features some additional interesting properties"
  - [section 4]: "the classical hitting set duality [17] carries over to maximal consistent subsignatures as well"
  - [corpus]: Weak—no direct corpus evidence; relies on formal proof in paper.
- **Break condition**: If the duality does not hold, the relationship between inconsistency and consistency cannot be characterized structurally.

### Mechanism 3
- **Claim**: The inference relations based on maximal consistent subsignatures satisfy key rationality postulates from non-monotonic reasoning.
- **Mechanism**: By ensuring reflexivity, left logical equivalence, right weakening, cut, and cautious monotonicity, the reasoning approach aligns with established non-monotonic frameworks.
- **Core assumption**: The subsignature-based approach can be proven to satisfy these postulates without collapsing into classical logic.
- **Evidence anchors**:
  - [section 5]: "We consider reasoning with maximal consistent subsignatures, define corresponding inference relations, and analyse their properties"
  - [section 5]: Lists properties satisfied by | ∼⊟ i and | ∼⊟ w (reﬂexivity, left logical equivalence, right weakening, cut, cautious monotonicity)
  - [corpus]: Weak—no external validation; proof is internal to paper.
- **Break condition**: If any postulate fails, the reasoning framework loses its non-monotonic character.

## Architecture Onboarding

- **Component map**:
  - Forgetting operator (⊟) -> Projection (|⊟ S) -> Consistency checker -> Subs signature analyzer -> Inference engine

- **Critical path**:
  1. Input KB and target formula α
  2. Compute all maximal consistent subsignatures (MCSig⊟)
  3. For each S ∈ MCSig⊟, check if K|⊟ S |= α
  4. Aggregate results for | ∼⊟ i (all must hold) or | ∼⊟ w (at least one must hold)

- **Design tradeoffs**:
  - Formula-wise vs occurrence-wise forgetting: formula-wise (⊟ ve) is simpler but less expressive; occurrence-wise (⊟ na) is more flexible but computationally heavier
  - Syntactic vs semantic consistency: syntactic consistency is easier to check but may not capture semantic conflicts

- **Failure signatures**:
  - All projections remain inconsistent: forgetting cannot restore consistency
  - MCSig⊟ is empty: indicates no consistent subsignature exists
  - Complexity explosion: DP-complete or higher problems may be intractable for large K

- **First 3 experiments**:
  1. Verify that {a, ¬a ∧ c} has MISig⊟ na = {{a}} and MCSig⊟ na = {{c}}
  2. Test that reasoning with MCSig⊟ na yields K1| ∼⊟ na i c (as claimed in Example 9)
  3. Confirm that the hitting set duality holds for a simple KB with known MISig and MCSig

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out

### Open Question 1
- **Question**: Can the inference relations based on maximal consistent subsignatures be extended to handle defeasible reasoning or other forms of non-monotonic reasoning beyond the rationality postulates tested?
- **Basis in paper**: [explicit] The paper states "A deeper analysis of the properties from [10] is left for future work."
- **Why unresolved**: The paper only analyzes compliance with basic rationality postulates from non-monotonic reasoning, leaving more complex properties unexplored.
- **What evidence would resolve it**: A formal proof showing whether these inference relations satisfy additional non-monotonic reasoning properties like rational monotony, pseudo-transitivity, or properties from other non-monotonic frameworks.

### Open Question 2
- **Question**: How do maximal consistent subsignatures compare to maximal consistent subsets in terms of the size of the resulting consistent knowledge bases after forgetting?
- **Basis in paper**: [inferred] The paper contrasts reasoning with maximal consistent subsets (syntax-dependent) versus maximal consistent subsignatures, but doesn't analyze the size of the resulting consistent bases.
- **Why unresolved**: While the paper shows conceptual differences, it doesn't provide empirical analysis of the practical impact on knowledge base size.
- **What evidence would resolve it**: Empirical studies comparing the average size of projected knowledge bases (MCKB(K)) between the two approaches across various types of inconsistent knowledge bases.

### Open Question 3
- **Question**: Are there forgetting operators beyond ⊟ve and ⊟na that could provide better trade-offs between consistency restoration and inference preservation?
- **Basis in paper**: [explicit] The paper states "For future work, we intend to investigate further operators" and only analyzes two specific forgetting operators.
- **Why unresolved**: The paper restricts its analysis to just two forgetting operators despite acknowledging the possibility of others.
- **What evidence would resolve it**: Development and analysis of alternative forgetting operators that could balance consistency restoration with inference preservation, followed by comparative analysis against ⊟ve and ⊟na.

### Open Question 4
- **Question**: Can the inconsistency measures based on maximal consistent subsignatures (IMISig, IMISig-C, IMCSig, IP) be shown to satisfy desirable properties like monotony, subadditivity, or minimality?
- **Basis in paper**: [explicit] The paper defines new inconsistency measures but only proves they are technically measures (I(K) = 0 iff K is consistent), stating "We leave a further investigation of the above measures for future work."
- **Why unresolved**: The paper only establishes the basic property of being an inconsistency measure, without analyzing more sophisticated properties.
- **What evidence would resolve it**: Formal proofs demonstrating which (if any) of these measures satisfy standard properties of inconsistency measures, and comparison with established measures from the literature.

## Limitations
- The framework's practical utility and computational feasibility for large knowledge bases remains unverified due to lack of implementation or scalability analysis
- The relationship to existing paraconsistent logics, while asserted, lacks extensive validation through practical examples beyond Priest's 3-valued logic
- The paper relies heavily on formal proofs rather than empirical validation, leaving uncertainty about real-world applicability

## Confidence
- **High confidence**: The theoretical framework for maximal consistent subsignatures and their relationship to minimal inconsistent subsignatures via hitting set duality
- **Medium confidence**: The complexity class assignments (ΠP₂-complete, DP-complete) based on cited literature, though direct algorithmic analysis is not provided
- **Low confidence**: The practical utility and computational feasibility of the approach for large knowledge bases, as no implementation or scalability analysis is presented
