# Emergent social conventions and collective bias in LLM populations

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2410.08948
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.08948
- Reference count: 40
- Primary result: LLM populations spontaneously develop shared linguistic norms through local interactions, revealing collective biases and critical mass dynamics

## Executive Summary
This study demonstrates that populations of Large Language Models can autonomously develop social conventions through local pairwise interactions without explicit programming. The research reveals that while individual agents appear unbiased in their convention choices, collective bias emerges at the population level in convention selection. Most notably, the study uncovers critical mass dynamics where minority groups of committed agents can drive social change by imposing alternative conventions once they reach a threshold size. These findings have important implications for understanding AI system behavior in collective settings and highlight the need to test alignment at the group level rather than just for individual agents.

## Method Summary
The researchers conducted simulations with populations of LLM agents (Llama 3 70B, Llama 3.1 70B, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Llama 2 70B) interacting in pairs to coordinate on naming conventions. Each population consisted of N=24 agents selecting from a name pool of size W=10, with memory length M=5 to track past interactions. The interaction protocol used text-based prompts with system, memory, and instructional components, rewarding successful coordination with +100 points and penalizing failures with -50 points. Multiple simulation trials were run to observe convention emergence patterns, measure individual and collective biases, and test critical mass dynamics by introducing committed minority agents.

## Key Results
- LLM populations spontaneously converge on shared naming conventions through local interactions without centralized coordination
- Collective bias emerges in convention selection despite individual agents appearing unbiased in their choices
- Minority groups of committed agents can drive norm change when they reach a critical mass threshold

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
The mechanism appears to rely on reinforcement learning through the payoff structure, where successful coordination is rewarded (+100 points) and failures are penalized (-50 points). This creates selective pressure for agents to converge on shared conventions. The memory system (M=5) allows agents to track past successful interactions and build upon them. The pairwise interaction protocol ensures that local coordination attempts gradually propagate successful conventions through the population via repeated interactions. Critical mass dynamics emerge because once a minority group reaches sufficient size, their coordinated interactions become frequent enough to overcome the initial majority convention through the same reinforcement mechanism.

## Open Questions the Paper Calls Out
### Open Question 1
- Question: How do different initial conditions, such as starting with a more diverse set of conventions or introducing a small number of "committed agents" from the beginning, affect the spontaneous emergence of social conventions in LLM populations?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper mentions that the spontaneous emergence of conventions holds for a variety of simulation parameters, but does not explore the effects of different initial conditions.
- Why unresolved: The study focused on a specific set of initial conditions (N=24 agents, W=10 name pool) and did not explore how variations in these conditions might influence the dynamics of convention formation.
- What evidence would resolve it: Running experiments with different initial conditions, such as varying the population size, name pool size, or introducing committed agents from the start, and observing how these changes affect the emergence and stability of social conventions.

### Open Question 2
- Question: How does the structure of the social network (e.g., random vs. structured) influence the dynamics of convention formation and the critical mass required for norm change in LLM populations?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper mentions that future work could explore embedding the population in more realistic social networks, as the current study considered only unstructured populations.
- Why unresolved: The study focused on unstructured populations with random interactions, but real-world social interactions often occur within structured networks. The impact of network structure on convention formation and norm change remains unexplored.
- What evidence would resolve it: Conducting experiments with LLM populations embedded in different social network structures (e.g., random, scale-free, small-world) and comparing the dynamics of convention formation and the critical mass required for norm change across these structures.

### Open Question 3
- Question: How do LLM populations develop and maintain higher-order social norms, such as fairness, reciprocity, and cooperation, beyond simple naming conventions?
- Basis in paper: [inferred] The paper mentions that future research avenues include developing frameworks to promote the emergence of specific conventions and higher-order social norms, but does not explore this aspect.
- Why unresolved: The study focused on the emergence of simple naming conventions, but real-world social norms are often more complex and involve concepts like fairness, reciprocity, and cooperation. Understanding how LLM populations develop and maintain these higher-order norms is an open question.
- What evidence would resolve it: Designing experiments where LLM populations engage in tasks or games that require the development and maintenance of higher-order social norms, such as fairness games or cooperative tasks, and observing the emergence and evolution of these norms within the population.

## Limitations
- Specific model versions and complete prompt templates are not provided, making exact replication difficult
- Results rely on stochastic LLM outputs with limited number of runs (40) per condition
- Study does not address how different LLM architectures or training paradigms might affect convention emergence
- Network structure is simplified to random interactions, not reflecting real-world social network patterns
