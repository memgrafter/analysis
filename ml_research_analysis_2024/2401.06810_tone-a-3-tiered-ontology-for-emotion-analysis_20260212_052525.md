# TONE: A 3-Tiered ONtology for Emotion analysis

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2401.06810
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.06810
- Reference count: 11
- TONE achieves 97% accuracy in emotion detection and 85% accuracy in product review prediction

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces TONE, a three-tiered ontology for emotion analysis built on Parrott's hierarchical model of emotions. The ontology structures emotions into Primary (6), Secondary (25), and Tertiary (135) levels, providing a granular framework for emotion categorization. TONE includes extensive vocabularies for each emotion and automated methods for establishing three types of dependencies between emotions. The authors demonstrate TONE's effectiveness across three use cases: emotion detection, product review analysis, and empathetic response generation, where it outperforms existing approaches.

## Method Summary
The authors developed TONE through a semi-automated process that begins with gathering emotion definitions and synonyms from Oxford Dictionary and Thesaurus. They construct emotion vocabularies by assigning overlapping synonyms using cosine similarity and human annotation. The ontology structure follows Parrott's hierarchy, defining classes and subclasses in Protege with "is-a" relationships. Dependencies between emotions are automatically established through three mechanisms: isOppositeOf (using thesaurus lookups), isComposedOf (using hierarchical relationships), and plus-LeadsTo (using emotion classification of combined statements). The ontology is implemented in OWL and evaluated through human annotation and DL queries using the HermiT reasoner.

## Key Results
- TONE achieves 97% accuracy in emotion detection tasks
- 85% accuracy in product review prediction compared to baseline methods
- Successfully demonstrates three use cases: emotion detection, product review analysis, and empathetic response generation

- Mechanism: By mapping emotions to Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary tiers, TONE allows users to select emotion granularity based on task requirements. This avoids the computational overhead of using all emotions simultaneously.
- Core assumption: Parrott's hierarchical model is suitable for ontology creation and effectively captures emotion relationships.

  - [abstract] TONE uses Parrott's hierarchical model to create an ontology with 6 primary emotions, 25 secondary emotions, and 135 tertiary emotions.

  - Evaluation: Human evaluation by annotators and automatic evaluation using DL queries
  - Applications: Emotion detection, product review prediction, empathetic response generation

- Critical path:
  1. Construct extensive emotion vocabularies using semi-automated process
  2. Define ontology classes and subclasses based on Parrott's hierarchy
  3. Establish dependencies between emotions using automated methods
  4. Evaluate ontology using human and automatic methods
  5. Demonstrate applications in various domains

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Granularity vs. computational complexity: More granular emotion tiers provide better task-specific performance but increase computational overhead
  - Automation vs. accuracy: Automated dependency establishment reduces manual effort but may introduce inaccuracies
  - Vocabulary size vs. exclusivity: Larger vocabularies improve emotion detection but may increase overlapping terms

- Failure signatures:
  - Inconsistent or inaccurate emotion relationships due to automated dependency establishment
  - Overlapping or incomplete emotion vocabularies due to limitations in the semi-automated construction process
  - Poor performance in applications due to suboptimal ontology design or implementation
