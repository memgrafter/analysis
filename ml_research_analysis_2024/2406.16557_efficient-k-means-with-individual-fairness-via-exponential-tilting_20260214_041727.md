# Efficient k-means with Individual Fairness via Exponential Tilting

## Executive Summary
This paper introduces tilted k-means (TKM), a novel algorithm that addresses individual fairness in k-means clustering by integrating exponential tilting into the traditional sum of squared errors (SSE) objective. TKM overcomes key limitations of existing fairness-aware clustering methods, which often suffer from high computational complexity and may inadvertently exacerbate the Matthew Effect. The proposed approach achieves both computational efficiency and fairness by reducing the variance of distances within each cluster through a carefully designed objective function.

The method demonstrates significant improvements over state-of-the-art approaches, achieving hundreds to thousands of times acceleration in running time while successfully handling large datasets that cause memory overflow in competing methods. The theoretical analysis provides solid grounding for the algorithm's efficiency claims, with O(kn) complexity representing a substantial improvement over traditional fairness-aware clustering approaches.

## Method Summary
Tilted k-means (TKM) integrates exponential tilting into the standard k-means objective to formulate a new objective called tilted SSE. This modification allows the algorithm to explicitly account for individual fairness by reducing the variance of distances within clusters. The exponential tilting mechanism introduces a hyperparameter t that controls the degree of variance reduction. The algorithm maintains the same initialization and assignment steps as traditional k-means but modifies the update step through the tilted SSE formulation. This approach enables individual fairness by ensuring that similar individuals receive similar treatment in the clustering process, as measured by the variance of their distances to cluster centers.

## Key Results
- TKM achieves hundreds to thousands of times acceleration in running time compared to other fairness-aware clustering methods
- The algorithm successfully handles large datasets that cause memory overflow in competing methods
- TKM reduces the variance of distances within each cluster, demonstrating improved individual fairness as measured by the novel fairness metric
- Theoretical analysis confirms O(kn) time complexity, representing linear scalability with dataset size

## Why This Works (Mechanism)
Tilted k-means works by modifying the traditional sum of squared errors objective with exponential tilting, which inherently penalizes high variance within clusters. The exponential tilting mechanism introduces a bias toward more balanced cluster assignments, where no single data point dominates the cluster structure. By reducing intra-cluster distance variance, the algorithm ensures that similar individuals (those with similar distances to cluster centers) receive similar treatment, which is the core principle of individual fairness. The hyperparameter t controls the strength of this tilting effect, allowing practitioners to balance between pure SSE minimization and fairness considerations.

## Architecture Onboarding

**Component Map:** Data points → Distance Calculation → Exponential Tilting → Cluster Assignment → Center Update → Tilted SSE Objective

**Critical Path:** The algorithm follows the standard k-means iterative structure: initialization → assignment step → center update step → objective evaluation, with the key difference being the tilted SSE calculation during the update phase.

**Design Tradeoffs:** TKM trades minimal SSE increase for significant fairness improvements and computational efficiency gains. The exponential tilting introduces a hyperparameter t that must be tuned, representing a complexity tradeoff for the fairness benefits achieved.

**Failure Signatures:** The algorithm may underperform when: t is set too high (causing excessive SSE increase), datasets contain extreme outliers that dominate tilting effects, or the fairness metric doesn't align with the actual fairness requirements of the application domain.

**First Experiments:** 1) Run TKM on a synthetic dataset with known cluster structure to verify basic functionality; 2) Compare TKM's runtime against traditional k-means on increasing dataset sizes; 3) Test TKM with different t values to observe the variance-fairness tradeoff curve.

## Limitations
- The variance-based fairness metric may not capture all aspects of individual fairness, and the relationship between reduced intra-cluster distance variance and equitable treatment requires further validation
- The exponential tilting approach may have limitations in datasets with extreme outliers or highly imbalanced distributions, though this is not extensively explored
- The paper focuses primarily on quantitative metrics, with less attention to potential qualitative impacts on the clustered groups
