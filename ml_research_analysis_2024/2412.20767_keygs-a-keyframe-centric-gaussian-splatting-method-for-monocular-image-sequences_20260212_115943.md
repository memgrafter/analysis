# KeyGS: A Keyframe-Centric Gaussian Splatting Method for Monocular Image Sequences

## Quick Facts
- arXiv ID: 2412.20767
- Source URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.20767
- Authors: Keng-Wei Chang; Zi-Ming Wang; Shang-Hong Lai
- Reference count: 6
- Achieves 33.01 average PSNR on Tanks and Temples dataset, outperforming previous methods by 2 dB

## Executive Summary
KeyGS addresses the challenge of efficient 3D reconstruction from monocular image sequences by proposing a keyframe-centric Gaussian splatting method. The approach combines Structure-from-Motion (SfM) for rapid initial camera pose estimation with joint refinement using 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) and a novel coarse-to-fine frequency-aware densification technique. This method significantly reduces training time from hours to minutes while improving novel view synthesis and camera pose estimation accuracy.

## Method Summary
KeyGS processes monocular image sequences by first subsampling keyframes and applying sequential SfM to obtain initial camera poses within seconds. It then performs joint refinement of both camera poses and 3D Gaussian Splatting representation using a novel coarse-to-fine frequency-aware densification strategy. The method progressively reduces Gaussian smoothing scale during training to prevent premature splitting and local minima trapping. This approach maintains continuous pose optimization throughout training, preventing error accumulation that occurs when poses are fixed after initial registration.

## Key Results
- Achieves 33.01 average PSNR on Tanks and Temples dataset, outperforming previous methods by 2 dB
- Demonstrates superior pose accuracy with mean RPEt of 0.020 and RPEr of 0.015
- Shows 4 dB higher PSNR on CO3DV2 dataset compared to baseline methods
- Reduces training time from hours to approximately 10 minutes

## Why This Works (Mechanism)

### Mechanism 1
Coarse-to-fine frequency-aware densification prevents premature Gaussian splitting and local minima trapping during joint pose refinement. By progressively reducing Gaussian smoothing scale σ, the method first aligns low-frequency signals (stable gradients) before allowing high-frequency details to influence densification, preventing spiky Gaussians and overfitting to incorrect poses. Core assumption: High-frequency gradients cause misalignment and excessive splitting when camera poses are inaccurate.

### Mechanism 2
Keyframe-centric SfM preprocessing significantly reduces computational complexity while providing sufficient initial pose estimates. By subsampling 1/N keyframes and using sequential SfM instead of exhaustive matching, the method reduces time complexity from O(n⁴) to O(n²), enabling rapid initial pose estimation within seconds. Core assumption: Sequential structure of image sequences allows reliable pose estimation from sparse keyframes.

### Mechanism 3
Continuous joint refinement throughout training prevents error accumulation compared to methods that fix camera poses after initial registration. Unlike CF3DGS which stops refining poses after registration, KeyGS maintains pose optimization throughout the densification process, preventing trajectory drift and Gaussian over-splitting. Core assumption: Pose errors accumulate over time when not continuously corrected during training.

## Architecture Onboarding

- Component map: Input images -> Keyframe subsampling -> Sequential SfM (seconds) -> Initial poses -> 3DGS initialization -> Joint refinement with frequency-aware densification -> Final reconstruction and refined poses

- Critical path: Input images → Keyframe subsampling → Sequential SfM (seconds) → Initial poses → 3DGS initialization → Joint refinement with frequency-aware densification → Final reconstruction and refined poses

- Design tradeoffs:
  - Speed vs accuracy: Sequential SfM is faster but less accurate than exhaustive mode
  - Memory vs detail: Frequency-aware densification controls Gaussian splitting to balance detail and stability
  - Training time vs pose accuracy: Continuous refinement improves poses but increases computation

- Failure signatures:
  - Gaussian explosion (excessive splitting) → Check frequency scale σ and regularization parameters
  - Trajectory drift → Verify keyframe subsampling interval and sequential SfM accuracy
  - Blurry reconstruction → Examine photometric loss weighting and densification thresholds

- First 3 experiments:
  1. Compare keyframe subsampling intervals (1, 5, 10) on a simple indoor scene to find optimal balance between speed and accuracy
  2. Test frequency-aware densification with different initial σ values to observe effect on Gaussian splitting behavior
  3. Validate continuous pose refinement by comparing with fixed-pose training on a dynamic scene sequence

## Limitations
- The approach relies heavily on sequential SfM accuracy for initial pose estimates, which may fail in textureless or repetitive scenes where feature matching becomes unreliable.
- The frequency-aware densification strategy lacks specific parameter tuning guidelines that could affect reproducibility.
- The method assumes static scenes, limiting applicability to dynamic environments.

## Confidence

- High Confidence: Claims about computational speedup (10-20× reduction in processing time) and novel view synthesis quality (2 dB improvement on Tanks and Temples) are well-supported by quantitative metrics and comparative results.
- Medium Confidence: Claims about joint pose refinement preventing error accumulation are theoretically justified but could benefit from more extensive ablation studies comparing different refinement strategies.
- Low Confidence: Claims about the specific mechanism of frequency-aware densification preventing premature Gaussian splitting lack direct empirical validation and rely primarily on qualitative observations.

## Next Checks

1. Test KeyGS on scenes with low texture or repetitive patterns to evaluate robustness of the sequential SfM initialization and identify failure modes.

3. Evaluate performance on dynamic scene sequences to assess limitations of the static scene assumption and identify potential extensions for motion handling.
